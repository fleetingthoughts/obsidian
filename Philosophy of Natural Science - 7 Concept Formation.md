This chapter expands on the internal principles and bridging principles that are necessary requirements of a theory and expands on one element of theoretical explanations: theoretical concepts. The chapter emphasizes the importance of the internal principles by arguing against Bridgman's Operationism which places an overemphasis on the empirical import of statements and devalues the systematic import and subsequently theoretical explanations. The chapter is outlined as follows:
- The structure of a definition and the two kinds of a definitions and their components:
	- The structure of a definition: definiendum and definiens
	- The kinds of definition: analytic (descriptive) and stipulative
	- The need for axiomatic undefined terms: Primitive terms
	- Theoretical terms follow the same concept with each one being stipulative with pretheoretical terms defining them. By themselves, they are tautologies with no empirical content. We now examine the bridging principles that allow us . The sentences that do so are referred to as "interpretive sentences"
- Hempel's criticism of Bridgman's Operationism criteria of concept formation in favor of theoretical explanation described in [6 Theories and Theoretical Explanation](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%206%20Theories%20and%20Theoretical%20Explanation.md) on 3 separate points:
	1) The requirement of consistency and loss of simplicity
	2) The loss of systematic import
	3) The limitations operational definitions impose on scientific inquiry as the operations only provide partial meanings


Hempel provides an overview of Operationism formulation of interpretive sentences by how it defines the quantification and qualification of concepts by two components:
1) Testing operation
2) Test result
The purpose is to ensure objectiveness by testability. While Hempel agrees that empirical import is required for an explanation, what distinguishes Hempel is his model of theoretical explanations which requires not only bridging principles for testability, but also internal principles to give the concepts in an explanation what he calls "systematic import". Hempel characterizes Operationism in three different ways and criticizes them on each point:

The first point is the requirement of consistency of operational definitions. Bridgman's Operationism distinguishes between the various methods of measuring the same quantity. The axiomatic starting point is the testing operation that gives meaning to a term and if two methods can measure the same thing, then there is an underlying empirical generalization. For instance, of the various ways to measure length, such as tactual length vs optical length, to Bridgman, there is a difference is what we use as our axiomatic starting point, the method to measure length or the theoretical concept of length? Hempel calls the latter the systematic import ). 

What if we find a discrepancy between the methods? Depending on what we consider to be the axiomatic starting point, we can say the methods refer to separate things thus a concept becomes bloated with several different operational tests compared to a theoretical explanation which uses the internal principles to unify different testing procedures. Hempel's criticism is on a practical front. Theoretical explanations provide predictive power beyond special cases: theories widen our search scope which leads to several definitions, to distinguish each by their operation as opposed to the motivating internal principle would be defeating for theoretical explanations and we lose the main reason we have them: simplification and unification: "simplicity in the sense of economy of concepts is an important feature of a good scientific theory (p.94)"

As a second point, Hempel highlights the ability for theoretical explanations to iterate on the bridging principles and testing operations to enhance the internal principles and systematic import by historical justification. Hempel first addresses the apparent circular reasoning in that the theory that is tested by its bridging principles also being able to modify its bridging principles to justify itself (ad hocs). This is only an apparent circular reasoning. What is actually happening is that the empirical import is only one side of the theory. What we actually modify are the internal principles as well which changes the subsequent empirical import. "concept formation and theory formation goes hand in hand (p, 97)." Hempel also provides historical justification that history shows we modify the operational criteria using a theory as the axiomatic starting point. Time measured by sun which was modified as opposed to saying time-measurement by the sun is different concept from time-measurement by other methods

For the final third point, Operationism narrows the scope of science by rendering certain scenarios meaningless because it defines concepts by operations in isolation of of other concepts. Hempel sees this as a problem solved by systematic import of theoretical explanation which puts a concept in the context of other concepts. T<u>he problem with Operationism is that it views the scenario in isolation</u>. So the advantage in theoretical definitions over operational definitions is the ability to test for scenarios that Operationism would deem meaningless.

Hempel instead proposes we go back to his model of theoretical explanations where terms are given systematic import by the internal principles of a theory that solves problem 3) and 2) and uses bridging principles to provide a variety of applications to solve 1) and 3).
# Terms
- ***Stipulative Definitions.*** A special definition outside of the commonly accepted meaning that is assigned to a term.
- ***Descriptive Definitions***. Colloquially and commonly accepted meaning of a term.
- ***Analytic Definitions.*** A more specific <u>descriptive definitions</u>. The definiendum is derived deductively from another concept or term so that the definiens is a tautology for another definiens.
- ***Definiendum.*** The term to be defined
- ***Definiens.*** The defining expression
- ***Primitive Terms.*** Undefined terms that are axiomatically accepted to use as a starting point for all our following definitions
- ***Interpretive Sentences***
