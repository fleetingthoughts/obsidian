This chapter expands on the internal principles and bridging principles that are necessary requirements of a theory. The chapter emphasizes the importance of the internal principles by arguing against Bridgman's Operationism which places an overemphasis on the empirical import of statements and devalues the systematic import and subsequently theories themselves.
- The structure of a definition: definiendum and definiens
- The kinds of definition: analytic (descriptive) and stipulative
- The need for axiomatic undefined terms: Primitive terms
- Theoretical terms follow the same concept with each one being stipulative with pretheoretical terms defining them. By themselves, they are tautologies with no empirical content. We now examine the bridging principles that allow us . The sentences that do so are referred to as "interpretive sentences"

Operationism formulation of interpretive sentences that 1) qualifies (i.e. is it acidic or basic) and 2) quantifies (how long, how fast):
	- testing operating
	- test result
- Purpose is to ensure objectiveness by testability

- Hempel disagrees with the operational school of thought because it hinders theoretical explanations which he believes plays a critical role in inquiry both as a methodology and in literal significance

- Characteristics of an operational definition that Hempel criticizes on each point:
	- Requirement of consistency. Furthermore Bridgman distinguishes between the various methods of measuring the same quantity. The starting point must always be the testing operation that gives meaning to a term and if two methods can measure the same thing, then there is an underlying empirical generalization. (tactual length vs optical length as opposed to just length the difference is what we use as our axiomatic starting point, the method to measure length or the theoretical concept of length? Hempel calls the latter the systematic import ).
		- Hempel's criticism: what if we find a discrepancy between methods? Depending on what we consider to be the axiomatic starting point, we can say the methods refer to separate things or that one of the operational criteria needs to be modified. Hempel's criticism on a practical front presumes the necessity and advantageous nature of a theory to provide predictive power beyond special cases: theories widen our search scope which leads to several definitions, to distinguish each by their operation as opposed to the motivating internal principle would be defeating for theoretical explanations and we lose the main reason we have them: simplification and unification.
		- "simplicity in the sense of economy of concepts is an important feature of a good scientific theory (p.94)"
		- Hempel also provides historical justification that history shows we modify the operational criteria using a theory as the axiomatic starting point. Time measured by sun which was modified as opposed to saying time-measurement by the sun is different concept from time-measurement by other methods
		- Hempel also addresses the apparent circular reasoning in that the theory that is tested by its bridging principles also being able to modify its bridging principles to justify itself (ad hocs). This is only an apparant circular reasoning. What is actually happening is that the empirical import is only one side of the theory, what we are actually doing is modifying the internal principles as well which changes the subsequent empirical import. "concept formation and theory formation goes hand in hand (p, 97)."
- Operationism narrows the scope of science by deeming certain scenarios meaningless. 
	- Hempel sees this as a problem that the systematic import (i.e. the motivating internal principles) solves by viewing the scenario in the context of other scenarios. The problem with operationism is that it views the scenario in isolation. So the advantage in theoretical definitions over operational definitions is the ability to test for scenarios that operationism would deem meaningless.

- Summary by hempel for why we reject operationism
	1) several alternative criteria of application for a term
	2) the importance of systematic import
	3) the limited range of conditions that narrows our understanding 
Hempel instead proposes we go back to his model of theoretical explanations where terms are given systematic import by the internal principles of a theory that solves problem 3) and 2) and uses bridging principles to provide a variety of applications to solve 1) and 3).
# Terms
- ***Stipulative Definitions.*** A special definition outside of the commonly accepted meaning that is assigned to a term.
- ***Analytic Definitions.*** Also called descriptive definitions. These are colloquially and commonly accepted meaning of a term.
- ***Definiendum.*** The term to be defined
- ***Definiens.*** The defining expression
- ***Primitive Terms.*** Undefined terms that are axiomatically accepted to use as a starting point for all our following definitions
- ***Interpretive Sentences***
