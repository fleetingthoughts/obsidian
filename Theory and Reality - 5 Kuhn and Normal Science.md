Chapter 5 and 6 will discuss the views by Thomas Kuhn around his book "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions". PGS imparts how big of an impact this book has had not only in the ideas it provided, but also the way it approached philosophy of science from a sociological and historical perspective. He linked questions in the fields of history and sociology to reinterpret scientific history and the way we organize scientific thought. The impact is made evident by the fact that term "paradigm shift" used in the book made its way into the English colloquialism. The book is commonly attributed to the death of logical positivism, but an interesting note is that the book was edited and published by the Vienna Circle.

Through out this chapter and the next, it will be necessary to distinguish PGS's critical commentary from Thomas Kuhn's ideas. PGS also notes that its hard to distinguish which of Kuhn's ideas are normative and which are descriptive which leads to misinterpretation. As well, Kuhn has used some terms very ambiguously. Even the seminal term "paradigm" had several different meanings throughout the book. It'll be important to clarify which points are PGS's interpretations.

PGS starts with a general overview, by explaining Kuhn's general model for Science by giving an overview of what a paradigm means. PGS Kuhn's use of paradigm as follows: 
	- Paradigm in the narrow sense: "whole way of doing science in a particular field \[...\] it is a package of claims about the world, methods for gathering and analyzing data (p. 76.)" This represents a paradigms impact on normal science to be discussed
	- Paradigm in the broad sense" a package of claims about the world that when put together make up a way of viewing the world and conducting science in general (p. 77)" This represents a paradigm's relationship to scientific revolutions
	- Paradigm in the narrowest sense: an exemplary achievement that is used as a model for science moving forward. Examples include Mendel's experiment or Newton's laws of motion
Paradigms from now on will be used in the broad sense unless specified.

To describe Kuhn's general views of Science, it is contrasted against Popper:
- Science is characterized by a close-minded indoctrination as opposed to the risk-taking behavior to falsify a theory
- Science is not a linear progression and accumulation of ideas where one follows a certain [recipe](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%201%20Introduction.md) like risk-taking refutation, but it occurs in phases characterized by revolutions and punctuations. In fact, it is difficult to define progress as Kuhn struggled with it. $$\text{Normal Science\quad} \to \text{\quad Crisis\quad} \to \text{\quad Scientific Revolution\quad} \to \text{\quad Normal Science\quad} \to ...$$
We now discuss the Kuhnian model of Science beginning with Normal Science. I categorize PGS's interpretation between the characteristics of a paradigm (a descriptive approach) which leads to some normative comments that are PGS's interpretation as Kuhn makes no direct normative claims on how Science should operate. Its worth noting that the justification for a lot of Kuhn's ideas is an appeal to what actually happened in history which can be found in my examples section below. Normal science is characterized as follows:
- Normal science is guided by a paradigm that results in a close-mindedness. This close mindedness is specifically a "consensus-forging" on fundamentals which leads to no debates on what these fundamentals are. (see examples).
- When a particular scientific field is in a period of normal science, it is operating under one paradigm at a time. 
- The kind of scientific work was described by Kuhn as a form of "puzzle-solving" as opposed to "problem-solving". The idea is that with a puzzle, there is an assumed solution with existing tools/pieces that we use to solve it. 
Its more intuitive to see that the "puzzle-solving" scientific work helps further refine the paradigm and determine its applicability resulting in progress. The paradigm is necessary here to narrow the scope of a Kuhnian scientist's work. But in terms of the type of problems and consensus between the scientists, should scientific discussion close off fundamentals? PGS interprets it as practical to narrow the scope scientific work by serving as a guide for what's important and what's not important. This is contrasted with the "disorganized and random" search for refutations by the risk-taking Popperian scientist. The purpose of the paradigm therefore is organize Science as a whole. There is a sense of community or institution under a paradigm.

Naturally, not all "puzzle-solving" scientific work described by Kuhn will be neatly resolved. Some of these will be "anomalies" with no or unsatisfactory resolutions and will lead to a "crisis". The move from normal science to the rejection of a paradigm occurs with the following (p.84):
1) a critical mass of anomalies has arisen
2) a competing paradigm exists to take its place
It should be noted for 1), that the anomalies are characterized by "deep problems" that lead to questioning the fundamentals. This is contrasted with the Popperian scientist who is trigger-happy to refute a theory. The anomalies faced by a Kuhnian scientist need to be far more convincing and it is not conducive to science for there to be frequent revisions in the paradigm. This has lead to criticism of Kuhn's model of Science to be close-minded.
# 5.5 Wrap-up
PGS will later introduce criticisms to Kuhn, but for now he will simply categorize them on two fronts:
1) The idea that only one paradigm can exist at a time
2) The degree of close-mindedness described by a Kuhnian scientist during Normal Science. PGS seems to interpret Kuhn as not taking "seriously the possibility that scientists could agree to work together in a coordinated way, not wasting time on constant discussion of fundamental issues, while retaining a cautious attitude toward their paradigm (p.85)."
In support of Kuhn, PGS provides an interesting anecdote in fields related to his expertise that justifies the need for close-mindedness under a single paradigm. He describes the use of AI to simulate life to the point that that the AI may be considered "life". This field included people like Stephen Wolfram. But PGS notes that the field has died down and never approached the "Normal Science " phase because there were too many competing theories and people went off in different directions with their ideas. There was a need for a paradigm to organize the scientists under a single banner. Additionally, there was a strong desire to commercialize and apply the ideas outside the scope of the field. Kuhn described the importance of the scientists desire to "puzzle-solve" within the parameters of the paradigm to refine it, but the field never reached such levels of refinement as they had branched off to other fields. There was a need for an almost "indoctrinating" paradigm to motivate the scientists to collaborate together and to commit to a "puzzle-solving" that refines the paradigm. Both were lacking in the now dead field of AI life modelling.
