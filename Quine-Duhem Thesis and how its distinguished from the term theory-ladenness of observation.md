The idea was put forth by Quine in his refutation of [logical positivism](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%202%20Logic%20Plus%20Empricism.md) in his seminal paper "The Two Dogmas of Empricism". The thesis attacks the verification criteria of the positivists as confirmation by observation is not possible as a hypothesis always relies on testing one or more background assumptions. Therefore, the observation does not test the singular statement made by the hypothesis, but a conjunction of statements and these hypothesis cannot be tested in isolation.

Later on, this has implications on falsificationism as the difficulties to unambiguously confirm a hypothesis is the exact same for the refutation of a hypothesis. 

The thesis is similar to the theory-ladenness of observations (a term developed after Quine by Kuhn, Feyerabend and others) in that they both describe how hypotheses always have presuppositions and are in reality a conjunction of several other hypotheses.  The theory-ladenness of observations coined after Quine focuses more on the theoretical presuppositions and positions held by an individual when proposing a hypothesis or testing it while Quine focused more on the inability to isolate a hypothesis into a formal logical statement that can be abstracted into an inductive logic as it was attempted by the logical positivists. It can be thought that theory-ladenness leads to the Quine-Duhem Thesis, but it also includes some subjectivity of the individual or community's previously held positions.
