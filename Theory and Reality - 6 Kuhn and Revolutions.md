Now discussing Revolutions, as described in [Theory and Reality - 5 Kuhn and Normal Science](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%205%20Kuhn%20and%20Normal%20Science.md), the breakdown of normal science and subsequent revolutions are triggered by two things:
1) A critical amount of anomalies resulting in a crisis
2) A competing paradigm to take its place characterized by its ability to problem-solve the anomalies (more on this problem-solving metric later)
Its worth contrasting this with Popper who would've deemed that 1) was sufficient to overthrow a paradigm. It is also worth noting that the problem-solving capabilities of the new paradigm could not be described by a philosophical theory of evidence or testing and that they are instead more subjective. Kuhn softens this position in his later work by stating that there is a common "shared basis for theory selection (p.90-91)" such as predictive power, be able to explain observations, etc... but this alone is not enough to characterize a paradigm as there is no such universal formula. Instead, the paradigm is characterized by its devaluing and emphasizing of the common basis shared by paradigms (i.e. incommensurability of standards explained later). For instance, some paradigms will value quantitative prediction over qualitative prediction for example. PGS's interpretation is that Kuhn believes this disorder and scientific revolution is good for Science.

PGS also counter Kuhn's description here in his own field of Biology quoting that it is very hard to find instances of these "crisis" with a critical amount of anomalies in biology so perhaps 1) is not needed at all and believes that Kuhn was motivated by a need for a hypothetical mechanism to incentivize people to move to a paradigm's competition.

We further characterize the competing paradigms and what distinguishes two paradigms and its larger implications in the ideas of relativism and what it means for scientific progress. Kuhn has stated that during peaceful "Normal Science", only one paradigm can exist at a time because paradigms are incommensurable with each other and would lead to disorganization preventing Normal Science from happening in the first place (See PGS's anecdote on AI life in last chapter). This incommensurability was described by Kuhn in 2 parts:
1) Linguistic incommensurability: a literal language barrier between scientists belong to 2 different paradigms that use terms differently resulting in miscommunication inconducive to normal science.
2) Incommensurability of standards: two scientists will not agree on what's important or not important. This relates to a concept called relativism.
Linguistic incommensurability should be observable in history and PGS rebuts this claiming there has been no such instances in history and does not believe it is a valid characteristic of paradigms. PGS does agree with 2) and elaborates on this point by looking at the role of causal explanation in Science. Different fields have different standards on the criteria for a satisfactory explanation of observations. Newton's laws are purely mathematical, but Mendel's testing in Biology promotes a theory that elaborates on the actual mechanisms.

The incommensurability of standard extends to an idea called relativism and the idea of "progress" in science. Relativism is a new field that has many different meanings, but in general is the idea that "truth or justification of a claim, or the applicability of a rule or standard depends on one's situation or point of view (p.94)". Relativism in a nutshell is the idea that "all truth is relative and there is no absolute truth". Kuhn's incommensurability of standard has caused some to label him as a "relativist", but in the context of scientific progress, there are some differences. Kuhn gave two explanations for progress:
1) The psychological view of progress with each successive paradigm because the victors will always believe their position to be the superior one ("History is in the eye of the beholder"). This is in-line with the relativists views as the measure of "progress" is determined  by one's own subjective situation
2) Efficiency in problem-solving. Science will shift to paradigms that have more problem-solving power. Progress and the switch to a new paradigm is characterized by an objective metric of "problem-solving power" that is not relativist. 
On 2), PGS criticizes Kuhn for it clashes with his views on the incommensurability of standards. How can we describe one paradigm as having better problem-solving efficiency if we can't agree on the problems worth solving or the solutions that are valuable.

# 6.5 Final thoughts on Kuhn
If I were to compare and contrast Kuhn's ideas to the logical positivists and Popper, I would do so as follows:
- Kuhn provided a model for science that is driven by a delicate balance between the close-mindedness required for coordinated work to the breakdown and reconstitution of scientific ideas
- The logical positivists and Popper were more concerned with describing Science in terms of the abstract philosophical ideas of language, epistemology, logic, etc.. that apply equally at the individual and the organizational level, but Kuhn makes a distinction between the individual work of the scientists in-service to the larger organization under a single paradigm. This description brings sociological descriptions similar to the "invisible hand of the free market" driven by the actions of self-interested individuals. Science in a sense is a whole greater than the sum of its parts.
The criticisms PGS will elaborate on in future chapters are:
- The possibility for multiple paradigms to exist. Single paradigms rarely have the level of dominance described by Kuhn and are more commensurable than he thought
- The need for close-mindedness as a normative description of Science.
