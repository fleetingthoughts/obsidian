## 📐 Section 1: Core Definitions & Topological Frameworks

## 1. The Structural Spectrum of Continuity

- Pointwise Continuity: A function $f$ is continuous at $x_0$ if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x) - f(x_0)\vert{} < \epsilon$ whenever $\vert{}x - x_0\vert{} < \delta$ (calculus_a... p. 17).
- Uniform Continuity: A function $f$ is uniformly continuous on an interval if $\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \delta > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x_2) - f(x_1)\vert{} < \epsilon$ whenever $\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{} < \delta$ for _any_ $x_1, x_2$ in the interval (calculus_a... p. 25).
- Lipschitz Continuity: A function $f$ is Lipschitz continuous on an interval if $\exists L > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x_2) - f(x_1)\vert{} \leq L\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}$ for all $x_1, x_2$ in the interval (calculus_a... p. 27).
- Implication Flow:  
    $$\text{Lipschitz Continuity} \implies \text{Uniform Continuity} \implies \text{Pointwise Continuity}$$
    
    - _Lipschitz implies Uniform_: Setting $\delta = \epsilon/L$ provides a universal modulus that depends strictly on $\epsilon$, liberating it from local coordinate dependencies (calculus_a... p. 27).
    - _Uniform implies Pointwise_: Holding one point fixed as $x_0$ satisfies local continuity universally (calculus_a... pp. 25-26).
    
- Significance & Context: This hierarchy represents the rigorous standard of 19th-century analysis developed by Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Karl Weierstrass to eliminate reliance on geometric intuition.

## 2. Hölder Continuity of Radical Paths (Scaffolded Proof)

- Proof:
    
    1. We begin with the elementary square-root property for $x_2 \geq x_1 \geq 0$ (calculus_a... p. 28):  
        $$\vert{}\sqrt{x_2} - \sqrt{x_1}\vert{} \leq \vert{}\sqrt{x_2} + \sqrt{x_1}\vert{}$$
    2. Multiply both sides by the positive difference factor $\vert{}\sqrt{x_2} - \sqrt{x_1}\vert{}$ (calculus_a... p. 28):  
        $$\vert{}\sqrt{x_2} - \sqrt{x_1}\vert{}^2 \leq \vert{}\sqrt{x_2} + \sqrt{x_1}\vert{} \cdot \vert{}\sqrt{x_2} - \sqrt{x_1}\vert{} = \vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}$$
    3. Taking the square root of both sides establishes the target inequality with a constant $L = 1$ and an exponent $\alpha = 1/2$ (calculus_a... p. 28):  
        $$\vert{}\sqrt{x_2} - \sqrt{x_1}\vert{} \leq \vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}^{1/2}$$
    4. Given $\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{} < \delta$, we set $\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}^{1/2} < \delta^{1/2} = \epsilon$, which yields the non-linear modulus of continuity $\delta(\epsilon) = \epsilon^2$ (calculus_a... p. 28).
    
- Significance & Context: Named after Otto Hölder. This proof confirms that $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ is uniformly continuous on $[0, \infty)$ despite having an unbounded derivative (infinite steepness) at $x = 0$ (calculus_a... pp. 27-28).

## 3. The Mechanics of Uniform Continuity Boundaries

- Solution ($f(x) = x^2$): On a closed bounded interval $[a, b]$, the difference quotient is controlled by the maximum domain values (calculus_a... p. 26):  
    $$\vert{}x^2 - x_0^2\vert{} = \vert{}x - x_0\vert{}\vert{}x + x_0\vert{} \leq 2(\vert{}b\vert{} + \vert{}a\vert{})\vert{}x - x_0\vert{}$$  
    This provides a fixed global modulus $\delta = \frac{\epsilon}{2(\vert{}b\vert{} + \vert{}a\vert{})}$ (calculus_a... p. 26). However, on an infinite interval, as $x \to \infty$, an arbitrarily small shift $\Delta x$ yields an infinitely large output shift $\Delta y$, violating uniformity (calculus_a... p. 26).
- Solution ($f(x) = 1/x$): On $[a, b]$ for $a > 0$, the denominator cannot drop below $a$, bounding the rate of change by $\delta = a^2\epsilon$ (calculus_a... p. 26). On the open interval $(0, 1)$, as $x$ approaches the origin, the slope shoots to infinity, making a global $\delta$ choice impossible (calculus_a... p. 27).
- Significance & Context: This tracks Heine’s Theorem (or the Heine-Cantor Theorem): _A function continuous on a closed, bounded (compact) set is automatically uniformly continuous on that set_ (calculus_a... p. 26).

## 4. Uniform Modulus Contradiction for $1/x$ (Scaffolded Proof)

- Proof:
    
    1. Assume for contradiction that a uniform modulus $\delta$ exists for a chosen $\epsilon = 1$ on $(0, 1)$ (calculus_a... p. 27).

- Significance: This classical proof-by-contradiction highlights that open boundaries require local, adaptive coordinate windows rather than flat global constants (calculus_a... pp. 25, 27).

## 5. Oscillatory Topology: Bounded vs. Unbounded Discontinuities

- Solution ($f(x) = \sin(1/x)$): As $x \to 0$, the argument $1/x$ grows infinitely, compressing the standard sine wave. The function values continuously swing between $-1$ and $+1$ an infinite number of times inside any neighborhood of zero (calculus_a... pp. 21, 23). Because the function approaches multiple different limit values simultaneously, the discontinuity is permanent and cannot be resolved (calculus_a... pp. 19, 24).
- Solution ($f(x) = x \sin(1/x)$): While the $1/x$ core still triggers infinite oscillations, the leading multiplier $x$ works as a compression envelope (calculus_a... pp. 23-24). As $x \to 0$, the magnitude of these oscillations is crushed down to zero (calculus_a... p. 23). By defining the continuous extension $f(0) = 0$, the singularity is cleanly removed (calculus_a... pp. 19, 24).
- Significance: This illustrates Removable Singularities vs. Essential Oscillatory Discontinuities (calculus_a... pp. 19, 24).

## 6. The Sequential Characterization of Functional Limits

- Theorem: $\lim_{x \to \xi} f(x) = \eta$ if and only if for every sequence $x_n \to \xi$ (with $x_n$ in the domain of $f$ and $x_n \neq \xi$), the output sequence satisfies $\lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) = \eta$ (calculus_a... p. 13).
- Proof (Forward Direction): If the functional limit exists, then for a given $\epsilon > 0$, we can choose a window $\delta > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x) - \eta\vert{} < \epsilon$ whenever $\vert{}x - \xi\vert{} < \delta$ (calculus_a... p. 13). Since the input sequence $x_n$ converges to $\xi$, we can find an index threshold $N$ such that $\vert{}x_n - \xi\vert{} < \delta$ for all $n > N$ (calculus_a... p. 11, calculus_a... p. 13). Substituting these terms directly satisfies the output boundary $\vert{}f(x_n) - \eta\vert{} < \epsilon$, proving that $f(x_n) \to \eta$ (calculus_a... pp. 13-14).
- Proof (Reverse Direction via Contradiction): Assume that $f(x_n) \to \eta$ for every sequence $x_n \to \xi$, but assume the functional limit _fails_ to equal $\eta$ (calculus_a... p. 14). If it fails, there must exist an $\epsilon_0 > 0$ such that for any choice of $\delta = 1/n$, there is a point $x_n$ satisfying $\vert{}x_n - \xi\vert{} < 1/n$ but $\vert{}f(x_n) - \eta\vert{} \geq \epsilon_0$ (calculus_a... p. 14). This constructs a specific sequence $x_n$ that converges directly to $\xi$ but whose outputs structurally fail to converge to $\eta$, breaking our initial condition (calculus_a... p. 14). The functional limit must equal $\eta$ (calculus_a... p. 14).
- Significance & Context: This is the Heine Definition of a Functional Limit. It links continuous calculus with the discrete infrastructure of sequence spaces, allowing mathematicians to translate properties between both domains (calculus_a... pp. 2, 13).

## 7. The Uniqueness and Boundedness of Limit Boundaries

- Uniqueness Proof: Assume for contradiction that a sequence possesses two distinct limits, $l$ and $l'$ (calculus_a... p. 2). Let the distance between them be $\Delta = \vert{}l - l'\vert{} > 0$. If we set our target neighborhood error threshold to a size less than half this distance ($\epsilon = \Delta/2$), we can draw non-overlapping intervals around $l$ and $l'$ (calculus_a... p. 2). By definition, the neighborhood around $l$ must contain all but a finite number of sequence terms, leaving only a finite pool available for the $l'$ interval (calculus_a... p. 2). This contradicts the rule that $l'$ must also contain infinitely many terms past its own threshold (calculus_a... p. 2). The limit is unique (calculus_a... p. 2).
- Boundedness Proof: Let $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = l$. Choosing a standard test error step $\epsilon = 1$, the limit definition guarantees that all terms past a specific index threshold $N$ fall inside the interval $(l-1, l+1)$ (calculus_a... p. 2). The only elements that can lie outside this region are the finite collection of preceding tail terms: $\{a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{N-1}\}$ (calculus_a... p. 2). We can pick a global bounding constant:  
    $$M = \max(\vert{}l-1\vert{}, \vert{}l+1\vert{}, \vert{}a_1\vert{}, \vert{}a_2\vert{}, \dots, \vert{}a_{N-1}\vert{})$$  
    This ensures $\vert{}a_n\vert{} \leq M$ for all $n$, proving the sequence is bounded (calculus_a... p. 2).
- Significance: These represent foundational stability theorems in analysis. They ensure that stable operations do not manifest multiple outcomes or escape to infinity prematurely (calculus_a... p. 2).

## 8. The Product of Limits Derivation

- Proof:
    
    1. We analyze the distance from the target using the text's balanced identity (calculus_a... p. 3):  
        $$\vert{}ab - a_n b_n\vert{} = \vert{}b(a - a_n) + a_n(b - b_n)\vert{}$$
    2. Apply the Triangle Inequality to split the product components (calculus_a... p. 14, calculus_a... p. 3):  
        $$\vert{}ab - a_n b_n\vert{} \leq \vert{}b\vert{}\vert{}a - a_n\vert{} + \vert{}a_n\vert{}\vert{}b - b_n\vert{}$$
    3. Since $a_n$ is a convergent sequence, it is bounded by a constant $M$, meaning $\vert{}a_n\vert{} < M$ for all $n$ (calculus_a... pp. 2-3). Substituting this into the inequality yields (calculus_a... p. 3):  
        $$\vert{}ab - a_n b_n\vert{} \leq \vert{}b\vert{}\vert{}a - a_n\vert{} + M\vert{}b - b_n\vert{}$$
    4. For a chosen $\epsilon > 0$, we can choose our individual sequence steps small enough so that $\vert{}a - a_n\vert{} < \frac{\epsilon}{\vert{}b\vert{} + M}$ and $\vert{}b - b_n\vert{} < \frac{\epsilon}{\vert{}b\vert{} + M}$ (calculus_a... p. 3). Combining these bounds yields:  
        $$\vert{}ab - a_n b_n\vert{} < \vert{}b\vert{}\left(\frac{\epsilon}{\vert{}b\vert{} + M}\right) + M\left(\frac{\epsilon}{\vert{}b\vert{} + M}\right) = \epsilon$$  
        This completes the validation, proving $\lim_{n \to \infty} (a_n b_n) = ab$ (calculus_a... p. 3).
    
- Significance: This is the formal proof of the Algebraic Limit Theorem for Products (calculus_a... pp. 2-3).

- Proof:
    
    1. Using the Binomial Theorem to expand $T_n = (1 + 1/n)^n$ yields (calculus_a... p. 10):

    2. Because every term inside the parentheses $\left(1 - \frac{k}{n}\right)$ is strictly less than 1, replacing them with 1 creates a term-by-term upper bound (calculus_a... pp. 10-11):  
        $$T_n \leq 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2!} + \frac{1}{3!} + \dots + \frac{1}{n!} = s_n$$

    3. To show they converge to the exact same value, select a secondary index $m > n$ (calculus_a... p. 11). The expansion can be written as (calculus_a... p. 11):  
        $$T_m > 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2!}\left(1 - \frac{1}{m}\right) + \dots + \frac{1}{n!}\left(1 - \frac{1}{m}\right)\dots\left(1 - \frac{n-1}{m}\right)$$

    5. This creates the permanent double inequality $T \geq s_n \geq T_n$ (calculus_a... p. 11). Finally, allowing $n \to \infty$ forces the lower and upper bounds to compress around the same coordinate, proving $T = \lim_{n \to \infty} s_n = e$ (calculus_a... p. 11).
    
- Significance: This proof establishes the bridge between the compound interest limit and the exponential Taylor series definition of $e$ (calculus_a... pp. 7, 10).

## 11. Analytical Construction of Irrational Exponentials

- Solution: Since an irrational exponent $\alpha$ cannot be directly evaluated via roots and powers, it must be constructed via rational approximations (calculus_a... p. 6, calculus_a... p. 17). Let $(r_n)$ be a sequence of rational numbers converging to $\alpha$ (calculus_a... p. 17). We analyze the sequence of rational power outputs $a^{r_n}$ using the Cauchy convergence test (calculus_a... p. 17):  
    $$\vert{}a^{r_n} - a^{r_m}\vert{} = a^{r_m}\vert{}a^{\delta} - 1\vert{} \quad \text{where } \delta = r_n - r_m$$  
    Since the rational terms $r_n$ converge, they are bounded, making $a^{r_m}$ bounded (calculus_a... pp. 17-18). As $n, m \to \infty$, the distance $\delta \to 0$ (calculus_a... pp. 17-18). By applying Bernoulli's linear root limits ($a^{1/l} \to 1$), the term $\vert{}a^\delta - 1\vert{}$ is driven to zero (calculus_a... p. 5, calculus_a... p. 18). This confirms that $a^{r_n}$ is a Cauchy sequence, which guarantees it converges to a unique, well-defined real number named $a^\alpha$ (calculus_a... pp. 5, 17).
- Significance: This construction extends exponentiation from discrete rational steps into a continuous functional field, laying the groundwork for calculus operations on exponential and logarithmic paths (calculus_a... p. 5, calculus_a... p. 18).
