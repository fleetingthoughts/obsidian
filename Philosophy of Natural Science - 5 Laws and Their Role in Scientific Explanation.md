Main idea: 
- What is the point of scientific process. The product of science: the explanation
- Hempel outlines his requirements and the model for a good explanation: the deductive nomological model (Covering law mode)
Outline
- Characteristics of a good explanation: 
	- Explanatory relevance: the explanation gives reason for the phenomenon to actually occur. Explanatory relevance is necessary for a good explanation but is not sufficient.
	- Requirements of testability. This is weaker than the first one. Explanatory relevance implies testability but not the converse.
- Th D-N model overview: Explanans deductively deduced from explandum
	- The deductive reasoning gives relevance to the explanans
	- Testability requirements are met since it specifies the conditions that a phenomena occurs
- Elements of the D-N model: characteristics of a good covering law:
	- D-N model provides a universal connection between law and consequence that always happen (deductively) so they must be of universal form. See underlined quote in p.54 (expanded for approximations) 
	- example of bad covering law in "accidental generalizations" showing being a universal statement is only a necessary but not sufficient condition for a good covering law.
	- Distinguish accidental generalizations from good universal laws by Nelson Goodman by how they support hypothetical or conditional statements in the future.
		- Supports all counterfactual conditionals
		- Supports all subjunctive conditionals
# Terms
- Explanans: The thing doing the explaining. Colloquially, the antecedent, or set of assumptions.
- Explandum: The statement that is being explained. Colloquially, the consequent, conclusion or the final product
- Counterfactual Conditionals: Statements that are not classically logical and presumes the possibility that the antecedent is either true or false. The difference with classical logic is that the antecedent is never a null set that is always false so while logic may have [vacuous Truths](The%20Empty%20Set%20and%20Vacuous%20Truths.md), counterfactual conditionals in natural language cannot.  : "If $A$ had been the case, then $B$ would be the case"
- Subjunctive Conditionals: Some sources say that the subjunctive conditional is the same as counterfactual conditionals but is a characteristic of natural languages being in the subjunctive mood: "If $A$ should come to pass, then so would $B$"
# Historical Examples and People
- ***Fact, Fiction, and Forecast.***  by Nelson Goodman provided the ideas of counterfactual conditionals and subjunctive conditionals
