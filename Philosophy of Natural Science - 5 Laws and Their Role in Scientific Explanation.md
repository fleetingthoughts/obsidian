The previous chapters went over the characteristics and limitations in the two main elements of scientific inquiry in testing and hypothesis generation; and the criteria for soundness of argument in confirmation and acceptance, this chapter aims to provide a model for a product produced by scientific inquiry: an explanation. I would consider this to be the most important chapter that ties together the whole book as it describes one of the end goals and every element of a scientific inquiry is tied together in achieving a proper explanation. We describe the product of scientific inquiry as follows:
- The two characteristics of a good explanation: explanatory relevance (the stronger requirement), requirement of testability (weaker because it is implied by explanatory relevance).
- Hempel's proposed model for a scientific explanation: the deductive-nomological model abbreviated as D-N model and also known as the covering law model
- The requirement of covering law to be in universal form and how it is distinguished from accidental generalizations: subjunctive/counterfactual conditionals.
- Probabilistic explanations

Scientific explanations must meet two systematic requirements:
1) ***Explanatory relevance***: The explanation must be relevant in the sense that it gives a reason for why the phenomena or thing being explained would occur the way they occur. As we shall see with the D-N model, the <u>relevance will be a result of the fact that the occurrence of the phenomena is deduced from more general statements</u>
2) ***Requirement of testability***: The explanation must offer conditions that can be tested in-principle. 
Hempel notes that 1) is the stronger requirement as it implies 2), but the converse does not hold, that is a testable explanation does not imply it is relevant to the phenomena at hand. Furthermore, both these conditions are necessary for a scientific explanation but not sufficient as it is possible to offer an explanation that is relevant for a phenomena, but it may not answer the question "why".

Hempel proposes a model for a scientific explanation that meets both those criteria while also answering the question of "why" called the deductive-nomological model. The components of the model are as follows:
- ***Explandum phenomenon***: the phenomenon to be accounted for and explained
- ***Explandum sentence***: the sentence describing the explandum phenomenon
- ***Explanans***: the sentences providing the explanatory information that meet the two criteria of an explanation
- ***Covering law***: the laws invoked in the explanans that are subsumed. This provides the "why"
The explanation in essence is the explanans from which the explandum phenomena deductively follows and the explanans itself deductively follows from the covering law: $$\text{Covering law(s)}\implies \text{explanan(s)}\implies \text{explandum}$$
From this chain of deductions, the D-N model meets both requirements of an explanation. The deduction necessitates the explandum providing relevance and the testing conditions are specified in the antecedent covering laws. I would also note that the covering laws are assumed to have empirical import. The direction of the implication also gives reason for the "cause and effect" which answers the question of "why" something occurs. If the conditions of the covering laws are met, then the phenomena occurs. "Same cause, same effect (p. 53)".

The end goal of a scientific inquiry is to produce explanations of this kind and to discover the components that can explain a phenomena, that is, scientific inquiry aims to find the covering laws and the explanans for the explandum. And so as Hempel notes: "an explanation rests on general laws is not to say that its discovery required the discovery of the laws." The covering law can be an empiricism of the available facts until a more suitable covering law takes its place in the same way Balmer's empirical formula explains the hydrogen emission spectrum until a Bohr's law provides a stronger covering law. Hempel's example with the hydrogen emission spectrum hints at the potential ambiguity in the direction of implication that an explanans can come from. We can adequately explain the emission spectrum from either a formula or with Bohr's law but he subtly implies that Bohr's law is the stronger one as it implies Balmer's formula as well. The ambiguity in the direction of implication of an explanation as to which is the antecedent and which is the consequent and Hempel's subsequent course correction by choosing the "stronger" covering law by the one that implies all others is the essence of the [Unificationism model of explanation that corrects the asymmetry problem of the D-N model](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2013%20Explanation.md).

In order to answer the question of "why" and provide explanatory relevance, the deductive nature of the covering law model makes it irrespective of time and place, and so in that sense, it must be universal. The statements must have a sort of uniform conformity. "It is a statement to the effect that whenever and wherever conditions of a specified kind occur, then so will, always and without exception, certain conditions of another kind (p. 54)". 

The requirement of universality is a necessary but not a sufficient case as Hempel provides a counterexample in universal statements of a form that "happens to be the case" called accidental generalizations such as "all rocks in this box contain iron". Accidental generalizations are universal statements that accidentally support a certain class of objects but do not apply to all objects of that class. Goodman provides clarity on this issue by providing the following two criteria to distinguish a proper law from accidental generalizations:
1) ***A law can support all counterfactual conditionals***: sentences that describe hypothetical scenarios that presuppose the possibility that the antecedent can be true or false, but never just one of the two (this avoid [vacuous truths](The%20Empty%20Set%20and%20Vacuous%20Truths.md)). So an accidental generalization cannot support sentences like "<u>If </u>this paraffin candle had been put in a kettle of boiling water, then it would melt (p. 56)".
2) ***A law can support all subjunctive conditionals:*** similar to counterfactual statements but in the subjunctive form. Accidental generalizations cannot support statements of the form "If this candle <u>should</u> be put in boiling water, it would melt".
Hempel notes it is tempting (as I have done), to classify accidental generalizations as those that only apply to a finite number of objects. He gives the example that "all bodies of pure gold have mass less than 100,000 kg" as being an obviously wrong law but true by virtue of us not yet finding a counterexample. But his example seems shaky to me, because you can definitely frame it to support counterfactual conditionals. Hempel does provide a caveat that gives him an out on this issue by clarifying that  "whether a statement of universal form counts as a law will depend in part upon the scientific theories accepted at the time."

The rise of scientific theories offering probabilistic accounts challenges the D-N model as probabilistic outcomes cannot follow deductively from a law. Hempel begins by noting the standing issues with probabilistic explanations by the common probabilistic explanation of drawing colored balls from an urn. In this example, if there are 600 white balls and 400 red balls, then the explanation is that the probability of drawing a white ball is commonly said to be 0.6. Some issues Hempel notes include
1) What do the probabilities mean
2) A probabilistic interpretation based on the number of successes out of the total possible amount works for the finite case, but fails in the infinite case.
3) The classical frequentist approach of probabilities as ratios given a hypothetically infinite number even infinite amount still requires a priori assumptions such as "equiprobability" which leads to circular reasoning. An example of these assumed a priori assumptions failing can be found in physics with the breakdown of the parity principle.

The main issue with probabilistic explanations, is that deductive methods such as modus tollens no longer apply, and so "appropriate standards are called for" instead (p. 65) which falls in the realm of statistics.

Hempel further notes a distinction between laws and probabilistic laws are in the claims. While the two share the common characteristic of making universal statements that support counterfactual and subjunctive conditionals, they fundamentally differ in the claims they make and therefore the way they are supported is different as a result. The difference in claims also results in a difference in their explanation. Hempel specifically distinguish deductive-nomological explanations as those that are deduced from a covering law of universal form while probabilistic explanations are inductive in nature from laws that make probabilistic claims. The main difference, to me, appears that we cannot apply deductive logic to produce probabilistic explanations.


# Terms
- Explanans: The thing doing the explaining. Colloquially, the antecedent, or set of assumptions.
- Explandum: The statement that is being explained. Colloquially, the consequent, conclusion or the final product
- Counterfactual Conditionals: Statements that are not classically logical and presumes the possibility that the antecedent is either true or false. The difference with classical logic is that the antecedent is never a null set that is always false so while logic may have [vacuous Truths](The%20Empty%20Set%20and%20Vacuous%20Truths.md), counterfactual conditionals in natural language cannot.  : "If $A$ had been the case, then $B$ would be the case"
- Subjunctive Conditionals: Some sources say that the subjunctive conditional is the same as counterfactual conditionals but is a characteristic of natural languages being in the subjunctive mood: "If $A$ should come to pass, then so would $B$"
