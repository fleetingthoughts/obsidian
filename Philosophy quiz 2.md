## 🔑 Complete Master Answer Key (Questions 71–105)

## 💡 Day 1 Answers (Additions)

1. Explicit Definitions vs. Definitions-in-Use (_Ayer, Ch. 3_). An explicit definition provides a direct vocabulary translation (synonym). A definition-in-use translates an entire sentence containing an ambiguous symbol into an alternative sentence that contains no such symbols or synonyms, exposing its underlying logical construction.
2. The Reduction of Terms (_Hempel, Ch. 8_). Requires that all technical terms of the target science (e.g., biology) be completely redefined using the foundational vocabulary of the primary science (e.g., physics and chemistry).
3. Direct empirical outcomes can be deeply deceptive due to hidden variables (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 1_). Pettenkofer's lucky survival (due to high stomach acid or a dead culture) proves that a single positive or negative empirical test provides no foolproof structural guarantee of a theory's truth value.
4. No, they remain mere hypotheses (_Ayer, Ch. 5_). Because synthetic claims refer to an infinite line of potential future sensations, no finite series of observations can ever establish them with logical certainty; they can only achieve high operational probability.
5. A puzzle has a guaranteed solution within the paradigm rules; a problem does not (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 5_). Normal science solves puzzles using known algorithmic rules of the established matrix. A true problem threatens the matrix itself and lacks a guaranteed recipe for a solution.

## 💡 Day 2 Answers (Additions)

1. They rule out data-rigging/ad-hoc adjustments (_Hempel, Ch. 4_). Predictions that successfully anticipate entirely "new" or uncalculated physical observations provide immense confirmation value, whereas a formula engineered to fit a known historical data bank could simply be a complex mathematical artifact.
2. Realism holds that reality is mind-independent; constructivism argues reality is built by human culture (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 12_). Realists assert natural laws exist regardless of human thought. Constructivists believe the structural entities of science are social artifacts manufactured by the matrix of human conceptual systems.
3. Treating existence as a logical attribute (_Ayer, Ch. 2_). _“I exist”_ does not deductively follow from _“there is a thought now,”_ because an isolated event does not logically entail a continuous substantive self.
4. Universal laws support counterfactual conditionals; accidental generalizations do not (_Hempel, Ch. 5_). A true universal law states a structural constant of nature (e.g., _"All gold spheres have a diameter under 1 mile"_ due to critical mass limitations), whereas an accidental generalization is an empirical coincidence (e.g., _"All coins in my pocket are silver"_).
5. They undergo a total visual and conceptual transformation (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 6_). The scientist does not merely adjust a data calculation; they read identical sensory data inputs through a completely different conceptual framework, effectively working in a different visual world.

## 💡 Day 3 Answers (Additions)

1. Emotivism strips moral claims of truth value; Utilitarianism defines morals as empirical calculations (_Ayer, Ch. 6_). Utilitarianism treats value assertions as synthetic claims about happiness. Ayer rejects this, arguing ethical symbols are entirely non-factual, emotive expressions of personal feeling and command.
2. Traditions coexist and compete simultaneously; Paradigms rule exclusively (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 7_). Kuhn held that only one dominant paradigm can control a science during normal operations. Laudan argued that multiple research traditions routinely toggle, compete, and overlap within the same historical timeline.
3. It fails to establish clear explanatory laws connecting it to other concepts (_Hempel, Ch. 7_). A concept might possess clear operational criteria (empirical import), but if it cannot be integrated into a mathematical network of universal laws, it remains systemically useless to natural science.
4. By treating material objects as logical constructions out of sensations rather than spiritual effects (_Ayer, Ch. 2_). Ayer strips away Berkeley's unobservable theological cause (God) and retains his phenomenalist logic: things are defined entirely by the relational consistency of their immediate sense-contents.
5. All beliefs must be explained by identical social and psychological causes, regardless of whether they are true or false (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 8_). Symmetrical explanation dictates that we do not explain true science by "logic" and false science by "bias"; both are structural outcomes of social environments.

## 💡 Day 4 Answers (Additions)

1. D-N demands absolute deductive certainty; Probabilistic updates provide only inductive support (_Hempel, Ch. 5_). D-N explanations guarantee the event follows necessarily from the premises. Probabilistic explanations derive an event from statistical frequencies, meaning the premises can be entirely true while the conclusion remains merely highly probable.
2. The logical anomaly that looking at a white shoe confirms the law that all ravens are black (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 3_). Because _"All ravens are black"_ is logically equivalent to _"All non-black things are non-ravens,"_ looking at any object that is neither black nor a raven provides a minute unit of mathematical confirmation to the original statement under simple verification models.
3. Analysis is concerned with how we speak, not with spatial dissection (_Ayer, Ch. 2_). Philosophical analysis does not imply that physical reality is structurally composed of isolated atomistic bits; it merely clarifies the logical structure of our linguistic conventions.
4. They connect abstract internal theoretical terms to measurable observation points (_Hempel, Ch. 6_). Without these "bridge principles," an advanced mathematical formula remains a closed, self-referential linguistic loop with zero connection to physical laboratory tools.
5. The Alan Sokal Hoax (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 9_). Physicist Alan Sokal submitted a intentionally nonsensical parody article to a leading cultural studies journal; its publication proved that parts of postmodern academia lacked rigorous criteria for distinguishing real science analysis from empty verbal choreography.

## 💡 Day 5 Answers (Additions)

1. No individual can independently test scientific frameworks; science requires networks of trust (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 1, 7_). mainstreams empiricism ignores that scientific discovery relies on inherited testimony, division of specialized labor, and historical lineages of peer validation.
2. They function simply as assertion and negation flags, anticipating the Deflationary/Redundancy Theory of Truth (_Ayer, Ch. 5_). To say _"It is true that it is raining"_ is logically identical to simply asserting _"It is raining."_ The term adds zero conceptual metadata; it merely reinforces linguistic assertion.
3. It ties a concept directly to a specific testing procedure; it limits a term to a single test (_Hempel, Ch. 7_). Under strict operationalism, if you change your physical measuring tool (e.g., shifting from a mercury thermometer to an electronic sensor), you have technically invented a completely new concept, destroying systemic unification.
4. Science aims for empirical adequacy regarding observables, not absolute reality regarding unobservables (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 12_). Constructive empiricists argue that a theory succeeds if its claims about observable items match our physical laboratory findings; demanding that its mathematical maps of unobservables (e.g., quarks) reveal absolute reality is an unscientific metaphysical leap.
5. Because religion deals in metaphysical claims that possess zero literal meaning (_Ayer, Ch. 6_). Since theological assertions about a transcendent realm yield no test implications, they cannot logically contradict or intersect with the synthetic hypotheses of natural science.

## 💡 Day 6 Answers (Additions)

1. Terms must be explicitly defined; Laws must be deductively derived (_Hempel, Ch. 8_). The complete reduction of a science requires that its technical concepts be translated into primary terms (reduction of terms) _and_ its core behavioral regularities be mathematically derived from primary laws (reduction of laws).
2. The logical problem that identical evidence supports mutually exclusive future predictions (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 3_). If we define the predicate "grue" as an object examined before a future date that is green, but blue after that date, then every green emerald examined up to this point confirms both that all emeralds are green _and_ that all emeralds are grue—yielding conflicting future expectations from identical evidence lines.
3. It mistakes a grammatical feature for a logical necessity (_Ayer, Ch. 8_). Thinkers are misled by superficial phrasing into assuming a thing is separate from its qualities. Logical analysis reveals that a thing is simply a logical construct out of the total relationship of its appearances.
4. We can specify what specific sensations _would_ validate it, even if we currently lack the tools (_Hempel, Ch. 3 / Ayer, Ch. 1_). Torricelli's moon-gravity hypotheses were testable-in-principle in the 17th century because thinkers knew what observations would verify them, despite lacking the rocketry required to execute the test.
5. The community optimizes progress by balancing high-risk innovators with conservative stabilizers (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 11_). A healthy scientific ecosystem operates efficiently because different individuals pursue alternative reward pathways, distributing risks across competing theoretical investments.

## 💡 Day 7 Answers (Additions)

1. It uses physical entity language to describe a non-physical parameter (_Ayer, Ch. 7_). To claim an empirical entity survives structural organic dissolution while simultaneously stripping that entity of all possible spatial and observation properties is a logical contradiction that strips the statement of meaning.
2. It confuses personal psychological comfort with objective logical explanation (_Hempel, Ch. 6_). A valid explanation does not require a phenomenon to be translated into everyday, familiar metaphors; it requires the phenomenon to be subsumed under precisely calculated universal laws.
3. It dictates the scientist's baseline confidence in a theory _before_ integrating new data (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 14_). Under Bayesian probability formulas, the strength of your final confidence depends heavily on your original subjective or objective assessment of the theory's plausibility before the new test implication was run.
4. It lacks a single designator; analysis maps out its distinct logical identities (_Ayer, Ch. 3_). Ambiguous symbols create deep category mistakes. Analysis resolves this by translating the symbol into distinct, non-overlapping operational phrases, dissolving the phantom problem.
5. Objectivity shifts from an individual logical algorithm to an institutional community standard (_Godfrey-Smith, Ch. 2, 4, 11 / Hempel, Ch. 2 / Ayer, Ch. 2_). Because no clean, mechanical discovery recipe can manufacture a hypothesis out of cold data, scientific objectivity is preserved by the public, social structure of testing—demanding independent replication, institutional peer critique, and systemic exposure to the observational veto (1).
