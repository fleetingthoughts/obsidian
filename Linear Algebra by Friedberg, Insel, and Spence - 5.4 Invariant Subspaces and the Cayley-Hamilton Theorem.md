Invariant subspaces are relative defined relative to a given vector space and transformation. They are subspaces of the domain vector space that are preserved under the given linear transformation. Vectors of a $T$-invariant subspace stay in the same subspace after transformation.

Every vector space must have a invariant subspace (for any linear transformation), because the $T$-cyclic subspace can be generated from any element in the vector space. They are defined based on two properties:
- The generation method from any general vector in the vector space by a given transformation $T$.
- The invariance to any linear transformation.
The $T$-cyclic subspace is the "smallest" $T$-invariant subspace containing a vector $x$ meaning any $T$-invariant subspace must contain the $T$-cyclic subspace. Note that this is for any general vector $x$. The eigenspace may look smaller (but it is in fact a cyclic subspace) but it is not for any general $x$.

Invariant subspaces serve as building blocks that let us decompose transformations into constituent parts. We can look at pieces of a linear transformation at time by looking at its "restriction". The restriction is defined by two things:
- The linear transformation being restricted
- The invariant subspace that we're restricting the domain of the transformation to create the new restricted transformation

Because the cyclic subspace generated by the given $T$ is invariant to $T$, If we can furnish properties of the cyclic subspace as formalized in ***Theorem 5.2.2*** if we have or determine the following:
- A choice of vector
- A given linear transformation $T$
- Determine the dimension of the linear transformation
With the listed items, we can then determine the following about the cyclic subspace:
- The basis for the cyclic subspace of a transformation is the powers of the transformation
- The characteristic polynomial of the restriction on the cyclic subspace is a $k$-th polynomial with coefficients determined by the particular $k+1$ linear combination of powers of the transformation. Since the dimension of the cyclic subspace is $k$ any $k+1$ vectors must be linearly dependent so the coefficients aren't 0.

This is then used to prove the Cayley-Hamilton theorem which states that a linear transformation satisfies its own characteristic equation. Replacing the variables of the characteristic polynomial  with $T(v)$ so that $p_T(\lambda)$ becomes $p_T(T(v))$ results in 0. The transformed variable is its own "root".
# Definitions
***Definition (Invariant subspace).*** Given a linear transformation $T: V\to V$, a subspace $W\subseteq V$ is called a $T$-invariant subspace if $T(W)\subseteq W$. In other words, an invariant subspace is preserved under a transformation such that $T(v) \in W$ for all $v\in W$.

***Definition (T-cyclic subspace).*** Given a linear transformation $T: V \to V$, the $T$-cyclic subspace of $V$ generated by a vector $x\in V$ is the following invariant subspace: $$W=span\{x, T(x),T^2(x),T^3(x),\dots\}$$
***Definition (Restriction).*** Given a linear transformation $T: V\to V$ and a $T$-invariant subspace $W$, the restriction is the linear transformation with the domain restricted to vectors in $W$, that is, the restriction of $T$ on $W$ is $T_{W}:W\to W$ defined by $T_W(w)=T(w)$ for  $w\in W$
