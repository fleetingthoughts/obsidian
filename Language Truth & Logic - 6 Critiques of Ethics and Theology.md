We deal with the proposition that statements of value and aesthetic are synthetic propositions that cannot be set up as hypotheses to set up the expectations of our senses and therefore conflict with the verifiability criteria. We reject this notion on the basis that statements in ethics are emotive rather propositions where the validity is to be tested and apply this to aesthetics:
1) We categorize ethics into 4 classes and immediately banish 2 of them to psychological inquiries
2) We look at the Subjectivist and Utilitarian assertions of ethics as reducible to propositions of factual and empirical senses of satisfaction. and show it is inconsistent with our form of philosophical analysis.
3) We reject the assertion that denying subjectivist and utilitarian forms of ethics permits only an absolutist view of ethics that is based on unverifiable intuition and is therefore literal nonsense. 
4) We reject it on the basis that there is another form of ethics to take that is compatible with empiricism. We argue absolutist ethics do not makes propositions at all that add any factual content. They are literal nonsense. Therefore all ethical propositions only add an emotive element to actual propositions. "It is wrong to steal" is translated to "YOU STOLE!!".
5) We reject that our position is a radical subjectivist position as subjectivism attempts to validate propositions by one's own personal feelings, but the type of propositions analyzed are not about feelings.
	1) Subjectivist assert the existence of feelings is necessary and sufficient to propositions of judgements.
	2) We assert they are propositions said with feeling not that they have feeling
6) We reject G.E Moore's claim that ethical propositions cannot be emotive or be equivalent to one's personal feelings. Moore's argument criticizes ours as well as our claim is unfounded as it means we cannot argue about value judgements at all. The issue is Moore presupposes that these arguments argue about value at all and we counter Moore on the  grounds that the ethical propositions that Moore presupposes are not empirical propositions at all to begin with.
	1) Ayer's argument feels anecdotal. He argues that most arguments about value argue about the facts of the case rather than convincing someone to change their moral beliefs.
7) The arguments against ethics are the same against aesthetics as we argue they are statements about one's emotions and not factual propositions
8) Goes into length arguing against theology that echoes the arguments from Chapter 1 but serves as a summary of the points of the past 4 chapters to reinforce those arguments from chapter 1:
	1) Theology claims the absolute existence of a god
	2) From Chapter 1 and 5 empirical propositions are weakly verified and make a hypothesis more probable. They are not certainties
	3) The only certainties are an a priori and empirical facts cannot be deduced from them. As argued in chapter 4, propositions and deduction from a priori are certainties that do not require verification so they do not satisfy the verification criteria for synthetic statements. In other words, they are tautologies.'
9) We argue that the supposed "verifiable evidence" of the existence of a god such as the uniformity of nature, only supports propositions in which god entails these uniformities, but they do not support the actual propositions by theologists. The theologists true propositions extends far past a uniform manifestation of phenomena, which the uniformity of nature does not support.
10) We distinguish our arguments from atheists, and agnostics in that we are not arguing against the validity of the proposition, we argue that the propositions themselves are nonsense.
11) On the same grounds as 10), we argue that there is no conflict between religion and science and they do not contradict one another for one deals with factual sentences and the other is literal nonsense so they cannot stand in a logical relation with each other much less a logical contradiction.
12) Ayer's philosophy says nothing about the means by which people arrive at propositions. We only impose a criteria on the end product to be verifiable. And so we argue that a supposed "mystic" who makes claims of factual nature on the basis that they have some divine intuition, we argue that their divine intuition has revealed no actual facts as they are unable to communicate them as propositions with literal significance (i.e. verifiable).
