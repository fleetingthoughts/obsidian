The fundamental theorem of calculus is effectively an existence and non-uniqueness theorem of the antiderivative. The first part of the FTC shows that a primitive function exists in the form of the indefinite integral, and the second part of the FTC shows that the primitive function is not a unique solution. The first part of the fundamental theorem of calculus establishes that the indefinite integral is a primitive function (antiderivative) of the function, so why do we bother defining a primitive function at all. The key is that we have demonstrated that the indefinite integral of a function is a primitive function (sufficiency equation), we haven't proven that all primitive functions are indefinite integrals (necessity condition) and indeed the indefinite integral and the primitive function are not equivalent. This is where the 2nd part of the mean value theorem comes in. The [Proof of the second part of the fundamental theorem of calculus (Newton-Leibniz Theorem)](Proof%20of%20the%20second%20part%20of%20the%20fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus%20(Newton-Leibniz%20Theorem).md) does not depend on the mean value theorems.

It was noted that the solution to an integral is not unique as it depends on what we set our lower limit, but as the 2nd part of the FTC shows, once we know one primitive solutions, we can determine all of them if we can determine the constant. The goal is then: if we know the primitive of a function, can we evaluate any definite integral. Given a definite integral and a known primitive function that we know differs from it by only a constant: $$F(x)+c = \phi(x)=\int^x_{a}f(u)du$$
We hence have two equations and two unknowns in $c$ and $\phi(x)$. The indefinite integral is 0 at $x=a$ so then we determine $c=-F(a)$. The result is that once we know the primitive $F(x)$ of a function $f(x)$, we can evaluate any definite integral using just our primitive function and plugging values into it:$$F(b)-F(a)=\int_{a}^b f(u)du$$
The use of the <u>difference in primitive functions to evaluate definite integrals</u>, as we had shown that the difference of  [indefinite integrals also calculates the definite integral](Introduction%20to%20Calculus%20I%20by%20Fritz%20and%20Courant%20-%202.4%20The%20Integral%20as%20a%20Function%20of%20the%20Upper%20Limit.md). But this proof just demonstrates consistency in the logic between the antiderivative in the fundamental theorem of calculus and the indefinite integral. We needed the indefinite integral to develop the MVT which developed the fundamental theorem of calculus, and we then show that antiderivative solutions are consistent with the results from our original definition of the indefinite integral.

As an extension of this, we can multiply both sides by $1/(b-a)$: $$\frac{F(b)-F(a)}{b-a}=\frac{1}{b-a}\int_{a}^b F'(u)du$$
And this shows that the difference quotient, is precisely our intuitive understanding of the average: It is the average of the instantaneous velocities

It is also worth commenting on the relationship between the two mean value theorems of integration and differentiation and the fundamental theorem of calculus. The fundamental theorem of calculus serves as the bridge between the two mean value theorems. It is tempting to say we can derive the MVT of integration, prove the fundamental theorem of calculus and then us both results to derive the MVT of differentiation, but one nuance in Richard Courant's [proof of the first part of the fundamental theorem of calculus](Proof%20of%20the%20first%20part%20of%20the%20fundamental%20theorem%20of%20calculus.md), but there is a slight nuance in that <u>we assume the derivative of the primitive function to exist.</u> The problem is that the MVT of derivatives makes the most assumptions by requiring the existence of differentiation which is more restricting requirement than the MVT of integration only requires continuity of the integrand for differentiation implies continuity but not the converse. Because we end up having to assume differentiation anyways when starting with the MVT of integration to prove the MVT of differentiation the derivation becomes circular, so the MVT of differentiation can be seen as the more "fundamental result" that we start with:
$$\text{MVT of differentation}\implies \text{first part of fundamental theorem of calculus}\implies \text{MVT of integration}$$

# Definitions
- ***Primitive Functions.*** Any function $F(x)$ such that $F'(x)=f(x)$ is said to be a "primitive function of $f(x)$" or simply "a primitive of $f(x)$". Essentially the primitive of a function is the antiderivative of a function.
# Theorems
- ***First part of the fundamental theorem of calculus (p.185).*** The indefinite integral $\phi(x)$ of a continuous function $f(x)$ always possesses a derivative $\phi'(x)$ and is related to the integrand as follows: $$\text{if }\phi(x)=\int_{a}^xf(u)du \text{\quad then \quad}\phi'(x)=f(x)$$
- Every indefinite integral $\phi(x)$ of the function $f(x)$ is a primitive of $f(x)$ 
- ***Second part of the fundamental theorem of calculus (p, 187).*** The difference of two primitive functions is always a constant. Thus from one primitive function, we can obtain all the others by a suitable choice of constant.
- ***Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.*** This simply combines the above two parts: Every primitive function $F(x)$ of a given function $f(x)$ continuous on an interval can be represented in the form: $$F(x)=c+\phi(x)=c+\int_{a}^xf(u)du$$
