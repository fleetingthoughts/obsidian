 The discussion of scientific realism and PGS's formulation of it in science telling us about the independent reality lends itself to the question of why and causation. The idea of explanation and its role arises from several questions about science:
- How does science give us an understanding of phenomena.
- What is the point of science
- Do we need to understand "why" things happen.
The topic of explanation can be seen as the ultimate goal of science that answers the question of "what science does for us". Questions about explanation, to me, question what the end product of science is and is in a sense the end goal of questions in other fields such as confirmation. Confirmation is to justify a hypothesis and a hypothesis has to be a good explanation.

In this chapter, PGS provides an overview of the literature on the topic of explanation and what role science should have in explaining things and what that explanation should constitute before proceeding with his own views. The chapter is outlined as follows:
- The traditional framing and categorization of the questions involving explanation
- The covering law model (D-N) of the logical positivists that will be used as a starting point for other views on explanation. The reason for the downfall of the cover model in the problem of asymmetry.
- The refinement of the cover model in Unification
- PGS's position on the need for contextualism in explanations
- Van Fraasen's pragmatic views on explanation contrasted with PGS.

To start with the traditional framing of the questions of explanation, the traditional empiricists have recognized the questions listed in the beginning of note had a relationship with confirmation, but they had split the question of confirming from the question of explanation. The two topics were separated to avoid metaphysical "pointless" questions about what constitutes "cause". The idea here is that generalizations and scientific theories would be established (their confirmation being a separate issue as stated before), but the kind of explanation they should offer is a whole other field. PGS believes this to be a mistake and makes his position clear that the two questions should be considered in his contextual views of explanation to be elaborated on. But to illustrate the relationship between confirmation and explanations he referenced his early categorizations of the types of non-deductive inferences in chapter 3:
- inductive inference: inferencing of the data leading to generalizations
- explanatory inference: inferencing of the data to justify a hypothesis that explains the data
The fact that confirmation has been distinguished between these two different end goals of simply generalizing or "explaining" illustrates the relationship.

# The covering law model
The covering law model developed by Hempel and Oppenheim sought a general non-causal model to explain a result. In this model, phenomena would follow in the language of logic. The covering law model (also called the D-N model) states to explain something is the same as generalizing something and to generalize it is to show that it deductively follows from presupposed natural laws (a covering law).  To explain something is to deductively show that the result of phenomena was expected from a priori laws. In this sense, explaining is equivalent to deductively predicting a result. "Cause" in this sense is a pattern of succession. While there were issues with apriorism (what would be the basic natural laws to start assuming) and issues with probabilistic outcomes, the main issue is the problem of asymmetry in explanation.

The asymmetry problem is the asymmetry is observed when evaluating competing explanations for a phenomena where the covering law model does not offer a criteria for. We can often take the same phenomena and reverse the explanans and the explandum. PGS offers two examples to illustrate
- A flagpole casts a shadow which we can explain the length of the shadow by optics with the sun, but we can also explain the height of the flagpole by the length of the shadow
- A disease can follow from a symptom, but we can also explain the disease by the symptoms
There is a bidirectionality between the explanans and the explandum when we try to frame a phenomena with the covering law. (we can flip them vice versa), but the asymmetry arises when wish to establish which phenomena is the one to be explained and decide between competing explanations. Hempel had foreseen this issue but he simply accepted the bidirectionality of his model and did not attempt to reject it. Indeed it can be hard in science to figure out which is the trigger for the phenomena and which is the one being triggered.

# Replacements for the covering law model
The problem of asymmetry in the covering law model and the downfall of the attempt to develop a non-causal model for explanation. The asymmetry problems can be solved if we ascribe the criteria of "cause" to the explanans to fix the direction of our explanation. An alternative view has been unificationism, which further refines the covering law model by adding the criteria of simplicity or "most general" to the covering law to make it a suitable explanans. Philip Kitcher who developed the idea noted that the positivists had unofficially endorsed this unificationism view but did not make it official. The ideas were always there in their publications. The idea is that we fix the bidirectionality issue by setting the explanans as the most general one that can account for and explain the most phenomena. For example, PGS notes:
- Darwin's theory of evolution is the explanans because it accounts for more observations
- Newton's law of gravitation is the explanans as opposed to Kepler's laws because Newton's laws accounts for more phenomena
Unificationism purports that science always aims to reduce the number of generalizations or axioms we have to make and so the criteria for a good explanation is one that makes the least assumptions. The unificationism model adds the criteria of the simplest explanation (i.e. makes the fewest number of assumptions of natural laws) to fix the bidirectionality issue in asymmetry by ascribing the simplest explanation as the explanans.

Between these two competing approaches to explanation in causal theory and unificationism, PGS does not endorse either and comments on the mistaken tendency for individuals to ascribe to one view or the other like a false dichotomy. While there are people who endorse a "pluralist" view of embracing both views simultaneously, PGS goes further in that a good explanation should depend on the context of the field that the explanation is in. His view, which he refers to as contextualism, borrows from Kuhn's ideas on the incommensurability of standards in paradigms. Kuhn recognized that standards for a sufficient scientific hypothesis changes with paradigm. Consider the [Boyle and Hobbes debate from chapter 8](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%208%20The%20Challenge%20from%20Sociology%20of%20Science.md) and Newton's purely mathematical description of gravity that is devoid of any "causal" explanation. The modification of an explanation to suit the current standards and requirements of science aligns with PGS's naturalist views: the question of a good explanation should be answered by Science itself. To PGS, it is a mistake to ascribe one general standard to explanation

# One last view on explanation from Van Fraasen
Before concluding, PGS offers one more "pragmatic" view of explanation from Van Fraasen. Like PGS, Van Fraasen does not believe there is one standard for what constitutes a good explanation for all of science. Where he differs from PGS is that he believes that a good explanation for science is one that answers questions outside of science, that is, the explanation has practical universal value and is not just for academic purposes. PGS believes that what constitutes a good explanation can be entirely internal to Science. I can endorse PGS's views when looking at the history of science and maths and observing the previously obscure topics that flourished with recent innovations such as linear algebra. It appears Science can exist for its own sake and does not depend on offering something outside of itself.
