We adopt the verification criteria and admit to a form of empiricism; therefore, we deal with the traditional criticisms against empiricism: the effectiveness of mathematics and their apparent certainties. We assert that math contains no factual content as they are necessarily true tautologies and its usefulness is in giving us a consistent linguistic way to talk in a uncontradictory way about synthetic propositions and the sense of "effectiveness" is psychological:
1) Hume's problem of induction means we can never be logically certain about any synthetic proposition and so it is irrational for us to believe. We solve the problem by flipping it around. It is irrational to assume that it is irrational for us to only believe something when it is logically guaranteed for that is expecting the impossible.
2) Our version of empiricism asserts [no fact can be guaranteed](Language%20Truth%20&%20Logic%20-%201%20The%20Elimination%20of%20Metaphysics.md), so we provide an explanation for the statements that are a certainty, namely in mathematics. There have been two traditional approaches to this:
	1) We deny that they are certainties to begin with, in which case we have to account for the possibility that they are
	2) We say they have no factual content. But we have to explain why they can be useful in the material world.
	If we cannott resolve these issues, then we give way to rationalism, or Kant's a priori regress
3) We do not accept Mill's view of math as an inductive generalization to be adequate and reject it on the grounds that math is not an empirical proposition:
	1) ***Math cannot be confuted the way empirical propositions can.*** In verifying empirical phenomena by mathematics, we can always ascribe any faults to the way math and logic was used as a representation rather than the math itself.
4) Our proposition is that math are tautologies and analytic propositions which we clarify by rejecting Kant's definition of analytic and synthetic propositions:
	- Kant does not provide a clear distinction between analytic and synthetic statements and in his own examples, he applied separate criteria to distinguish them. He applies a psychological criteria to synthetic propositions and logical ones to analytic ones.
5) We value the categorization of propositions into analytic and synthetic propositions by Kant but refine their definitions using the verifiability criteria.
	- We define analytic statements as propositions who's validity depends solely on the symbols contained in the proposition. Analytic statements are not metaphysical nonsense. They are useful for illustrating the way we use symbols. They call attention to linguistic usage.
	- We define synthetic statements as propositions who's validity cannot be verified by the symbols contained in them and must be verified. We make use of analytic propositions to speak in a noncontradictory way about synthetic statements. 
 6) The reason math was thought to be synthetic
	 - Geometry commonly traps people into thinking it is synthetic.
	 - Math wasn't formalized and rigorously defined from axioms until Russel's work formalizes math and logic as tautologies.
7) We refute Poincare's assertion that the principle of induction is synthetic a priori and explains the discovery and effectiveness of mathematics as other areas of math that do not use the principle of induction make discoveries.
8) The sense of effectiveness and discovery of mathematics is psychological and due to the limitations of our reasoning.

# Terms
- ***Rationalism.*** The belief that there are truths in the world independent of experience and can be known through independent unverified thought like mathematics. Rationalism stands in direct conflict with the verification criteria and is considered by Ayer to be nonsensical metaphysics.
- ***Kant's principle of contradiction.*** No predicate can belong to a subject if it contradicts the subject
# Cited examples
- John Stuart Mills' assertion that math and logic and its application in the real world are inductive generalizations and therefore theoretically fallible by the problem of induction. Cauchy and Fourier were born slightly before John so the formalization of math was not rigorous yet.
- Kant's view that all knowledge arises from experience but it cannot come 100% from it.
- Example of $2 \times 5$ cannot be confuted empirically because we can always argue that the situation the numbers are representing are at fault rather than the math itself.
- The use of formal logic with synthetic propositions. [[The Pedagogical and Empirical Problem of Applying Formal Logic to Synthetic Propositions.]]
- Kant's definition of analytic and synthetic propositions:
	- Analytic. A proposition provides predicate B about $A$ that already belonged to $A$ such as "All bodies are extended". The propositions "All bodies are heavy" is not analytic as "heavy" is not presupposed in "bodies". Analytic propositions add nothing but merely break up a proposition into other constituent propositions.
	- Synthetic. A proposition that provides a predicate $B$ that does not belong to $A$. Kant famously asserts that $7 + 5$ is a synthetic proposition as the concept that $7$ and $5$ are not inherent in $12$ 
- Henri Poincare's explanation for the effectiveness of math despite its position as a large tautology. He claims the principle of induction is synthetic a priori
