Adler splits his chapter into a discussion on the classical scientific or scientific texts, and the modern "pop" science literature. I disregard the latter and will focus only on the former. Adler spends the majority of the chapter expounding on the aesthetic behind the logical and deductive nature of these books likely because he recognizes his audience will not be scientifically inclined. These parts I set aside as well. The little bit of actual advice is worth noting

The key to reading a scientific or mathematical book is to recognize the problem they are trying to solve as everything in the book will be in-service to that problem. Science and math does very little wandering, their tools may have applications in other fields, but science or math is not for its own sake (G. H. Hardy would disagree). In support of this, I recognize that a lot of math book stress application to give a better sense of the problems they are trying to answer. With respect to scientific works, it is crucial to recognize the critical experiments that drive the author's conclusions

Adler impresses on the reader that much of the classical texts do not have the same level of specialization as modern texts and were written with the layman in mind as some scientific thinking was required of people at the time. From this, I would extrapolate that it becomes important to view how the great scientists of the past asked the important questions, how they answered it, and finally how they communicated it as opposed to the actual scientific content itself. It goes without saying that many of the ideas are outdated, but their ability to convince people of complex ideas to the unspecialized audience should be studied
