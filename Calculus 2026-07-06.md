## Section 1: Conceptual Foundations

## 1. The Continuum and Density

- Solution: A set of points is dense if between any two distinct points, there exists another point in the set. For the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$, taking the arithmetic mean $c = \frac{a+b}{2}$ always produces another rational number (calculus_a... p. 5). However, $\mathbb{Q}$ contains "gaps" because simple geometric lengths, such as the diagonal of a unit square ($\sqrt{2}$), cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers (calculus_a... pp. 5-6). Rational points can get _arbitrarily close_ to any point on the line, but they fail to form a continuous line because they lack a topological property called completeness.
- Continuity Axiom: The text introduces the Postulate of Nested Intervals (also known as the Cantor-Dedekind Completeness Axiom for nested intervals) (calculus_a... p. 8): _If $I_1, I_2, I_3, \dots$ form a nested sequence of closed intervals with rational endpoints whose lengths tend to zero, there is exactly one point $x$ contained in all $I_n$._ This axiom guarantees that the real number line is a continuous instrument free of physical or logical gaps (calculus_a... p. 8).
- Significance & Names: This property forms the modern logical foundation of the Real Number Continuum ($\mathbb{R}$) (calculus_a... p. 2). It bridges the ancient gap between discrete arithmetic (counting) and continuous geometry (measuring) (calculus_a... pp. 2, 6).

## 2. Pointwise vs. Uniform Continuity

- Pointwise Continuity Definition: A function $f(x)$ is continuous at a point $x_0$ if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x) - f(x_0)\vert{} < \epsilon$ for all $x$ in the domain satisfying $\vert{}x - x_0\vert{} < \delta$ (calculus_a... p. 17).
- Uniform Continuity Definition: A function $f(x)$ is uniformly continuous on an interval if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a $\delta > 0$ such that $\vert{}f(x) - f(x_0)\vert{} < \epsilon$ for _any_ two points $x$ and $x_0$ in the interval satisfying $\vert{}x - x_0\vert{} < \delta$ (calculus_a... p. 25).
- Logical Comparison: In pointwise continuity, $\delta = \delta(\epsilon, x_0)$ is a local property; it depends on both the error tolerance $\epsilon$ and the specific location of $x_0$ (calculus_a... p. 25). In uniform continuity, $\delta = \delta(\epsilon)$ is a global metric; it depends strictly on $\epsilon$ and must work uniformly across the entire interval regardless of location (calculus_a... p. 25).
- Significance & Names: Pointwise vs. Uniform properties was one of the most critical conceptual clarifications of 19th-century mathematics. The theorem stating that _any function continuous on a closed and bounded interval is uniformly continuous_ is known as Heine's Theorem (or the Heine-Cantor Theorem) (calculus_a... p. 26).

## 3. The Neighborhood Definition of a Limit

- Solution: The statement $\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = l$ means that for any assigned positive value $\epsilon$ representing an open interval (neighborhood) $J = (l - \epsilon, l + \epsilon)$ centered at $l$, all points of the sequence $a_n$ fall inside $J$, with the exception of at most a finite number of terms (calculus_a... p. 11, calculus_a... p. 2).
- Significance: This is the Weierstrassian $\epsilon$-$N$ Definition of a Limit (calculus_a... p. 11). It completely stripped calculus of the confusing dynamic notions of motion ("approaching infinity") that plagued early analysis, replacing them with a purely static configuration of subsets (calculus_a... p. 11).

## 4. Distance Magnification in Continuity

- Lipschitz Condition: $\vert{}f(x_2) - f(x_1)\vert{} \leq L\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}$ for all $x_1, x_2$ in the interval, where $L > 0$ is a fixed constant (calculus_a... p. 27).
- Hölder Condition ($\alpha = 1/2$): $\vert{}f(x_2) - f(x_1)\vert{} \leq L\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}^{1/2}$ (or $L\sqrt{\vert{}x_2 - x_1\vert{}}$) (calculus_a... p. 28).
- Geometric Interpretation: A Lipschitz condition acts as a strict ceiling on distance stretching; it states that the mapping $y = f(x)$ magnifies distances on the $x$-axis by a factor of at most $L$ (calculus_a... p. 27). A Hölder condition allows the distance magnification or scaling factor ($\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$) to grow infinitely large as $x_2 \to x_1$ (since $\frac{\sqrt{\Delta x}}{\Delta x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Delta x}} \to \infty$), which means it accommodates graphs that have vertical tangents, such as $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ at $x=0$ (calculus_a... pp. 27-28).
- Significance & Names: Named after Rudolf Lipschitz and Otto Hölder. These properties classify functions into specialized spaces (Lipschitz and Hölder spaces) that are vital for establishing the smoothness and convergence properties of solutions to differential equations.

## Section 2: Theorems and Analytical Behavior

## 5. The Intermediate Value Theorem

- Statement: If $f(x)$ is continuous at every point of an interval containing $a$ and $b$, and $\eta$ is any number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists at least one value $\xi$ between $a$ and $b$ for which $f(\xi) = \eta$ (calculus_a... p. 28).
- Geometric Description: If a continuous function is not monotone, its graph fluctuates up and down (calculus_a... p. 13). Therefore, a horizontal line $y = \eta$ positioned between the starting and ending heights must cross the continuous graph path to get from one side to the other (calculus_a... p. 29). Because the graph is allowed to weave back and forth, the line $y = \eta$ can intersect the path multiple times, creating several distinct solutions for $\xi$ (calculus_a... p. 29).
- Significance & Names: This is the Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT), historically formalized by Bernard Bolzano in 1817. It is foundational for root-finding algorithms (like the Bisection Method) and establishes that continuous mappings cannot bypass spatial thresholds (calculus_a... p. 28).

## 6. Order Preservation Under Limits

- Sequence Counterexample: Let $a_n = \frac{1}{n}$ and $b_n = \frac{1}{2n}$ (calculus_a... p. 4). For every natural number $n$, we have $a_n > b_n$ (e.g., $1 > 0.5$, $0.5 > 0.25$) (calculus_a... p. 4). However, evaluating their limits yields:  
    $$\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{n \to \infty} b_n = 0 \implies a = b = 0 [0.1.74]$$
- Explanation: As terms progress toward infinity, the strict buffer distance between $a_n$ and $b_n$ pinches down to zero (calculus_a... p. 4). Thus, strict inequalities are weaked under limiting operations. We can only guarantee the non-strict condition $a \geq b$ (calculus_a... p. 4).
- Significance: This principle is the operational bedrock of the Squeeze Theorem (or Sandwich Theorem). It reminds us that limits map strict open relations into closed boundary configurations.

## 7. Monotone and Bounded Sequences

- Convergence Principle: A sequence of real numbers that is both monotone and bounded converges to a finite limit (calculus_a... p. 4).
- Bounded from Below Proof: By definition, a monotonically increasing sequence satisfies $a_n \geq a_{n-1}$ for all $n$ (calculus_a... p. 4). Writing out the terms reveals:  
    $$a_1 \leq a_2 \leq a_3 \leq \dots \leq a_n [0.1.74]$$  
    Because every single subsequent term $a_n$ must be greater than or equal to the very first term, $a_1$ serves as an automatic lower bound for the entire infinite sequence ($a_n \geq a_1$ for all $n$) (calculus_a... p. 4). Therefore, an upper bound $M$ is the only remaining condition required to fully bind the sequence within a finite interval (calculus_a... p. 4).
- Significance & Names: This is the Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT) (calculus_a... p. 4). It is an intrinsic existence theorem; it allows mathematicians to prove a limit exists without having to guess its numerical value beforehand (calculus_a... p. 4).

## 8. The Cauchy Convergence Criterion

- Condition: A sequence $a_n$ converges if for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists a natural number $N(\epsilon)$ such that $\vert{}a_n - a_m\vert{} < \epsilon$ whenever $n > N$ and $m > N$ (calculus_a... p. 5).
- Intrinsic Advantage: Standard limit definitions require an _a priori_ benchmark value $l$ to test the expression $\vert{}a_n - l\vert{} < \epsilon$ (calculus_a... p. 11, calculus_a... p. 4). The Cauchy criterion bypasses $l$ entirely by checking if the terms of the sequence are packing close to _each other_ (calculus_a... p. 5).
- Significance & Names: Named after Augustin-Louis Cauchy. The Cauchy criterion defines what it means for a metric space to be complete. It allows analysis to construct and validate completely new irrational numbers (like $e$ or solutions to differential equations) purely out of the asymptotic tracking of rational terms (calculus_a... pp. 4, 7).

## 9. The Irrationality of $\sqrt{2}$

- Derivation: Assume $\sqrt{2} = \frac{p}{q}$ where $\gcd(p, q) = 1$ (calculus_a... p. 5). Squaring both sides yields:  
    $$2 = \frac{p^2}{q^2} \implies p^2 = 2q^2 [0.1.5]$$  
    Because $p^2$ is a multiple of 2, $p^2$ must be an even number, which mathematically implies that $p$ itself must be an even integer (calculus_a... p. 5). We can therefore rewrite $p$ as $2p'$ for some integer $p'$ (calculus_a... p. 5). Substituting this back into the squared equation yields:  
    $$(2p')^2 = 2q^2 \implies 4(p')^2 = 2q^2 \implies q^2 = 2(p')^2 [0.1.5]$$  
    This equation structurally isolates $q^2$ as a multiple of 2, meaning $q^2$ is even, and consequently, $q$ must also be an even integer (calculus_a... pp. 5-6).
- Contradiction: If both $p$ and $q$ are even, they both share a common factor of 2 (calculus_a... p. 6). This directly contradicts our foundational step that $\frac{p}{q}$ was written in lowest terms ($\gcd(p,q)=1$) (calculus_a... pp. 5-6). Thus, the initial assumption is false, proving $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational (calculus_a... p. 6).
- Significance: This is the classical Euclidean Proof of Irrationality via Indirect Contradiction (calculus_a... p. 6). It shattered the ancient Pythagorean worldview that all spatial structures could be explained via whole-number ratios (calculus_a... p. 5).

## 10. The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

- Derivation: We begin with the absolute fact that a sum of squared real terms can never be negative (calculus_a... p. 15):  
    $$0 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n (a_i + t b_i)^2 [0.1.15]$$  
    Expanding each squared binomial under the summation operator yields:  
    $$0 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n (a_i^2 + 2t a_i b_i + t^2 b_i^2) \implies 0 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i^2\right)t^2 + 2\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i\right)t + \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2\right) [0.1.15, 0.1.60]$$  
    This is a standard quadratic polynomial $Ct^2 + 2Bt + A \geq 0$, where:  
    $$A = \sum a_i^2, \quad B = \sum a_i b_i, \quad C = \sum b_i^2 [0.1.15]$$  
    Since this parabola sits entirely on or above the horizontal axis, its minimum value must also be non-negative (calculus_a... p. 16). The vertex occurs at $t = -\frac{B}{C}$ (calculus_a... p. 16). Evaluating the quadratic at this point yields:  
    $$0 \leq C\left(-\frac{B}{C}\right)^2 + 2B\left(-\frac{B}{C}\right) + A \implies 0 \leq \frac{B^2}{C} - \frac{2B^2}{C} + A \implies 0 \leq A - \frac{B^2}{C} [0.1.16]$$  
    Multiplying through by the positive sum $C$ yields:  
    $$0 \leq AC - B^2 \implies B^2 \leq AC [0.1.16]$$  
    Substituting our coefficient definitions back into this structure yields the final Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (calculus_a... p. 15):  
    $$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i\right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i^2\right) [0.1.15]$$

## 11. The Limit of $(\sin x)/x$

- Derivation: Consider a sector of a unit circle ($r=1$) with a radiant angle $x$ (calculus_a... pp. 3-4). Geometry outlines three nested regions (calculus_a... p. 15):
    
    1. An inscribed inner triangle $OAB$ with an area of $\frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{base} \cdot \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}(1)(\sin x) = \frac{1}{2}\sin x$ (calculus_a... p. 15).
    2. The central circle sector $OAB$ with an area of $\text{fractional arc slice}$ $= \frac{1}{2}r^2 x = \frac{1}{2}(1)^2 x = \frac{1}{2}x$ (calculus_a... p. 4, calculus_a... p. 15).
    3. A circumscribed outer triangle $OAC$ with an area of $\frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{base} \cdot \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}(1)(\tan x) = \frac{1}{2}\tan x$ (calculus_a... p. 15).  
        Visual nesting establishes the strict ordering (calculus_a... p. 15):  
        $$\frac{1}{2}\sin x < \frac{1}{2}x < \frac{1}{2}\tan x [0.1.85]$$

        $$\sin x < x < \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} [0.1.85]$$  
        Since $0 < x < \frac{\pi}{\nu}$, $\sin x$ is strictly positive. Dividing all three terms by $\sin x$ preserves inequality direction (calculus_a... p. 15):

## 12. The Irrationality of $e$

- Derivation: The error bound between $e$ and its $m$-th partial sum $s_m$ is bounded by (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$s_m < e \leq s_m + \frac{1}{m \cdot m!} [0.1.79]$$  
    Assume for contradiction that $e = \frac{p}{m}$ for integers $p, m$ with $m \geq 2$ (calculus_a... p. 9). Substituting this yields (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$s_m < \frac{p}{m} \leq s_m + \frac{1}{m \cdot m!} [0.1.79]$$  
    Multiply all three parts of the inequality by the factorial scale $m!$ (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$m! \cdot s_m < p(m-1)! \leq m! \cdot s_m + \frac{1}{m} [0.1.79]$$  
    Let us evaluate the term $m! \cdot s_m$ by expanding $s_m$ (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$m! \cdot s_m = m!\left(1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2!} + \frac{1}{3!} + \dots + \frac{1}{m!}\right) = m! + m! + \frac{m!}{2!} + \dots + 1 [0.1.79]$$  
    Because every denominator $k!$ in the expansion is a factor of $m!$ (since $k \leq m$), every single division resolves cleanly into a whole number (calculus_a... p. 9). Thus, $m! \cdot s_m$ evaluates to a flat integer, which we will call $I$ (calculus_a... p. 9). Substituting $I$ back into the inequality yields (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$I < p(m-1)! \leq I + \frac{1}{m} [0.1.79]$$  
    Since $m \geq 2$, the fraction $\frac{1}{m}$ is strictly less than 1. This means the expression states that the integer value $p(m-1)!$ must sit strictly greater than an integer $I$, yet be less than or equal to $I + 0.5$ (calculus_a... p. 9).
- Contradiction: It is logically impossible for an integer to exist strictly between two consecutive integers ($I$ and $I+1$) (calculus_a... p. 9). Thus, $e$ cannot be rational (calculus_a... p. 9).
- Significance: First proven by Leonhard Euler in 1737 using continued fractions, this specific factorial box argument is Fourier's Elegant Simplification.

        $$\vert{}x + x_0\vert{} = \vert{}(x - x_0) + 2x_0\vert{} [0.1.34]$$

        $$\vert{}(x - x_0) + 2x_0\vert{} \leq \vert{}x - x_0\vert{} + 2\vert{}x_0\vert{} [0.1.34]$$

        $$\vert{}x + x_0\vert{} < 1 + 2\vert{}\vert{}x_0\vert{} [0.1.34]$$

    6. To force this entire evaluation to stay under our error tolerance threshold $\epsilon$, we set:

    2. For all points in the domain where $x \neq \xi$, we can divide out the matching linear terms to resolve the indeterminate form (calculus_a... p. 14):  
        $$f(x) = x^{n-1} + x^{n-2}\xi + x^{n-3}\xi^2 + \dots + \xi^{n-1} [0.1.84]$$
    3. Apply the limit laws for sums (calculus_a... p. 14):  
        $$\lim_{x \to \xi} f(x) = \lim_{x \to \xi}(x^{n-1}) + \lim_{x \to \xi}(x^{n-2}\xi) + \dots + \lim_{x \to \xi}(\xi^{n-1}) [0.1.84]$$
    4. Since every individual term is now a continuous power function, evaluate each limit by direct substitution ($x = \xi$) (calculus_a... p. 14):  
        $$\lim_{x \to \xi} f(x) = \xi^{n-1} + (\xi^{n-2}\xi) + (\xi^{n-3}\xi^2) + \dots + \xi^{n-1} [0.1.84]$$
    5. Simplify each product using exponent addition (calculus_a... p. 14):  
        $$\lim_{x \to \xi} f(x) = \xi^{n-1} + \xi^{n-1} + \xi^{n-1} + \dots + \xi^{n-1} [0.1.84]$$
    6. Counting the terms reveals exactly $n$ identical components (calculus_a... p. 14). Summing them yields (calculus_a... p. 14):  
        $$\lim_{x \to \xi} \frac{x^n - \xi^n}{x - \xi} = n\xi^{n-1} [0.1.83, 0.1.84]$$
    
- Context: This is the fundamental algebraic proof for the Power Rule in calculus, evaluated before the shortcut rules of differentiation are defined.

## 15. Geometric Series Convergence and Tail Bounding

- Decimal Fraction Conversion:  
    Write out the repeating decimal as an infinite sum of components (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$x = \frac{34}{10^2} + \frac{34}{10^4} + \frac{34}{10^6} + \dots [0.1.68]$$  
    Factor out the leading structural term $\frac{34}{10^2}$ (calculus_a... p. 9):  
    $$x = \frac{34}{10^2}\left(1 + \frac{1}{10^2} + \frac{1}{10^4} + \dots\right) [0.1.68]$$  
    This isolates a geometric series with a common ratio of $q = \frac{1}{10^2} = \frac{1}{100}$ (calculus_a... pp. 8-9). Since $\vert{}q\vert{} < 1$, apply the convergence sum formula $\frac{1}{1-q}$ (calculus_a... pp. 8-9):  
    $$x = \frac{34}{100} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{100}} = \frac{34}{100} \cdot \frac{1}{\frac{99}{100}} = \frac{34}{100} \cdot \frac{100}{99} = \frac{34}{99} [0.1.68]$$
- Tail Bounding for $e$:
    
    1. The exact remaining error left over when truncating the $e$ series at index $m$ is (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$e - s_m = \frac{1}{(m+1)!} + \frac{1}{(m+2)!} + \frac{1}{(m+3)!} + \dots [0.1.79]$$
    2. Factor out the leading term $\frac{1}{(m+1)!}$ from the error expression (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$e - s_m = \frac{1}{(m+1)!}\left(1 + \frac{1}{m+2} + \frac{1}{(m+2)(m+3)} + \dots\right) [0.1.79]$$
    3. We establish a upper bound by replacing every subsequent denominator factor ($m+3, m+4,$ etc.) with the smaller value $(m+1)$ (calculus_a... p. 9). This makes the fractions larger, creating the inequality (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$e - s_m < \frac{1}{(m+1)!}\left(1 + \frac{1}{m+1} + \frac{1}{(m+1)^2} + \dots\right) [0.1.79]$$
    4. The bracketed expression is now a standard geometric series with a ratio of $q = \frac{1}{m+1}$ (calculus_a... p. 9). Summing it via $\frac{1}{1-q}$ yields (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$\text{Sum} = \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{m+1}} = \frac{1}{\frac{m}{m+1}} = \frac{m+1}{m} [0.1.79]$$
    5. Combine this back into our factored expression (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$e - s_m < \frac{1}{(m+1)!} \cdot \frac{m+1}{m} [0.1.79]$$
    6. Expand the factorial term $(m+1)! = (m+1) \cdot m!$ to cancel terms (calculus_a... p. 9):  
        $$e - s_m < \frac{1}{(m+1) \cdot m!} \cdot \frac{m+1}{m} = \frac{1}{m \cdot m!} [0.1.79]$$  
        This completes the proof, verifying that our approximation error is bounded by $\frac{1}{m \cdot m!}$ (calculus_a... p. 9).
