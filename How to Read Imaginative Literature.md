While Adler admits many of his rules are applicable only to non-fictional expository works and they have been framed as such, the analogs can be easily be generated for imaginative fictional works with slight modifications. Adler first provides the what you must not do for imaginative literature, then he provides modifications of the rules, and finally concludes with specific problems and tips associated with each kind of fictional work

Adler, by way of negation, illustrates the difference between actively reading imaginative literature and non-fiction:
- Approach the work with an open mind and let yourself be washed over by the experience the author is trying to tell. Do not resist the effect the work tries to have on you
- Do not look for terms, propositions, or arguments (Rules 5-7 must be modified)
- Do not judge a fictional work by its "truthfulness". They are intended to be hypotheticals.
The rules for the structural outlining, the interpretation (rules 5-8) and the criticism (rules 9-12) are then modified for imaginative works
- Rule 1: determine what class of fiction
- Rule 2: grasp the unity of the work by understanding its whole literal plot. Be able to provide a plot summary in your own words
- Rule 3: grasp the main events, conflicts, and the climaxes and break it down to its simplest units to the character level
For the interpretive rules:
- Rule 5: Be sympathetic to the characters 
- Rule 6: Live through the events as if they were your own
- Rule 7: understand the flow of the narrative and see the motivations and underlying themes that drive the story literally and thematically

The main modification is what constitutes an "idea" for literature is different from nonfiction. Active reading is to understand these ideas and make it your own. For works of nonfiction, they are propositions and arguments. For fiction they are "<u>experiences</u>". The narrative power of these experiences have more persuasive sway then even the strongest arguments and this is the "idea" we are trying to capture in a fictional work. To effectively communicate with a fictional work is to allow ourselves to be overcome with the experience the work is trying to offer.

Adler then offers advices and tips for each kind of fictional work:
- Stories: try to read in one sitting to get the best sense of the experience. Do not try to frame every action through your own experience and try to instead come to terms and sympathize with the character's motivations. This is what it means to surrender yourself to the work's experience
- Plays: Try to imagine how you would direct the play
- Tragedies: Be conscious of the theme of time and lack of it. 
- Lyric Poetry: Definitely read these in one sitting and read it out loud to get a sense of the rhythm. 
Overall do not try to depend on secondary sources or the author's biography. I believe this may taint your experience of the work.
