PGS discusses the input of feminist views on Science and later takes an overall view of the shape of the debate with the ongoing trend to incorporate the humanities. The outline is as follows:
- Feminist ideas on the general values and specific terms that underlie all of science
- The value of feminist ideals in the specific fields with primatology as an example
- Feminist views on the role of epistemology in Science
- The incorporation of nontraditional scientific fields to the interpretation of science leading to the field of Science Studies with the debate contextualized by a historic episode known as the Sokal Hoax.

To illustrate the male-dominant view of fundamental values in science, PGS cites ideas from Genevieve Lloyd's ***The Man of Reason.*** The book looks into the history of science using Francis Bacon as an example to illustrate the association of man with reason and female with nature with Science being a domination of nature with reason. A direct quote from Francis Bacon himself illustrates this: "Nature betrays her secrets more fully when in the grip and uner the pressure of art than when in enjoyment of her natural liberty." A more modern example used by PGS is our usage of terms like "yield" in phrases like a "phenomenon will yield to a particular method of analysis (p.139)" which, in his opinion has a sexual male dominating connotation. But PGS notes that while there is no concrete link whether any of these male-dominating metaphors have any real impact on scientific work itself.

A more concrete example of the value of feminist ideals is in the field of primatology. The field is noted for having a higher portion of female scientists that have led to observations and models of the female primates role in reproductive success. Conventional theories had observed more variation in behaviors leading to male reproductive success than female reproductive success with the underlying principle being that '"sperm is cheap" and so it is easier to lend itself to variations. Developments in the female-dominated field of primatology have overturned these theories by observing more intricate behavior from the females and the manipulation done leading to higher reproductive success. This observation of the increased role of the females contribution to reproductive success is largely attributed to a more sympathetic view held by the female scientists.

When discussing the role of feminism in the philosophy of science itself and the normative role that science should play, PGS borrows the categorization used by Sandra Harding to distinguish the general options and views:
- Spontaneous feminist empiricism is the least radical and controversial field that aims to criticize biases and other problems without challenging traditional methods in science
- Philosophical feminist empiricism aims to improve and revise traditional ideals in science while staying true to traditional empiricism. This view is distinguished by the call for reforms.
- Radical feminist epistemology is the most controversial and is further categorized into two camps
	- feminism post modernism which holds the relativist view that there is no objective truth and that the truth is seen fundamentally differently by different ethnicities, genders, and social classes
	- standpoint epistemology in contrast is not relativistic, but instead proposes that different ethnicities and genders simply have different levels of access to different facts. 
The debates are mostly centered between the philosophical feminist empiricism and standpoint epistemology which are both non-relativistic positions. Longino from the feminist empiricism camp holds that if different groups of people have different levels of access to different facts, then the so called "privileged" group will have their own useful facts that they are privy too. Standpoint epistemology then just treats epistemology as a community-level exercise maximize the efficiency of science by increasing the number of view points available. People from different fields are seen as a sort of resource that contributes to the "marketplace of ideas". This echoes the sentiments of Feyerabend. PGS offers further criticism noting that a concrete link hasn't been made to show that the experience and viewpoints of women are systematically different enough from other groups of people to have a material effect on scientific disputes.

PGS concludes by commenting on the overall theme of the past two chapters since Kuhn where science has now borrowed ideas from non-traditional scientific fields to interpret its functioning. The trend has led to a resulting field referred to as "Science Studies" which aims to incorporate as many different fields as possible to explain how Science operates. As a broad example without going into details, the incorporation has lead to ideas such as those from literary theory which offers views on language at a symbolic level as opposed to an abstract logical level. The result has lead to polarizing results into two camps: one that has welcomed the input and another camp that has noticed that the non-empirical standards of the humanities has corroded the original rigor of science for clear argumentation. The former has been referred to as left-leaning radical views of science with the latter being referred to as conservative views of science. The debate between the two camps culminated in a historical episode called the Sokal Hoax which the perpetrator admits was an effort to expose the lack of rigor in the so-called "left". In this moment, Alan Sokal published a nonsense jargon-laden article called ***Transgressing the Boundaries: Towards a Transformative Hermeneutics of Quantum Gravity*** in the left leaning ***Social Text*** publisher to see if the publisher would value the actual contents and argumentation of the article or if it contained enough buzzwords. The episode was declared as a success by the conservative scientists who viewed this as exposing the lack of rigor by the left-leaning fields while others felt the episode had burned bridges with other academics that Science would've benefitted from.

To PGS, while he believes that it is necessary to have less tolerance for jargon-laden obscure writing, he does not see value in obsessing over how Science Studies is conducted. One disturbing trend he does comment on is the tendency for Science studies to separate the actual explanatory power and problem-solving capabilities from the historical role of science. The importance of a particular scientific theory would be explained by social interactions or other concepts but there would be resulting gap in explaining how the theory relates to the structure of the world itself and its ability to explain phenomena. My guess is that a lot of the ideas in Science Studies comes from non-scientists who do not actually understand the actual scientific workings of the theory itself and so they skirt around it when explaining the popularity of said theory. The next few chapters by PGS will try to address this gap.
