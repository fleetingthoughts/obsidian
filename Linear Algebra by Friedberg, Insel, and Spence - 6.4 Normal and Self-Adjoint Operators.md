The diagonalizability of a matrix has important computational and conceptual results. In [5.2 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors](Linear%20Algebra%20by%20Friedberg,%20Insel,%20and%20Spence%20-%205.2%20Eigenvalues%20and%20Eigenvectors.md), the diagonalizability of a linear transformation is a sufficient and necessary condition for the domain vector space to have a basis of eigenvectors of the linear transformation. We examine the conditions for the domain vector space to have an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of a linear transformation. We show that only normal linear transformations have orthonormal eigenvectors that span the finite-dimensional complex inner product space, and furthermore the eigenvectors of a Hermitian transformation can span both the finite-dimensional real and complex inner product space. Therefore the corresponding inner product space has a basis consisting of its orthonormal eigenvectors. The steps taken to show this are as follows:

# Summary of concepts:
1)  ***Notable properties of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of a normal matrix.***
	1) The eigenvalues can be complex or real
	2) Possesses orthonormal eigenvectors that span the domain complex inner product space.
		- This means the matrix representation with respect to an orthonormal ordered basis is a diagonal matrix.
2) ***Notable properties of the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of a Hermitian matrix.***
	1) Some books only call it Hermitian if it is defined over the complex inner product space while self-adjoint is the catch-all term for both the real and complex inner product space.
	2) Are a subset of normal matrices so we instantly inherit the same properties
	3) The eigenvalues are real
	4) Possesses orthonormal eigenvectors that span the domain inner product space.

	- Test whether $A^*=A$ to determine whether it is Hermitian first as normal matrices are a subset of Hermitian matrices, then test for normality.
	- The matrix representation with respect to an orthonormal basis is diagonal

# Definitions

***Definition (normal transformation and normal matrix).*** Let $V$ be an inner product space and $T$ be a linear operator. We say $T$ is normal if $TT^*=T^*T$. Similarly, a matrix $A$ (i.e. the linear transformation $L_A$) is normal if $A^*A=AA^*$

***Definition (Hermitian matrix or self-adjoint matrix).*** A matrix $A$ is self-adjoint (or Hermitian) if $A=A^*$.
