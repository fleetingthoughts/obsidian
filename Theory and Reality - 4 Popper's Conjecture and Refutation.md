PGS begins with an admiration for the amount of influence that Popper had on the scientific community due to the simplicity of his ideas, but he notes that in the philosophical community, he has come under tough scrutiny that, in his opinion, Popper cannot escape. The main points of this section are as follows:

 - Popper's falsification criteria and the deductive basis on modus tollens
 - The myth of confirmation
 - The primary motive behind Popper's criteria: solving the problem of demarcation.
 - Popper's normative views on Science to be as bold as possible in-line with his falsification criteria
 - Popper's views on Scientific change as a linear progression of knowledge by repeated refutations
 - Objections to Popper's views on the criteria for Science and confirmation and Popper's response on the following:
	 - Refutation of theories with probabilistic outcomes
	 - Ambiguity of falsification by the Quine-Duhem Thesis resulting in the reduction of Science into subjective decisions blurring the demarcation of Science
	 - Rebuttal to the confirmation myth: Inability to evaluate competing hypothesis that have not been falsified

# 4.2 Popper's Theory of Science

Much of Karl Popper's ideas are in his book ***Logic of Scientific Discovery*** and ***Conjectures and its Refutations***. Popper's primary aim is to understand science and to distinguish it from pseudo-science. Popper wanted a clear demarcation of scientific investigation and his criteria was ***falsificationism:*** "a hypothesis is scientific if and only if it has the potential to be refuted by scientific observation (p. 58)". Furthermore, Popper believed confirmation is impossible. It is worth noting here that a common misconception is that Popper believed it was possible for evidence to support a theory if it resisted refutation, Popper was explicit in that Science did not need confirmation. 

Popper's normative description of testing and observation is therefore to falsify a hypothesis rather than to support it. In line with our understanding of "good Science", we should strive for hypothesis to be as general as possible to apply to a wide variety of cases because it becomes more likely to be able to find an observation that falsifies it. And so a hypothesis that only says something about a smaller # of cases compared to another hypothesis is worse in Popper's eyes. PGS does provide a rebuttal in that Popper's view go against our intuitive understanding of Science as the search for a truth and the best possible explanations, and so if there is such a thing, Popper's views will not permit it. We are in search of a "Holy Grail of truth and will never find it (p. 61)". More on this in the criticism against the Popper's view of confirmation

It was not explicitly mentioned in the book, but it should be noted that appeal of Popper's idea is not just the simplicity in the singular criteria of refutability, but also that this criteria was grounded in deductive logic. The confirmation of a hypothesis by observation is not permitted in deductive logic as that would be affirming the consequent, but denying the antecedent (modus tollens) is deductively valid.
# 4.3 Popper on Scientific Change
Popper describes scientific change as an endless iteration between two steps:
1) Conjecture: the proposal of a hypothesis
2) Attempted refutation
In this, Popper offers a normative view that in order to encourage refutation, ad hocs to save a theory should be discouraged, otherwise we lose the significance of refuting an idea and the demarcation between Science and pseudoscience if we endlessly try to save our own ideas in the face of objection. In these two stages of Science, Popper therefore has the following ["recipe"](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%201%20Introduction.md) encouraged at the individual level:
- Ability to come up with risky ideas so that they lend themselves to refutation
- Willingness to subject the ideas to rigorous testing
To Popper, these two attributes are the same at the individual and the organizational level. There is very little distinction between the two and Popper sees very little reason in trying to differentiate the role the community may play vs the individual. The distinction between the individual and the organization is challenged later in the sociological views of science.
# 4.4 Objections to Popper on Falsificationism
The crux of Popper's thesis is his falsifiability criteria of science and the motivation behind is to solve the demarcation problem between Science and pseudoscience. The criteria is strictly binary in the sense that there is no degree of falsifiability as that would fall dangerously close to confirmation and the idea of "support" but in the rejection sense. 

An objection to this is the [Quine-Duhem Thesis](Quine-Duhem%20Thesis%20and%20how%20its%20distinguished%20from%20the%20term%20theory-ladenness%20of%20observation.md), which renders it difficulty to refute a single hypothesis in isolation as the blame can always be laid on an auxiliary hypothesis. Popper was aware of this objection, but attempts to rebut this by appealing to his standard of good scientific practice in the name of refutability and should avoid shifting the blame when contradictory evidence is observed. In PGS's view, Popper has now softened his position from a clear binary and objective criteria for Science that was grounded in deductive logic to describing characteristics of scientific behavior. Popper's rebuttal is seen as a reduction of science to subjective human decisions on their quest to falsify a hypothesis. 

Our inability to unambiguously falsify a hypothesis also muddies the premise for Popper's falsification criteria: the demarcation of science. Popper recognized that the falsification criteria is not always clear cut and that a decision had to be made whether to refute a hypothesis when faced with the evidence. To Popper, what distinguished Science from the pseudoscience was that Science posits statements that are vulnerable to these kind of decisions (p. 66). But in rebuttal to this, Popper offers no clear criteria for how these decisions can be handled and it is ultimately a subjective decision. This offers a spectrum in how decisions like these are handled and muddies the demarcation of Science, but it does introduce the idea that even the fields considered pseudoscience by Popper can be ***handled scientifically***.

The final problem are scientific theories with probabilistic outcomes that encapsulate even the most improbable outcomes. To Popper, theories of this kind are unscientific, but to recognize the importance of probability to what was considered good science, Popper amended his views and stated good Science must define the criteria for the refutability of probabilistic outcomes and how "improbable is too improbable" so that observation of this most improbable outcome should lead to refutation of the hypothesis. Similar to the difficulties to unambiguously falsify a hypothesis, PGS sees this as Popper softening his position and reducing the logical demarcation for science to human decision on what is considered "too improbable"
# 4.5 Objections to Popper on Confirmation
The biggest misunderstanding of Popper's views is the claim that Popper believed in confirmation when he, in fact, denied it in any form and claimed Science did not need it. PGS highlights a normative issue with discarding confirmation arises when decisions have to be made between competing scientific theories. Popperian science can never find the best theory but only the least falsifiable one: the search for the "Holy grail of truth" is an impossibility. The illustrative example PGS uses is in the application of theories to Engineering when a suitable theory is chosen to build a bridge. In light of two competing theories that have yet to be falsified, the Popperian scientist would view both of these as equal theories, but a look in history and engineering best practices would show that the additional criteria of "the scientific theory that has survived the most amount of observations is the better one and is yet to be falsified". In other words, this would be the theory that was received the most confirmational support.

Popper has attempted to amend this problem by introducing the idea of corroboration. PGS attempts to explain what this is, but as he admits, it sounds like he "gave another name to confirmation (p. 68)". Corroboration was defined as a "back-ward looking attribute" and is a criteria that could be used to assist with our selection. Corroboration is a view of the historical past that says nothing of the future, while confirmation views the past to say something about the future. Admittedly I will have to read Popper's works to understand what is going on here.
# 4.6 Further Comments on the Demarcation Problem
PGS admits that he's been harsh on Popper's views. The tone of his language suggests that is frustrated by the idolization of his ideas by scientific communities. But PGS views the failure of falsificationism as the inescapability of the role of confirmation in Science. Popper's ideas were an attempt at solving confirmation by removing it entirely. PGS concedes that there are redeeming qualities to Popper's views specifically his normative views on the risk-taking qualities of Science. PGS does not see a clear demarcation of Science as Popper attempted, but there must instead be a spectrum in how ideas can be scientifically handled somehow by this risk-taking quality (p.71). In other words, there will always be scientific and non-scientific versions of any idea.
