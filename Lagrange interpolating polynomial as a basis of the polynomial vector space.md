The Lagrange interpolating formula is named after Joseph-Luis Lagrange who published it in 1795, but methods for it were mentioned as early as by Euler and Edward Waring more than a decade prior.

The Lagrange interpolating formula resulting from the Lagrange polynomial can be viewed as a way to generate the basis of the n-th degree polynomial vector space. The Lagrange interpolating polynomial is generated from the Lagrange basis $f_i(x)$: $$f_{i}(x) = \frac{(x-c_1)(x-c_2)...(x-c_{i-1})(x-c_{i+1})...(x-c_n))}{(c_i-c_1)(c_i-c_2)...(c_i-c_{i-1})(c_i-c_{i+1})\dots(c_i-c_n)} = \prod_{\substack{k=1 \\ k\neq j}}^n \frac{x-c_{k}}{c_{i}-c_{k}}$$
Colloquially, the i-th Lagrange basis is a polynomial expressed in terms of its $n-1$ roots (i.e. the $c_k$), and we skip the $(x-c_i)$ in the numerator and obviously have to skip $(c_i-c_i)=0$ in the denominator. As the addition and scalar multiplication operations are defined for nth degree polynomials such that [they form a vector space](Linear%20Algebra%20by%20Friedberg,%20Insel,%20and%20Spence%20-%201.2%20Vector%20Spaces.md), $P_n$, we can show by the [Steinitz exchange lemma](Proof%20of%20the%20Steinitz%20exchange%20lemma.md), that if they are $n$ independent vectors, then they span the $P_n$ vector space. To do so, we prove the linear independence of each $f_i(x)$. By definition of linear independence, we must show the following formula only has the trivial solution: $$\text{1)\quad}\sum_{i=0}^na_if_i(x)=0 \text{\quad for all x}\in \mathbb{R}$$
The Lagrange basis is conveniently formulated with the roots to show us that each polynomial only equals 0 when $x=c_i$ and so this we can use all $n$ roots to produce a system of $n$ linear equations that solves for the $a_i$ coefficients. Notably, we'll see that for each $x=c_i$: $$\text{2) \quad}\sum_{i=0}^na_if_i(c_j)=a_j \text{\quad for all }x= c_j$$
And so the only way to produce the 0 vector is if each $a_i =0$ therefore only the trivial solution exists and so the Lagrange basis produces $n$ independent set of vectors and they necessarily span the nth degree polynomial vector space by the Steinitz exchange lemma. 

The Lagrange basis is useful because it formulates the basis in a way that lets us "plug and chug" $(x_i, g(x_{i}))$ vectors and determine the lowest degree polynomial that goes through each of those points.  Restated in terms of linear algebra, given a set if $(x_i, g(x_{i}))$ vectors in the function space that is a superset of the polynomial vector space, we are trying to determine what $g(x)$ is as a polynomial. In other words we are trying to find a basis that spans $(x_i,g(x_i))$, or mathematically: $$g(x)=\sum_{i=1}^na_if_i(x)  \text{\quad where } f_i(x) \text{ is some basis}$$
The Lagrange basis is such a basis and the beauty in its formulation is that it gives us an easy way to determine what each of the $a_i$ has to be by simply setting each $c_k$ to each $x$ so that we have for the coefficients $a_i = g(x_i)$. Because the Lagrange basis is a basis, it is the smallest set of vectors that spans the polynomial space so we necessarily have the polynomial of the lowest degree. Another way of seeing why it has to be the polynomial of the lowest degree is to see that in trying to determine the analytical form of $g(x)$ we also have to ask which $P_n$ vector space the set of $(x_i, g(x_i))$ points belong to and then the the Lagrange basis gives us a basis that must span this set
