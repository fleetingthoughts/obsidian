PGS provides a view of the social structure of science and how it relates to the interactions and interests at the individual level all from a naturalist approach to philosophy. Specifically this approach takes into account the way Science responds to the natural world and the repercussions on Science as a whole. Naturalism looks to use the results from scientific inquiry to answer philosophical questions itself and produce a more firm connection the responsiveness of science to the natural world and its resulting social structure. To this end, the following ideas are discussed:
- A nuanced form of recognition as the primary incentive for Science at the individual level. Specifically how this form of recognition requires a responsiveness to the natural world.
- The coordination of Science at the community macro level is then explained by individual incentive for recognition
- PGS offers criticism of the views so far presented
- PGS further develops a form of empiricism from the traditional individualistic practice that takes into account Science at the organizational level as well.

The ideas of David Hull from his work ***Science as a Process*** outlines the incentives driving Science at the individual level that takes into account the way Science responds to the natural world. To David Hull, the primary incentive for a scientist is similar to that developed by Robert Merton: <u>recognition</u>. Where David Hull distinguishes himself is the way this recognition manifests. While Robert Merton focuses on "recognition by being first", David Hull emphasizes "recognition by your work being used."

- science characterized by balance of self interest and goals of science as a whole
- Like hull david stresses recognition but stresses use rather than first
- PGS criticism that science selects people who like those kind of rewards

- How should scientific labor be divided and what forces can incentivize an ideal division
