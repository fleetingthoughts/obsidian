PGS provides a view of the social structure of science and how it relates to the interactions and interests at the individual level all from a naturalist approach to philosophy. Specifically this approach takes into account the way Science responds to the natural world and the repercussions on Science as a whole. Naturalism looks to use the results from scientific inquiry to answer philosophical questions itself and produce a more firm connection the responsiveness of science to the natural world and its resulting social structure. To this end, the following ideas are discussed:
- A nuanced form of recognition as the primary incentive for Science at the individual level. Specifically how this form of recognition requires a responsiveness to the natural world.
- The coordination of Science at the community macro level is then explained by individual incentive for recognition
- PGS offers criticism of the views so far presented
- PGS further develops a form of empiricism from the traditional individualistic practice that takes into account Science at the organizational level as well.

The ideas of David Hull from his work ***Science as a Process*** outlines the incentives driving Science at the individual level that takes into account the way Science responds to the natural world. To David Hull, the primary incentive for a scientist is similar to that developed by Robert Merton: <u>recognition</u>. Where David Hull distinguishes himself is the way this recognition manifests. While Robert Merton focuses on "recognition by being first", David Hull emphasizes "recognition by your work being used." In science there is often a need for the imaginative portion that conjectures and the critical portion that refutes, Hull aims to give an explanation of this mechanism by citing the desire for recognition naturally motivates the creative portion, but the people who depend on this work will require it to be accurate producing the critical portion that aims to test. As such, scientists who conjecture can typically leave it to others to test their hypothesis. The need to ensure the work is as accurate as possible explains the desire for a hypothesis to be replicable which links the scientists' response and measure of the natural world to their personal incentive for recognition. 

What therefore characterizes Science is this special interest for recognition beyond other base needs and Science is a unique community that somehow organizes this interest (to be discussed in next paragraph). As a historical example to illustrate his point, David Hull cites and attributes the rapid development of science from the 17th century to the Royal Society of London that created a structure for crediting and recognition. The practices of the Royal Society continue to characterize modern science. David Hull argues that these motivations for recognition are fundamental to humans but here PGS argues that this feature may be common in Science by a survivorship bias: the people who do not care for this unique kind of recognition by use will not continue to study Science.

The personal desire for recognition by use is further developed to explain Science at a community-wide level and Philip Kitcher further develops it by providing normative rules for how Science should be conducted that aligns with one's personal interests. Kitcher's views are contrasted with Laudan and Lakatos who also aimed to develop normative rules to choose between competing theories, but while Laudan and Lakatos provided a criteria for the individuals, PGS notes that there is a gap in their model to explain what is best for Science as a whole. <u>The question to answer is: "what is the best distribution of scientific labor between competing theories?"</u>. Kitcher specifically used complicated mathematical models to explain diminishing returns, but without getting into the nuance, the essence of his solution was that the best division of scientific labor is one that hedges our bet and does not allocate all our eggs into the one theory that seems to be the most successful one at the moment. To discourage scientists from joining the most successful program, Kitcher proposes a reward model that equally distributes the recognition to everyone in the program. If a program contains too many people, the level of recognition is minimal disincentivizing people from joining the better program. Michael Strevens further develops Kitcher's ideas by instead distributing the rewards proportional to their level of contribution. Strevens believes Kitcher's model encourages a free-riding of scientists in the case that their joining offers little to no difference in the outcome of a program's success. The scientists that join late into a program will be free riding as they have less of a risk of wasting their time and effort compared to the scientists who developed the program from the beginning.

PGS summarizes these ideas by categorizing them as propositions that advocate for [Science as a strategy at the community level](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%201%20Introduction.md). In this way, the goals of Science as a community is aligned with the individual empiricists goal to investigate and respond to the natural world. PGS views a suitable structure as amplifying the individual's effort if we can encourage a mutually beneficial relationship between the individual and the community. PGS criticized the propositions of the sociologists and ideas from Science Studies which seeks to explain Science as a result of factors outside of the way they respond to the natural world. Empiricism is our connection between Science and the real world and as such, there needs to be a harmonious relationship between the individual and the community to maintain that connection. To create such a relationship, there are still questions left unanswered. While Hull and Kitcher offered their views on the primary incentives that <u>currently drives Science</u>, is it necessarily the best one? While their views reflect the community since the 17th century, PGS views Science as an iterative procedure that may result in an entirely different reward system that may threaten its relationship with empiricism. PGS asks if the recognition reward system is necessarily the best one as it encourages a competitive culture. PGS lends credence to feminist views that would comment on the development of the competitive culture of science arose out of its male dominated society and isn't necessarily the best environment that encourages a marketplace of ideas. The competitive culture of Science can be seen as a consequence of males, not necessarily what's best for Science. Hull himself comments on the potential cracks in the harmonious relationship between individual and the community through recognition by citing the competition between Skinner and Tolman. In PGS's eyes, psychology would've benefitted more greatly from Tolman's ideas, but it was Skinner's competitive nature that shaped psychology into what it is.

PGS will continue to address the gap in the descriptive and normative explanations for how Science currently and should operate, and link it with the way scientists respond to the natural world (i.e. empiricism). From this, we may develop an understanding for how to best encourage Science as a strategy to investigate the natural world.
