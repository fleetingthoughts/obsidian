The book now departs from a chronological listing of scientific theories and goes over the current modern fields in the philosophy of science starting with Naturalism. Chapter 10 provides a brief overview on what naturalism is and provides an example of it in practice to answer some of our most pressing questions in philosophy. The outline of the chapter is as follows:
1) What naturalism generally is and specifically what Naturalism is to PGS who is an advocate.
2) A further illustration of naturalism by outlining the basic questions it tries to answer with tentative solutions: what is the role of philosophy? What is the relationship between our common-sense understanding and scientific description of the world? What is the relationship between scientific fields.
3) PGS hones in on the 2nd question in 2) addressing the theory-ladenness of observation with naturalism. By answering some of the challenges in science with science itself.

As with many modern fields, the usage of and definition of Naturalism does not have a concrete definition, but it is illustrative to show what it stands against. Foundationalism seeks to establish a basic set of universal principles that can be applied to all of science and until such a basis is established, the status of scientific work should be met with skepticism. Naturalism has an iterative relationship where it redefines philosophy itself with developments in science. Such a view has been met with doubt as it appears dangerously circular. In general, naturalists do not believe it is feasible (at least currently) to provide a universal philosophical foundation for all of science and it is something we must develop as we go on with Science. 

The idea that the search for a "foundation" for science being flawed was famously echoed by Quine in ***Epistemology Naturalized*** but where he goes too far in PGS' opinion is his proposition that all of philosophy will be absorbed by science eventually. Epistemology for example will be reduced to theories of psychology. To PGS, there is a distinction between the questions that philosophy can answer that science can't. It is then useful to further illustrate naturalism by outlining some of the questions it tries to answer that science can't and how it approaches it. PGS provides 3:
- Philosophy aims to answer normative questions of "how should" ideas or observations be handled in science. Philosophy can answer what kind of theories are "rational" to let us distinguish the good ones from the bad ones. One tentative solution to this is instrumental rationalism: the rational theory is the one that best achieves the goal we're pursuing. 
- Philosophy tries to answer the relationship between our everyday understanding of the world with the scientific description of it. On this, PGS will further illustrate it by challenging the theory-ladenness of observation from a naturalists perspective
- Philosophy answers questions on the relation between sciences offering a synoptic view to better answer where science stands right now and what makes good science.
PGS then posits his own definition of naturalism which aims to use "Science as a resource for settling philosophical questions, rather than a replacement for philosophy or the source of philosophy's agenda." In a sense, this could be interpreted as philosophical questions being the true end goal and science is just a method of investigation to answer these higher level questions. To PGS, there is a gap in the way many sociologists and areas from Science Studies try to describe science in that they give no role to the way science as a community or whatever model is being used responds or is constrained by the natural world. To the sociologists and humanities, Science may seem nothing more than "expensive PR exercises and negotiations between factions (p.154-155)" but to see if that is the case or not, we need to answer whether, as PGS puts it, science responds to the outside world. In other words, is there something outside of social or human interactions that constrains the way scientific investigation is done and is this constraint separate from any social influence. In trying to answer these questions, we must separate whether it is possible "in principle" for us to respond to the natural world separate from our social influence and whether this is put in practice. the latter "put into practice", is one that sociologists attack fervently, but the former question, whether we humans in principle respond to the natural world free of social influences, is one we attempt to answer in this chapter by examining the theory-laddenness of observation

# Example of Naturalism in action on the theory-ladenness of observation
A central debate since the 1960s is the question whether data and observations in science are a true neutral source that lets us choose between theories or are all observations subjective and contaminated in some way that prevents us from doing this. Empiricists would generally agree observations are "theory neutral" and are a way to settle disputes between theories, but a challenge has arisen from N.R. Hansen, Kuhn, and Feyerabend who purport the theory-ladenness of observations: observations cannot function in an unbiased way of testing theories because they are affected by the theoretical beliefs of the observer. PGS outlines their arguments as follows:
- As empiricists would agree, a hypothesis is generally presupposed to limit the scope of our tests. But this leads to a "filtering" of observations and bias in what observations to prioritize. While the problem is still prevalent, PGS offers a tentative solution stating that if there is questions on the choice of priority and relevance given to an observation to substantiate a theory, that relevance itself can be tested.
- The choice of theory also effects the language used to support or refute a theory, but PGS claims this is the weakest argument and only unravels the logical positivists views on language. To examine if theories impact on our observational language affects the scientific work done, we need to examine which theories affect which observation and how for while a theory can affect our language, we still need to demonstrate that this affects our scientific work in any meaningful way. For example, scientific theories are generally constructed that permit refutation (a la Popper) in a way that the choice of observational language has no meaningful impact. The discovery of a Precambrian rabbit fossil will throw a wrench in evolution no matter what language we use. This is later addressed in PGS's answer to the third point where we must distinguish between the factors that are influenced by lower level theories of language and perception and whether they have any meaningful impact on our decisions for the higher level cognitive decisions on scientific theories.
- Finally, Kuhn and others have argued that our experiences itself shape the decisions one makes including their guiding theory (i.e. paradigm). Kuhn cites the work done by psychologists on the "active" role of perception that uses our underlying beliefs, including theories, to shape our perception. 
It is on this 3rd point that PGS elaborates further using naturalism. The demonstration of naturalism is done by pulling from psychology to show that our cognitive decisions about higher level theories is separate is separate from our cognitive perception which is influenced by lower-level theories of perception. An underlying belief in a higher level theory can be shown in psychology to have no affect on our raw perception. In Jerry Fodor's article "Observation Reconsidered", the Muller-Lyer illusion is used as an example. While Kuhn and others would use the illusion as an example of our observations being affected by our underlying unconscious theories, PGS notes tha<u>t even if we know about the illusion itself, it does not change the fact that we still see the illusion of one line being longer than the other
</u>. In other words, we have a case where our perception is unaffected by our underlying theory. We see the illusion regardless of what higher-level cognition we have. Fodor links this to research done in modules postulating that different <u>cognitive functions are affected by different subsets of beliefs</u>. In the case of the Muller-Lyers illusion, our perception module is not affected by our unconscious theories, but instead affected by even lower level biases on perspective etc.. This can be extended to show that things like a Kuhnian paradigm do not affect something low-level like raw observations. Note that this only answers the "in principle" question as mentioned before. The modular explanation of perception does not explain what someone would do with the observation so there is still the question of what a scientist does "in practice" with the observation which is one that sociologists fervently attack. To this last point, there are in general two questions on observations and the way data is used that we must answer:
1) To what extent, in principle, can observations using human senses be reliable. This question asks about the individuals capability to neutrally perceive observations
2) Are observations neutral between competing theories. Can humans interact with each other in away that allows for observations to be used as an intersubjective basis theory of choice. This questions asks about the ability for people to collaborate with each other to neutrally mediate between theories.
In summary, we demonstrate naturalism in action here by pulling from the science of psychology to answer a philosophical question on the ability for observations to be a neutral mediator of theories. As a final example to illustrate our ability to now allow our underlying theories impact the way we perceive the world, Einsteinian physics did overhaul Euclidean geometry and we were capable to move to a different framework in non-Euclidean geometry. The goal of naturalism here, as PGS puts it, isn't whether any particular scientific theory can model our philosophical questions. The question is to ask what scientific evidence is out there to settle our questions. In a sense, we are answering philosophical questions scientifically!
