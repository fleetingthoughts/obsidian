Since Kuhn's ***Structures,*** different fields have provided their ideas on what characterizes Science, and PGS will discuss the sociological views. The chapter looks chronologically at the development of ideas of Science from a sociological view making it easier to illustrate main points as new ideas are in reaction to and can be contrasted against old ideas. The sociological views explored are as follows:
1) Old view of sociology by Robert Merton that provides the general factors that characterize and motivate all scientists
2) New sociological view of the "Strong Programme" which postulated factors that influence the beliefs within particular scientific groups
3) The ideas in ***Leviathan and the Air Pump*** by Steven Shapin and Simon Schaffer which further developed the ideas of the Strong Programme
4) The ideas in ***Laboratory Life*** by Steven Woolgar and Bruno Latour which is as being critical of the Strong Programme by the new sociologists.

Robert Merton was the central figure for the earliest sociological views of science. His initial formulation of science has now been referred to as the "weak" sociological views in contrast to the newer "strong views" with the Strong Programme to be discussed later. Robert Merton provides 4 underlying values that characterizes all of Science:
- Universalism: the irrelevance of one's personal attributes and social background.
- Communism: the common ownership of scientific ideas.
- Disinterestedness: the willingness to let go of one's personal interests and ambitions
- Organized Skepticism:  the shared reaction to test propositions as opposed to taking them at face values
The underlying currency that motivates all these values is the need for recognition. The desire for for recognition can also lead to deviancy the most common example of which is accusations of plagiarism. It also promotes more "positive" forms of deviancy such as the mania to publish. Here the argument is made inductively citing historic priority disputes. PGS criticizes the idea that the value of disinterest is in conflict with the motivation.

Merton's views were later expanded to what's called the "Strong Programme" to account for the motivations and beliefs of scientists within specific communities within certain time periods. A central idea to the Strong Programme, is the symmetry principle: explanation of a particular belief should be accounted for by one underlying factor and should not be attributed to multiple explanations. With respect to science, the symmetry principle is applied by accounting for all scientific beliefs as being motivated (and according to PGS, the term "reflected" was used more often) by the societal views at the time. Sociologists fundamentally view communities as being driven by socially established norms that regulate belief and view scientific communities as being no different. Science itself is a community with socially established norms. As such, the strong programme views Science as being driven by the society they belong to and aim to relate particular scientific theories to the social circumstances. Examples are cited with statistics and Eugenics. The strong programme is frequently interpreted as saying that science is politically motivated, but sociologists themselves are reluctant to say so and would instead use phrases like "theories reflect their society". The strong programme is strongly associated with concepts of relativism. Here relativism refers to the absence of a single universal standard that governs the justification of beliefs and is applied by the Strong Programme to Science by proposing that science has no special characteristics outside of regular social communities and that its beliefs are social norms justified from within. PGS criticizes this relativistic view of the Strong programme holding that the sociologists are unable to defend themselves rom accusations of relativism. The views of the Strong Programme are itself a result of their own local norms and beliefs. The Strong Programme faces the paradoxical problem of reflexivity: the theories that view others through a relativistic lens can itself be criticized of having developed its views in a relativistic way.

Later, Steven Shapin and Simon Schaffer published ***Leviathan and the Air Pump*** which has been reviewed as refining the views of the Strong Programme by applying the relativistic view at the more granular level to the very language and terms used by scientists within a paradigm. The book looked the historical dispute between Boyle and Hobbes with Boyle being seen as winning the dispute leading to the establishment of the Royal Society of London which had a long lasting impact on the standards of science. The book viewed this episode sociological perspective to show how the interactions between individuals resulted in the establishment of norms determining the way Science would be conducted. Here specifically Boyle had shifted away from the more metaphysical views of Science which sought causal explanations and sought to explain phenomena by actual actionable criteria (i.e. operationalism). The book also places this episode in the context of the English Civil War where Boyle believed an operational view of things would be more concrete and establish order. In winning the dispute and establishing the Royal Society, Boyle had replaced conventional metaphysical definitions of terms such as "vacuum" which postulated on the idea of "nothing" with operational criteria in the context of experimental apparatus. A vacuum would not require postulation to the meaning of nothing and would instead be defined by pressure and measurements through apparatus like the air pump. In doing so, Boyle had established what's called a new language game for Science. The language game was a term used by Wittgenstein who proposed that the social uses of terms is all there is to language which is contrasted against the logical positivists who sought an abstract universal principle to ascribe meaning to language. 

PGS does criticize the book citing it goes too far with its relativistic views which is best summed up in a quote from the book: "it is ourselves and not reality that is responsible for what we know." PGS believes this is a false dichotomy and that there is a 3rd option in that Science is shaped by both reality AND ourselves. 

Finally, a book by Steve Woolgar and Bruno Latour called the ***Laboratory Life*** is published and is viewed as a criticism of the Strong Programme. The book details the Latour and Woolgar's observations in the Salk Laboratory as they shadow the scientists. The book satirizes the sociologists by framing their observations from an anthropologists view to the point of absurdity. The Strong programme thought the model of science being governed by data and facts as a naive one, but perhaps their views are itself naive as they model science as being governed by social factors instead of facts. They had simply swapped one factor for another. Latour and Woolgar, ideas are called the Actor-Network Theory (ANT) which is contrasted against the Strong Programmes hierarchal view of society being the driver and influence on human behavior. ANT flattens the hierarchal relationship viewing society as being equally influenced by individual human behaviors and vice-versa. Society and its impacts on individual human behavior are the result of an iterative process between each other in which both are the products not the cause for one another. For instance, while the Strong Programme may see Science as the influencing factor on scientific work, ANT would see the scientific work itself as the driver. Putting this idea into application, when one scientific theory wins out over the other, according to Latour, it would be viewed as the "construction" of new facts driven by the scientific work done as opposed to some external societal force. Here PGS takes a great interest in the methodologies ANT employs. To truly understand the language game of a scientific community, it is useful to see how the terms are used at the individual level. Complicated terms like "truth" and "nature" can be understood by how they are used as resources in interactions at the individual level, and from these interactions, their use becomes a unofficial definition of the term itself.

PGS summarizes his views on the contribution that sociologists offered to the philosophy of science. Sociologists would often admit that the real world does put a constraint on theories employed by Scientists, but due to the ambiguity of observations (e.g. Quine-Duhem thesis, the problem of induction), the data goes through many layers of reinterpretation and reconstruction. To PGS, it is this layer of interpretation and reconstruction that can be explained by social interactions.
