Outline:
- Old view of sociology by Robert Merton
- New view that gets more into the details about particuolar scientific beliefs. Answers why scientsts bleive what they do
Chapter looks at how sociology seeks to explain science
- Robert Merton. Science guided by 4 principles
- basic currency is recognition
- deviant behaviour caused by desire for recognition fraud libel, slander
- mild forms of deviancy such as mania to publish
PGS: distinteredness in conflict with recognition

- New sociology views science as a community no different than other communities but just in the specifics of how they regulate their beliefs.
- argue that scientific endeavours are motivated by social circumstances. Politically driven science
- PGS: argues new sociology is vague on this. They do not make a specific connection but will only note interesting correlations in which science was in service to a political belief.
- relativism as a driver for beliefs. These beliefs originate from within the social circle as opposed to some universal truth

Discuses Simon Schaffer and Steven Shapin.
- Development of the royal society of londong
- Reflection of civil unrest in england. Specific kind of scientific thinking torectify
- replacement of definitions: artistotelian metaphyiscal question of vacuum replaced with operational definitions
- Simon schaffer echo wittegenstein's concept of the language game propose that a lot of science is effectively a social construct. . "it is ourselves and not reality that is responsible for what we know"
	- PGS criticizes this as a false dichotomy and that there is a 3rd option: both reality and ourselves are both responsible for what we know.
Steven woolgar and bruno latour
- the work satirically describes the work done in the Salk Institute. Seen as a caricature of the sociologist.
- Employs actor-network theory. The idea that there are no great external societal factors influencing a the behaviours of a social group. There is no hierarchy. ANT flattens the hierarchal relationship between society and individual human behaviour. Both the behaviours and society iteratively influence each other. For example empiricists use data as their "end all be all" and sociologists used "social factors" as the driver for behaviour

PGS's summary of the sociologists: While nature puts constraints on how we review the world, in teh end, the interpretation of this data goes through so much warping that it is inevitable that social matters influence how we view the data.
