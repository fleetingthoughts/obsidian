1) Who do we want to be rulers:
	1) someone who knows what good and virtue is so they can create such laws (analogy of blind painter)
	2) Someone with experience in these virtues
2) We will dive into the nature of the philosopher to determine they have both of these
3) Define what it means to love something. A philosopher is defined as a lover of wisdom. Loving something else is a different aspect outside of being a philosopher (principle of noncontradiction)
4) Philosophers therefore do not like not wisdom: falsehoods. They have wisdom
5) Assumption of the specialization results in diminishing capacity for other virtues. 
6) A philosopher will have:
	1) Less capacity to love other things. Tehy have moderation
	2) Because we define them as lovers of knowledge which are forms, they are less concerned with worldly material things including death so they are not cowardly. Philosophers have courage
	3) Because they have wisdom, courage, and moderation, they will not cheat lie or steal, they will exhibit outward justice. Seems to be a shift in definition of justice to external justice here
7) Additional criteria for philosopher: to be able to learn well.
8) Summary: philosopher is a good learner, magnificent, charming, and a kinsman of truth justice courage, and moderation.
9) Adeimantus says that although each argument is sound, the net effect seems anything but, we frequently see people studying philosophy become useless or vicious
10) Socrates makes analogy of ship of state. Socrates proposes the blame is on the masses for not exercising the philosopher. It would actually be an anomaly if philosophers appeared useful
	1) The people vying for captain selfishly are the masses
	2) The actual skilled sailor is the apparantly useless stargazor = philosopher
11) Socrates proposes the blame is on the fake practicers of philosophy who are skilled at persuading. 
12) We will explain why the many are viscious and that philosophy isn't to blame:"
	1) We just argued that philosophers have the four cardinal virtues. To explain the belief that philosophers are useless and bad, by process of elimination, the fault must be with the masses
	2) We will look at at how the few that are philosophic become corrupted, and those that don't naturally are not employed to usefulness
13) Socrates argues how philosophers can be corrupted:
	1) Assumption: the most virtuous natures also have strong capacities for visciousness. 
	2) It is their rearing. In the current setting of a democratic country, we are persuaded by what is popular. We punish people when we cannot persuade in court, fines, etc./.
	3) Time with the democratic masses promotes populism and persuasion, we conflate what is popular and persuasive as what is true knowledge. In other words, democracy encourages opiners who are not true philosophers. Socrates uses the analogy of the great beast that is the mass of democracy and in trying to tame the beast, we confuse it with wisdom.
	4) From 3), we see a second force that inflicts damage to philosophy: opiners who attempt to appease the beast and conflate their success with wisdom.
	5) The multitude are assumed to be unable to perceive forms so they are only persuaded by opinions. Therefore the sophists who appease them also do not exercise wisdom in their art
14) Socrates nexts walks through what happens when someone with a true philosophic nature encounters the polloi and the sophists
	1) By their nature, they are talented and good at many things and so the polloi will want to persuade them to help them to opine away from his nature
	2) The true philosophers who escape this will then know that the majority are unable to perceive forms and will therefore be unconvinced and live a a private life
15) Socrates then introduces a third force against philosophy. Because true philosophers learn that the polloi cannot be convinced and leave, fakers will take up its place because philosophy has a esteemed title. " a little, bald-headed worker in bronze who has gotten some silver, and , newly released from bonds, just washed in a bathhouse, wearing a new-made cloak and got up like a bridegroom, is about to marry his master's daughter"

17) But hypothetically what city could "take philosophy in hand without being destroyed" (497 e). Is it our ideal city that we modelled in the previous books? This begins another long argument
18) Socrates makes another comment about a deficiency in the masses who are unable to perceive forms: They can never see hypotheticals, and even imagine a situation where a city that accepts philosophers can be set up because they've never seen it. They are unable to create or imagine the ideals
19) Socrates also humbles himself to not blame the masses as the fake philosophers argue like drunken revelers and give philosophy a bad name

	1) fast learners are quick chaotic
	2) Orderly people are slow learners.

24) The children will have to go through the usual training of music and gymnastics but there is the ultiamte test. First we need to know what "good" is
25) Socrates emphasizes that we need to understand what something is before talking about it. This lack of clarity is the source of many conflict. He gives two classic examples of good: it is prudence (circular reasoning. prudence is about the good), and it is pleasure (then bad is also good)
26) What we do establish: we want the objective good and are not satisfied with an opinion of it. This is different from other forms like justice and beauty where we can be satisfied by opinions. Socrates surmises a guardian is worth little if he doesn't understand the good in virtues like justice and beauty. So it is important we understand what "good" is
27) Despite glaucon and adeimantus, encouragement, Socrates admits good is outside his capacity at the moment but we will argue for a child of good (507 a)
28) the sun analogy of good
	1) 506 b. A complicated parapgraph to parse but basically restates his theory of forms from chapter 5. We refer to many material things as fair (beautiful) but fair itself is an idea (form) that is many and IS (knowledge). The material things we see while the idea is invisible and intelected
	2) Hearing is a two part system: the sound and the organ to hear
	3) Sight required a third part: the object itself, the organ to see, and light
	4) Light is the realm of the gods
	5) Breakdown of the analogy: knowledge is our eyes, truth is the form being perceived, and good is the light that illuminates and lets us perceive it
29) Line analogy to represent levels of knowledge and reality: 
	1) A line that is bisected both represent two sides: between things that which is seen (material) and that which IS (forms).
	2) The material is divided the ways we do conventionally, animals, paintings, etc..
	3) Forms is divided as follows: 
		1) axioms that do not justify themselves further (geometry and math).  We cannot go backwards. These use visible forms to make arguments. When we talk about a shape like a square the theorems are about the true square not the square I drew. These are intelligible forms that produce images that we can intellect
		2) Hypotheses that we use as "springboards" (511 b) to go backwards. Its images are forms
