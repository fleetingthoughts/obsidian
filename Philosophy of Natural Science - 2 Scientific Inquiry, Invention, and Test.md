In this chapter, Carl Hempel proposes that the essential criteria that distinguishes empirical natural sciences from non-empirical sciences and social sciences in its aim to discover the world through a process called the scientific inquiry. The chapter outlines a proposed basic structure of a scientific inquiry (i.e. hypothetico-deductive method that is not coined by him) from which specific elements such as the confirmation, the standard by which a proposition is accepted, etc. are explored in future chapters. The overview of the scientific inquiry is outlined as follows
- A historical case study in the scientific inquiry by Ignaz Semmelweiss to determine why one of the divisions experienced more childbirth deaths than another ward and how to prevent it.
- The dichotomy of a scientific inquiry in the formulation of a hypothesis and the testing of a hypothesis
- Hempel's proposed "method of hypothesis" (hypothetico-deductive method) of testing of the deductions of a hypothesis and an assessment of the deductively valid and invalid conclusions
- The formulation of a hypothesis. The caveats with inductive inference and Hempel's criticism of the narrow inductivist conception of scientific inquiry in which hypotheses are formed by inductive inference from antecedently collected data
- The role of imagination and creativity in the inductive and deductive inquiries due to the inability to formulate deductive rules to guide inferential procedures that formulate a conclusion from presupposed data
- Hempel's proposed analogy between math and science in that the "criteria for sound arguments"
- Hempel's summary of his position on scientific inquiry (hypothetico-deductive method or method of hypothesis) and a summary of the two key processes of hypothesis formulation and testing: Hypothesis must be presupposed and testing imposes a criteria of soundness on the proposition that can deductively negate but cannot deductively affirm.

Hempel uses the historical case of Ignaz Semmelweis as an example of what he considers to be quintessential scientific inquiry. The way Hempel breaks down the elements of this inquiry highlights what he considers to be the two most important parts of the process:
1) The formulation of a hypothesis
2) The testing of the hypothesis
Expounding on 2), Hempel proposes a hypothesis is tested following a deductive model that assesses the soundness of its implications, that is if $H$ is our hypothesis, then the hypothesis must make implications $I$ and so the the goal of testing is to determine whether $H$ is true by whether $I$ is true or not. Such a deductive model has limitations in the conclusions we can draw:
- We can make deductive conclusions on $H$ being false. $H\implies I$ is equivalent to $\neg I \implies \neg H$ so if we determine $I$ is false, then we can conclude $H$ is as well. (modus tollens or denying the consequent)
- We cannot conclude that $H$ is true if $I$ is true. $H \implies I$ does not equate to $I \implies H$. (fallacy of affirming the consequent).
And so following the rules of deduction, we cannot provide a complete proof of a scientific hypothesis

With regards to 1) Hempel emphasizes the creative and imaginative role of hypothesis formulation and criticizes the claims that there exists rules of deductions that allow us to formulate a hypothesis. In other words, we cannot deductively infer a hypothesis (this effectively is a fundamental problem of induction). Hempel sets up an alternative method of scientific inquiry that he calls the "narrow inductivist conception of scientific inquiry" and claims it is invalid both from a practical and deductive standpoint. The narrow inductivist conception that he claims is untenable is as follows:
1) All the observation and facts are gathered
2) The observation and facts in 1) are analyzed and categorized
3) A hypothesis is formed
4) The generalizations from this hypothesis are further tested to determine its extent
The criticism is as follows:
- for 1) it is impractical to determine all the facts and even if we try to narrow the scope and collect only the relevant facts, we need to presuppose a hypothesis to determine what is relevant. A problem does not determine what is relevant. We need a hypothesis to guide us
- The impracticality of categorization follows from the same points in 1) in which there are infinite ways to categorize the data and so a guiding hypothesis is required to narrow the scope
And so the only real steps Hempel considers a practical part of scientific inquiry is 3)-4). Hempel does not believe there is a mechanical procedure for constructing a hypothesis and that the hypothesis and theories are "invented (p.15)" and are "happy guesses (p.15)" and cites a historic example in Kekule dreaming the structure of Benzene. 

Hempel proposes instead that scientific inquiry is characterized by how they are "accepted into the body of scientific knowledge (p.16)". To convince us of this, he looks at the problem deductively to show 1) how impractical a deductive formulation of a hypothesis can be and 2) shows by example that creativity and imagination play an important role even in the most deductive field of math creativity.
1) <u>The infinite possibilities that can be deduced from a premise found to be true</u>: Assume a hypothesis can be inferred from a premise determined to be true, this assumes we can determine THE conclusion to use when in reality there is an infinite number of possibilities. If a set of premise $p$ is determined to be true, then it is natural to assume $p$ is the hypothesis, but it is also possible $p \lor q$ is a valid hypothesis deductively where $q$ can be any nonsense answer.
2) <u>Subjective appeal to creativity in deductive sciences by exposing its importance in what is considered the most deductive field </u>: Math is subject to the same issues as 1) in that there are infinite possibilities given the set of axioms but math yet endeavors. But it is noted that the rules of deduction in math give no direction to inferential procedures on "THE CONCLUSION"  to be drawn from axioms. Additionally, there are no deductive rules for even formulating the proof. <u>But the role of deductive rules is to provide a criteria for soundness of arguments </u>

The systematic role of deduction in providing criteria of soundness of arguments for math as noted in 2) is what Hempel proposes for scientific inquiry as well. Hempel therefore summarizes his position the two-step process of scientific inquiry unique to the empirical study of natural sciences and in doing so Hempel characterizes the role of induction in science as well:
- ***Testing***: Any rules of induction or science we conceive cannot be on hypothesis formulation but rather validation of a proposed hypothesis. Induction therefore is a set of rules or systematic way of validating the arguments made by a hypothesis. The elements of these rules and criteria such as confirmation and acceptance are discussed in future chapters.
- ***Hypothesis:*** Hypothesis must be presupposed and then the conclusions are tested and the soundness of the argument can be subject to some deductive rules such as modus tollens (hypothetico-deductive method)
