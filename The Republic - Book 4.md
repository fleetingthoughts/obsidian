# Plato completes his description of the republic
1) Adeimantus: How can we justify letting guardians live like mercenaries.
	- The goal is to maximize happiness for the larger group, in this case a city, as opposed to the happiness of just one group of people such as the guardians.

2) What practical advantage does shunning wealth have for the longevity of a republic in the face of other cities that accept and are wealthy
	- Argued by analogy with experienced boxers and rich untrained boxers
	- Socrates offers the strategic diplomatic solution: The wealthy are more inclined to cooperate with trained cities that shun wealth for they get to keep the spoils of war.
3) Adeimantus adds a caveat to Socrates' answer to 2): cooperation with the wealthy may poison the ascetic values we curated for our guardians.
	- Socrates argues that every rich city is a fragile cooperation between the wealthy and the poor whom we may not be able to fight as united whole but can sow the seeds of discord.
	- The argument is that wealth is not a strength to a republic but a structural disease and results in a fragmented structure to the republic hence why it must be shunned from Plato's republic. Every city must  only be as big so that they don't reach that  point of luxury and disease.
4) Socrates further adds that guardians should not let a city be too big or small but sufficient. 
5) We must also rear guardians and identify them early and not make any changes to their education. Innovation in music or the education will destroy the established order. 
	1) The argument seems to be on the practical side and Socrates cites Damon that changes in music always accompany political change
6) Socrates highlights the importance of upbringing to the established order as it is impossible to cover every aspect of order with literal laws. 
	- An analogy is made to sick people. Continuously attempts to treat without the underlying general well-being only makes one more sick. The medicine are the attempts at literal law while the general well-being and exercise is the zeitgeist.
7) The first great laws are to the gods
# The search for justice in the city
2) Now we've completed our city, because it is virtuous, it must have 4 things: wisdom, courage, modesty, and justice. We search for justice by process of elimination, once we've found 3 out of the 4, the remaining goodness of our city must be justice.
3) The wisdom in a city is classified by whether the rules have them or not. Wisdom is in the smallest part
	1) The good counsel of ordinary craftsman do not influence the governing of a city so it cannot be in the populace.
	2) Wisdom is in the perfect guardians called the rulers
4) Courage takes the form of preservation of our education.
	1) Courage is the one who defends the city and takes field on its behalf. This is a result of their virtues and opinions brought about by their education. Socrates treats the typical examples of courage as consequences of courage and points to the true thing that generates these consequences: their education
	2) Socrates illustrates by analogy with dyeing. In the same way wool is preserved undyed so that it can receive and retain the best possible color, a good education dyes our opinion in a way that we retain and let our virtues shine as brightly as possible
5) Socrates clarifies this is political courage randomly in one sentence
6) Moderation resides in all its citizens and is a form of harmony. Similar to courage, Socrates does not consider the usual suspects of moderation "true moderation" but looks at the generating principles: the harmony between all the classes:
	1) Conventional moderation is to overcome desires and be "stronger then oneself". Socrates considers that a contradiction and redefines it: there is a better and worse part in our selves and the better part over coming the worse is what it means to be "stronger then oneself"
	2) We accept that most diverse pleasures and pains reside in the common many (and children and women lol) while the simple desires lead by intelligence are in the best educated and few. The worse is in the many while the better is in the few
	3) Socrates equivocates 2) and 1) to the city. We are letting the better few get the better of the baser "many". Modesty is letting few virtuous people rule over the worse. It is a harmony between classes in letting the rulers rule over the many
7) Justice is then by process of elimination the last good thing in our city: minding your own business
	1) We presume that it is just to do what is one's own profession: rulers rule. We show that that justice is a necessary feature of doing one's craft. What remains is to show sufficiency.
	2) Socrates off hand alludes there are 3 classes
	3) We establish that people trying to do stuff outside their expertise harms the city. A carpenter shouldn't be a soldier and vice versa. People minding their own business is best for city. We assume then that injustice harms so this harming of the city is a form of injustice
8) Continue to test our proposition of justice by testing its consequences:  We agreed that justice in the city is reflected in the individual, so to test our proposition of justice in the city, lets see what its consequences are: what does it mean for an individual. If the consequences are faulty, then we have to reform our proposition of justice in the city. This is a form of modus tollens:
9) We assume the sum is made up of its parts. We can classify the 4 virtues of the city only because its citizens and the human soul is split into the 4 same values of wisdom, courage, modesty, and justice
10) Do we act based on what one part of the soul does or with the whole soul once all 4 parts are in unity? Socrates and glaucon acknowledge its difficulty
	1) Analogy standing still and moving is a contradiction
	2) We assume an even more precise definition of 1) even if a man is standing still but moving his hands or head, he is still considred standing still. 
11) How we define someone who wants something but doesn't do it. At some point we distinguish the two parts when we have conflicting actions (thirsty but not drinking) between the soul and spirit
12) There is a third part that is auxiliary to the calculating
13) Socrates equivocates the 3 classes in the city to the 3 parts in the soul continue our assumption of the city being the sum of its individual parts
	1) The calculating part of the soul rules
	2) The spirit is obedient to the ally and is soothed by harmony and is the auxialiary
