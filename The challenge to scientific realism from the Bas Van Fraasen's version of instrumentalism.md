PGS discusses the views of Bas Van Fraasen in opposition to scientific realism. Van Fraasen's views is a version of instrumentalism: a pragmatic view opposed to scientific reality that believes the only worth of an idea is the one that solves our problems, predicts, and explains phenomena. Instrumentalism would view the search for causal explanations and the underlying mechanisms to be a worthless endeavor. If the theoretical model lets me make accurate predictions, what point is there in one trying to find the underlying cause. Though Van Fraasen's views differ on some key points. His viewpoints are summarized as follows. To accept a theory is to:
1) provisionally believe the theory is empirically accurate to explain phenomena
2) accept the use of the theory when thinking about further problems and expanding the scope
It is in point 2) that Van Fraasen differs from traditional instrumentalists. While the instrumentalists believes there is not value in figuring out the underlying mechanisms, Van Fraasen accepts that scientists "live in" their own theories to create hypothetical scenarios worth testing to expand their theories (see Einstein's thought experiments).

Where Fraasen's views come into conflict with PGS's views is that "empirical accuracy" per 1) is the only requirement for a theory, and there is no need to go beyond that. The justification for 2) is to produce more empirically accurate theories. Whereas the scientific realist would seek to understand the independent reality, Van Fraasen reduces the aims of science to only empirical adequacy.

The criticism to Van Fraasen's views is that it does not distinguish between the different levels of being "observable". Specifically, PGS distinguishes between observable and detectable objects that cannot be directly observed. Van Fraasen's views would see no value in learning about the detectable objects if its detection serves our purpose. What PGS seems to be getting at is that one of the goals in science is not just to explain causal relationships, but also to push the boundaries on what is observable. Many of the great works in Science made new discoveries as they sought to explain causal explanations or took causal explanations literally. Van Fraasen's views would throw out the part of science that discovered germs which he would view as a pointless endeavor as the theoretical model of germs served our purposes. The blurred boundary between the observable, unobservable, and detectable is permitted in PGS's view of scientific realism which pushes the responsibility on science to tell us what is observable.
