### Does justice follow Simonides' definition: Justice is giving a man what he is owed.
1) Polemarchus interjects and says this must be the case as it is Simonides' definition of justice
2) Socrates refutes by counterexample where such a situation is obviously wrong: giving a mad man his weapon back. 
### Alternative interpretation of Simonides' definition of justice. Giving enemies back what they are owed: harm
***Elaboration***: Socrates gives Simonides the benefit of the doubt in that he fashioned a riddle, and we examine whether it is just to give enemies what they are owed.

Socrates looks for concrete examples of this form of justice and demonstrates that in these general scenarios, justice is useless so:
1) If justice is giving a person what they are owed. The practitioner such as a doctor, or ship captain are in the best position to do so in their respective expertise
2) We assume that Justice can be executed during war time naturally and Polemarchus asserts that it is useful in peacetime in the form of contract writing
3) We find in peacetime, the one who is most useful is skill expertise in the area of the contract.
4) Polemarchus:  Justice is useful when the money itself in contracts must be kept safe
5) Socrates: Then justice is only useful when the very thing is useless. Skilled expertise can take care of the practical side of a contract, but justice only comes in when discussing things that are not needed: in useless things
Socrates illustrates by example that one who is good at preventing is also good at executing it
6) Justice then looks like a robber
