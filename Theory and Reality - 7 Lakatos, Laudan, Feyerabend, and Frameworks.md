- intro overview of the legacy of 3 key philosophers
- description of lakatos
	- direct reaction against kuhn. Seek to provide a methdological more normative hard-lined description of science
	- competition between research programmes
	- scientific change: 1) change within individual research programs always progressing and is risk-taking. 2) change at collection of research programs
	- unable to decide on criteria to select appropriate non-degenerating research programme
		- Both feyerabend and PGS criticise lakatos here. Lakatos has given neither a sufficient normative approach to science nor a descriptive one (rationalizing the past
- Kuhn vs Lakatos
	- Kuhn trusts the natural process
	- Lakatos wants a firm methodological approach (here his inability to decide when to abandon a research programme is a big  hole)
- Laudan's ideas based on "Progress and its PRoblems
	- research traditions model is like research programme but not strictly linear in progression and capable of morphing its hard core
	- pursuit vs acceptence
	- provided clear criteria and methdologies on selection of research tradition based on balance of pursuit and acceptance
	- PGS's criticism: does not take into account # of ppl working on it. If we're to find a normative description for science we need to hedge our bets better
- Feyereband ideas from "against method"
	- Nothing should get in the way of science. The only rule is no rule
	- Characterized by his reaction to Kuhn:
		- While lakatos and Laudan saw him as an anarchist, Feyerebendnd saw the opposite and thought he was too close-minded
- Most of Feyerebend's "against method" stems from historical justification of Galileo's dialogue
	- Science is about challenging everyday observations
	- Feyerebend's challenge: Could any methodology, theory of confirmation, philosophy of science, or some other tool been able to come up with the seemingly contradictory and counterintuitive ideas that occured in major episodes of science. Feyerebend does not think so, this is what it means to be constantly thinking outside the box and being "against method"
	- PGS's criticism: Feyerebend claims because a scientific principle may go wrong, we should always be doubtful of it, but then that gives us little ground to work with or to refine if we're constantly doubting everything.
- Principle of tenacity
- Principle of proliferation
- Both these principles are working in tandem constantly and one does not whave to wait for hte other (contrast with Kuhn where it happens in sequence)
- No idea is truly off the table. But back to the popperian problem with how do we choose the best bridge? Feyerbend's view is almost an aesthetic artistic one where every idea has value but when it comes to practicality, it fails
