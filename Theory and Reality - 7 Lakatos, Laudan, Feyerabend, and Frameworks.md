Since Kuhn's ***Structures***, reactions to his ideas on Science and the way he approached the philosophy of Science in general were plenty. Here we will discuss three main philosophers in order: 1) Imre Lakatos, 2) Larry Laudan, and 3) Paul Feyerabend. PGS impresses on us the reputation and legacy of each. Both Lakatos and Laudan were repulsed by their interpretation of Kuhn's idea of science as a propagandist and irrational affair which they sought to repair by providing a methodological approach to science. On the other hand, Paul Feyerabend, in PGS's view, was more astute in his assessment of Kuhn and saw his approach to Science, especially Normal Science, as too close-minded. Feyerabend in particular was quite a maverick in the philosophy of Science

# Imre Lakatos and the Research Programme
Imre Lakatos interpreted Kuhn's view of science as an irrational process largely driven by mob psychology. PGS throughout the book makes it a point to rebut this interpretation and instead believes Kuhn's views were actually more structured and the supposed turmoil during a revolution is all part of a "natural" and necessary process for science. Science to Kuhn, as interpreted by PGS, was a delicate balance of change and close-mindedness; and instead, Kuhn's view of science should be criticized for the stringent need of close-mindedness.

As mentioned previously in chapter 5 on the normal science described Kuhn, one of the criticism levied against him was the possibility of multiple paradigms coexisting. Lakatos was one such critic and characterized his view of science by replacing Kuhn's paradigm with the research programme. The research programme is similar to the paradigm but it differs in that multiple research programmes exist at once and Science should instead be driven by a competition between research programmes. In this way, Lakatos seeks to undo the damage done by Kuhn and provide a normative approach to science, but he still makes an appeal to history to justify his views in the same way Kuhn does. The research programme is structured as follows: 1) a hard core that serves as the basic ideas central to the research programme, and 2) the protective belt that consists of the ideas less fundamental than the hard core and are ideas or tools in service to the hard core. 

Using the research programme structure, Lakatos provides his own view and methodology on how Scientific change should occur. Lakatos first distinguishes between two kinds of changes:
1) Changes within individual research programmes,
2) Change at the level of a collection of research programmes within a certain field. This involves a shift between research programmes
For 1), these changes should only be made to the protective belt and do not affect the hard core. These changes are always progressive and seek to expand its application and predictive power. The progress envisioned by Lakatos draws on the Popperian risk-taking attribute (Lakatos himself was a student of Popper). If this progress is unable to materialize and the research programme can only cover up its problems, it is, in Lakatos' terms, degenerating. Changes for 2) therefore requires a criteria to evaluate between research programmes and it seems obvious to choose the one that is the least degenerating, but Lakatos admits that in history and that it is also rational to hold onto even degenerating programmes. This is in contrast to Popper who would abandon the programme at the first sight of refutation. But the issue here is Lakatos is unable to provide a criteria for when to abandon a research programme. To PGS and Feyerabend, the lack of a criteria is the Achilles heel to Lakatos' argument who aims to provide a normative methodology for Scientists to follow. To PGS, Lakatos, by his own admission, believes it is justifiable to try to rationalize past historical examples to crystallize a thesis even if it means misrepresenting the original history (PGS admits astonishment at how few people have criticized Lakatos on this front). To PGS, it appears Lakatos' project was a way of retrospectively describing episodes in science as rational.

Lakatos wanted Science to be guided by methodological rules or at least be a told a story of that kind. Kuhn believed that the methodology can only exist during normal science and that there was no such thing during the revolution WHICH IS FINE AND HEALTHY FOR SCIENCE. Lakatos' views attempt to bridge Kuhn's Science with the risk-taking nature of Popperian science
# Laudan and Research Traditions
Larry Laudan's come from his book "Progress and Its Problems" and are a refinement of Lakatos' ideas. Laudan's goals are aligned with Lakatos in the sense that he wanted to provide a methodology to scientific change and save it from the chaos of Kuhnian science. Laudan's model is that of the "research tradition" and is distinguished from Lakatos' Research Programme by not requiring a strict progression in ideas and allowing for changes in the hard core.

Laudan further expands on Lakatos by providing a criteria by which a research tradition is selected which fills in the gap of Lakatos' model which did not have an adequate criteria to choose between research programmes. Laudan characterizes a scientists choice in a research programme in two ways:
- Acceptance: The research tradition that the scientist believes in more
- Pursuit: The research tradition that a scientist wants to try out but doesn't have the same confidence in it as the one he "accepts"
The critical thing here is the Laudanian scientist can work in multiple research traditions at once which is in contrast to the indoctrination of a Kuhnian scientist to one paradigm. Thus, this allows for a scientist to work for even a failing research tradition (i.e. a degenerating research programme in Lakatos' terms) for he would simply "pursue" it just to try it out. There are still some cases where Laudan's pursuit and acceptance model doesn't take into account, but PGS instead rebuts both Laudan and Lakatos here by their failure to take into account the number of people working in a research tradition or programme when choosing between a programme/tradition. Both Laudan and Lakatos sought to provide a method to Kuhn's madness but they should surely see the benefits in hedging the risks in science by not putting all your proverbial eggs in one basket and flooding the most successful research tradition with all the good scientists. To PGS, Lakatos and Laudan fail to provide an answer to the ideal distribution of people across rival research programmes that is the best for science nor a means to attain this distribution.
# Feyerabend
- Feyereband ideas from "against method"
	- Nothing should get in the way of science. The only rule is no rule
	- Characterized by his reaction to Kuhn:
		- While lakatos and Laudan saw him as an anarchist, Feyerebendnd saw the opposite and thought he was too close-minded
- Most of Feyerebend's "against method" stems from historical justification of Galileo's dialogue
	- Science is about challenging everyday observations
	- Feyerebend's challenge: Could any methodology, theory of confirmation, philosophy of science, or some other tool been able to come up with the seemingly contradictory and counterintuitive ideas that occured in major episodes of science. Feyerebend does not think so, this is what it means to be constantly thinking outside the box and being "against method"
	- PGS's criticism: Feyerebend claims because a scientific principle may go wrong, we should always be doubtful of it, but then that gives us little ground to work with or to refine if we're constantly doubting everything.
- Principle of tenacity
- Principle of proliferation
- Both these principles are working in tandem constantly and one does not whave to wait for hte other (contrast with Kuhn where it happens in sequence)
- No idea is truly off the table. But back to the popperian problem with how do we choose the best bridge? Feyerbend's view is almost an aesthetic artistic one where every idea has value but when it comes to practicality, it fails
