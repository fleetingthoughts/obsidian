The problems addressed:
- Phenomenalism cannot account for self
- Mind vs body issue. The distinction between mental and physically material things. We say this is a linguistic problem caused buy us assuming that a mind an physical body exists
- Accusations of solipsism and that our phenomenalism is a private account of material things leading us to never be able to reconcile with each other on synthetic statements

Definition of material things required us to adopt a phenomenalist standpoint. The system of definitions avoided the problem of perception. The same principles will avoid traditional philosophical problems of existence.
1) Ayer reiterates his position. Most problems are empirical, namely psychological, in nature and to be solved by scientific inquiry
2) The 3 questions that cannot be solved empirically with psychology
	1) Mental vs physical sense contents.
	2) Are senses private to an individual? How can we know the senses we have are the same as others.
	3) Can we know that a sense exists without being experienced.
3) The solution by a priori by our method of analysis as there is verifiable criteria.
4) We do not accept the model that purports the existence of a substance that does the sensing. We propose sense contents are compared among each other and so they entail an entire sense experience. Sense contents "occur". They do not "exist"
5) Existence is a statement of material things. We avoid it when talking about sense contents for sense contents "occur" to establish the "existence" of material things at a higher level of hierarchy.
6) physical and mental distinguish material things (i.e. logical constructions of sense content) rather than sense content themselves. The same sense content can belong to both mental and physical objects so applying it to sense content is not a useful feature. Our method of analysis solves the physical vs mental distinction by reducing it to the comparison of sense contents that have "mental" or "physical quality" which Ayer explicitly has no interest in defining.
7) The experiencing of a sense content can be broken down syntactically so that they are all empirical propositions that cannot be resolved analytically using a priori.
8) Self as a substance (that exists) is a source of misunderstanding producing the traditional philosophical question. The question of self is incompatible with the verification criteria and Ayer's system of analysis as it results in a paradox that we can never determine what the self is. Its an impossible question. Because it is unverifiable, it is an analytic proposition that contradicts itself so it is the a priori "the existence of the self as a substance" that is wrong. 
	1) Another way of seeing it is there is no justification that the self exists so we must account for it then to just flip the hierarchy and say the sense experiences is what entails the self. 
	2) There is no reason why sensing should not have epistemological priority over existence.
9) This addresses the traditional problem of self by Hume as well. Hume accepted that the self is a bundle of perceptions, but could not find a way to unify the perceptions to explain self because the number of incoherent perceptions far exceeds the coherent perceptions we use to characterize material things. Hume could not give a phenomenalist account of self. 
	1) Our solution is that the problem is a useless paradox but is solved if we accept the sensations at a higher level of hierarch than the self. The mistake is that we assume the "self" to be a substance that is at a higher level than perceptions. We reframe the problem of self as a linguistic problem with senses at the top of epistemological priority.
10) Ayer's formulation of self as a logical construction of sense-content that are private to himself leads to accusations of solipsism and it is purported by the accusers such as L. S. Stebbing that solipsism is false so Ayer's idea is as well. Ayer rejects this description of his linguistic redefinition of self
11) The accusation of solipsism asks what rational reason we have for believing in the existence of other people and not the psychological tendency empirically validated in infants to believe in the existence of other people.
12) Our counter argument is that it isn't that the contents of their argument is false, its the underyling assumption leading to that argument that is false: the assumption that other's peoples experiences are inaccessible to me is false.
13) We address the question of whether we live in the common world. If sense-contents are private thoughts, how can we ever agree on any synthetic proposition. The source of confusion is the metaphysical way we define "common world" and "sharing the same experience. We solve the question by defining it as propositions that can be answered empirically
	- Define the qualitative identity and difference of two people's sense contents in terms of their reactions to empirical tests
	- Define a common world as to assert that we understand and agree on the same synthetic proposition and have minimal qualitative differences in reactions to the material thing.

# Historical Examples
***Hume's rejection of a substantive ego.*** Hume's phenomenalist view rejected the substance of ego as we cannot observe it. But he could not use his phenomenalist view to justify the self through senses alone. He distinguishes between self-conscious (having coherent thoughts) and self-identity (the capacity to sense) because he purports that the incoherent thoughts, such as a lethologica, also constitute self-identity and all of incoherent and coherent memory is a part of self. And so if incoherent senses constitute self, we cannot use senses to justify self: <u>we cannot give a phenomenalistic account of self</u>
