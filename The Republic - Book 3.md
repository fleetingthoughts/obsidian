# Outline
1) ***Censorship of art to avoid corrupting the youth.*** Continuing the censorship of artists like Homer and Aeschylus, we censor art that does not promote ideals of courage and moderation required in soldiers. Additionally we censor most imitative styles of speech in favor of simplistic modes so people can focus on honing their respective crafts.
2) ***The criteria for appropriate artistic upbringing of soldiers in our city***. Music must be simple and reinforce virtues and to be a reflection of the virtues we want our citizens to practice. All other forms of music must be censored
3) ***The criteria for the appropriate physical training of soldiers in our ideal city***. Soldiers require moderation in diet to maintain overall health. Medicine must be practiced in service to the city in that only people who are otherwise systematically healthy as it necessitates only the virtuous people are treated, for virtues lead to health.
4) ***The criteria for a ruler***. The ruler is defined as the best of the guardians and so all the arguments regarding the education of soldiers is inherited in the education of rulers. The ruler has the 3rd requirement other than music and gymnastics: they need an unwavering conviction to protect the ideals of the city.
5) ***The noble lie to maintain order in the city.*** We tell the citizens of the city a lie that we are destined for our respective positions in the city by the innate value, represented by metals, that we are born with.
6) ***Additional safeguards to prevent soldiers from turning on the city.*** We provide soldiers with the bare necessities in line with our values of moderation as an additional safeguard on top of the noble lie and their education to prevent them from turning on the city

8) Socrates summarizes: we've stated how speeches about gods, demons, heroes and Hades' domain should be spoke about. Now we talk about the speeches made about the less divine: humans: 
	1) Speeches should be in praise of justice not about the happiness of unjust men. This addresses Adeimantus' argument that justice can only be instilled through its transactional value and is a fraudulent celebration of reputation. Socrates lays the blame on the storytellers and that they are wrong to tell such tales because it harms the city
9) We conclude on the contents of speeches, now we concern ourselves with how speeches must be made.  Socrates clarifies this for Adeimantus using Homer's Illiad as an example showing how his storytelling is an impersonation as if he was actually at the event rather than a factual retelling. This is the imitative speech we will address and whether it should be permitted. Adeimantus surmises we're basically testing whether comedy and tragedy should be permitted.
10) Socrates tests where the argument goes. His line of questioning leads to the censorship of imitative speech as it impedes one's craft:
	1) We continue with our assumption that people can only be good at something when they focus on one thing at a time. 
	2) We equivocate it with imitation. We are only good at imitating one thing at a time. Hard for comedy writers to be a tragedy writer.
	3) We said guardianship is a skilled craft and to be a good guardian, you must focus on this one thing. Therefore imitation will interfere with their training.
	4) Socrates acknowledges the pedagogical value of imitation, but to preserve the first argument, they cannot imitate anything else other than what is proper for guarding. They cannot imitate other crafts.
	5) Socrates addresses the style of speech. He recounts soldiers will deliver reports as if they were the person if the deed is good, but won't do so for a shameful report.
	6) Socrates differentiates between two forms of storytelling. The narrative factual speech and the imitative speech that following his anecdotal evidence, common men are prone to do as nothing is unworthy to them.
	7) Socrates attributes modes and rhythm to imitative speech and that factual narration has is consistent with limited variety in modes and rhythm
	8) Socrates acknowledges imitative speech is more pleasing, but from this line of argument, it impedes one's craft and so must be banished. "We would send him to another city, with myrrh poured over his head and crowned with wool".
11) We are now done with speeches and tales in both content and style. Now we address music and melody. Socrates gets in the weeds about the various modes associated with the unwanted virtues in a guardian such as Lydian mode with lamentations, etc.. The end result is that most of music should be censored along with their respective instrument-makers.
12) In doing all this. We have purged a good chunk of the luxury in our luxurious city.

15) By analogy to reading, we cannot read without recognizing indivudual letters, so we cannot be musical without the virtues that they must enforce.

# Propositions
- ***Art*** (music in this case) derives its value from the ideal of what is virtuous in the sense that they do nothing but enforce those presupposed values.
- Laws of the city:
	- On the argument of moderation as a virtue, love must be moderate as well to ensure we only have virtuous people in our city
	- Censorship of crafts and arts that do not extoll the virtues required for the upbringing of guardians.
- The prospering of doctors and lawyers is a symptom of a degenerate city
- Medicine should be practiced as a civic craft in service to the overall benefit of the city, not the individual.
