Plato is pressed by the brothers Adeimantus and Glaucon to argue for the values of justice, for its own sake, goods valued for their consequences, and goods valued for the hedonistic pleasures it offers. To begin his argument, Socrates begins the definition of a city and argues that searching for justice in the larger, we may find justice at the individual level as well

The book is outlined as follows:
1) Glaucon is not satisfied with Socrates' arguments against Thrasymachus and wants to hear from him more concretely about the value of justice when it does not appear to be in man's nature to honor it. Glaucon argues justice is merely a compromise between the individual benefit of the 
2) Adeimantus adds Glaucon's arguments stating that justice is instilled in with reasons that do not appear just:
	1) Parents teach children about justice by arguing for the material goods it will bring
	2) Poets and storytellers tell stories of how good people are punished and unjust people get away with their actions and live the good life
3) Adeimantus lays out the summary and ultimatum of the arguments:
	1) We must argue for the just person and unjust person who are perfect in their respective craft. By this definition, the unjust person will always appear just and gets away with it while the just person will appear unjust because he does not care for the honors attached
	2) We argue qua unjust person for no matter how hard it is, we follow what appears best and at this moment, it is injustice for the benefits it brings us.
4) Socrates searches for justice by finding it in the larger model of a city to find it at the individual level. He begins constructing the definition of a city
	1) A city is founded on the cooperation of people who's needs can only be satisfied by people skilled in their respective crafts. This is on the assumption that people are only skilled at one thing at a time hence the need for this cooperation.
	2) Glaucon interjects calling Socrates' initial definition of a city a "city of sow" that satisfies only the bare minimum requirements. 
	3) Socrates constructs a luxurious city and in doing so we require more resources. This gives rise to the need for guardians
	4) Based on our assumption that a person who dedicates himself to one craft is the most skilled, we also assume the art of war is not a trivial task,  hence guardians are dedicated people.
5) Socrates focuses on the requirements for the guardians:
	1) Guardians need opposite natures simultaneously: to be gentle towards their countrymen and harsh towards enemies. This contradiction is resolved by the existence of animals like dogs who behave this way so it must be possible in people too.
	2) The guardians need to be trained. They need to be trained in these two contradictory natures. Socrates proposes music and gymnastics to train these two conflicting natures
6) Socrates hones in on the education and argues for the censorship of the artists and the stories to raise the ideal guardians.
7) Socrates deduces that the only stories we can tell about god are unimitated, truthful, and can only depict the gods in the best light as it is their true nature on the assumption that the gods are perfect beings and any slight alteration or change will take away from their perfection

# Concepts
- Glaucon's definition of justice as a societal compromise
- Ring of Gyges thought experiment for the practicality of justice
- Definition of a city of sow
- Definition of a luxurious city resulting in the need for war and guardians
- The two features of perfectly good things (god in this case):
	1) Perfectly good things only cause other good things
	2) They are non-imitative in their presentation for any alteration would take away from it
- The need for censorship for the perfect education of guardians
