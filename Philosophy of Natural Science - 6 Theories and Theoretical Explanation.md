Continuing the discussion from [chapter 5](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%205%20Laws%20and%20Their%20Role%20in%20Scientific%20Explanation.md) on the outputs of scientific inquiry, we expand on types of explanations and look at theories and kind of explanations they offer as distinguished from empirical laws. The chapter is outlined as follows:
- The two characteristics of a theory
- What makes a good theory
- Hempel's counterarguments to the critics of the realism of abstract ideas of a theory
- Hempel's criticizes the need for theories to provide intuitive explanations and understanding

 As he has in previous chapters, Hempel begins with a comparison between accepted and good scientific theories and bad theories such as the neovitalistic doctrines to provide an intuitive understanding of a proper scientific theory. In his comparison, Hempel highlights that scientific theories are form of explanation and have the same necessary requirements, testability in principle and explanatory import. What distinguishes a scientific theory is how these requirements are manifested.
 - Internal principles: The abstract concepts that are invented to provide explanatory import, that is, they provide the deductive reason that a phenomena occurs. The internal principles are characterized by its abstract ideas couched in the positivists language theory in <u>theoretical terms</u> that are defined by <u>antecedently available terms</u> also referred to as <u>pretheoretical terms</u>.
 - Bridging Principles: The concepts that relate the internal principles and theoretical entities to quantities that can be observed or measured whether directly or indirectly. These principles provide the conditions for testability and explanatory import by relating it to the phenomena in question.
Regarding the bridging principles, Hempel stresses the in-principle testability as opposed to the practical testability. The bridging principles are not required to relate the theoretical entities to direct observables but can be indirect observables such as Bohr's theory with the bridging principles in wavelengths that are indirectly observed in the emission spectrum. In a sense, theories are higher than laws in the explanatory hierarchy in the sense that they explain empirical laws through bridging principles

Internal and bridging principles are necessary requirements built on the principles of a good scientific explanation, but they are not sufficient. Testability and explanatory import give a theory explanatory power, but to distinguish them as good theories of scientific interest they also require the following:
1) The theory has a special relationship to other pre-established laws and explanations: it provides a framework from which the relevant laws and explanations can be deduced. In essence, the theory unifies the explanations to the same underlying processes described in its internal principles. Hempel here echoes the modern [Unificationism](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2013%20Explanation.md) view of explanation.
2) Furthermore on the theory's relationship to pre-existing laws and explanations, on top of unifying them, the theory does not trivially deduce the pre-existing explanations and they are instead special cases of the internal principles. The theory explains other explanations and offers more possibilities beyond the special cases
3) From 1) and 2), we require a theory to fit with existing ideas by providing a framework from which they can be deduced non-trivially leaving room for other cases not yet explored. From these unexplored cases, we have the final requirement of a theory to go beyond existing laws and explanations in providing other cases explained by the internal principles that have not been explored. In other words, the theory must provide predictive power and guide us to look in places we did not think to look before

The internal principles of a theory are couched in abstract concepts and theoretical terms that are unintuitive and the literal meaning and significance of abstract theoretical concepts leads to arguments against the existence of theoretical concepts. Without directly referencing scientific realism, Hempel criticizes the arguments made against the literal existence of theoretical concepts by summarizing their arguments and providing counterarguments as follows:
1) The first argument imposes a criteria of meaningfulness that theories do not meet. For concept to have meaning, it must be fully defined in terms of concepts that are already available namely the concepts must be fully defined in terms of pre-theoretical terms. Here Hempel alludes to the strict logical positivist criteria of verification by a sentences ability to reduce to empirical senses that are directly verifiable. The strict logical positivist's end goal is to reduce all concepts to that of senses, and so, theoretical abstract concepts are nothing more than symbolic computations waiting to be reduced.  Hempel argues that this requirement is overly stringent and that the verification criteria can has been met with an appropriate bridging principle. Hempel effectively flips the end goal where instead of reducing theoretical concepts to antecedently available terms to have meaning, they instead meet the verification criteria by being testable. If a concept is testable it is  therefore verifiable and has meaning by itself without needing to be fully reduced. The strict logical positivist sets themselves up for a goal that has already been met.
2) The second argument against the existence of theoretical is a practical one. There are an infinite number of possible explanations for a phenomena and so it would be fruitless to find the correct one. Hempel does not directly argue against impossibility of finding the one correct theory and instead proposes that progress can be made if we change the end goal. Instead of finding the one correct theory, we can instead narrow the field and determine what explanations are not correct. Here Hempel touches on PGS's argument for [confirmation by eliminative inference](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2014%20Bayesianism%20and%20Modern%20Theories%20of%20Evidence.md). The inability to determine the one correct theory does not mean progress cannot be made.
3) The third argument is the weakest to me. Critics argue that explanation of observable phenomena should themselves be observable. This seems to be nothing more than a heuristic, like instrumentalism, that has no force behind it other than "its intuitive". Here the arguments for 2) in eliminative inferencing applies, but a more convincing argument by Hempel is the blurry line that distinguishes observable from unobservables. 

The final point Hempel argues against is the need for scientific theories to reduce phenomena and ideas to familiar concepts. Hempel views this as a self-defeating objective, for if everything can be reduced to a familiar intuitive explanation then there is no need for an explanation to begin with. Hempel's argues that the point of scientific theory in explaining phenomena is to allow us to grapple with the unintuitive. "What scientific explanation, especially theoretical explanation, aims at is not this intuitive highly subjective kind of understanding, but an objective kind of insight that is achieved by a systematic unification, by exhibiting the phenomena as manifestations of common underlying structures and processes that conform to specific, testable basic principles" (p.83). Hempel justifies this with historical examples in Einstein's theory of relativity and quantum mechanics that further our understanding with the unintuitive and unfamiliar notions of mass, time, and determinism.
