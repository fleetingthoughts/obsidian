Characteristics of a theory:
- 5.2: Internal Principles (this gives explanatory import?) and bridging principles to make it testable at least in principle
- 5.3: Characteristics of a good theory further developed from cHapter 4:
	- Unified account (unificationism)
	- Soft characteristic: relation by being a superset of previously established laws
	- Predictive power. Tell us wheere to look next
	- p.77 Hempel allows for the science to have ideas that are not couched in direct observables. Hence the internal principles?
- Hempel acknowledges success in going beyond direct observables and criticizes the denial of the existence of these "theoretical entities" (See eddington)
- What distinguishes theories from those who wish to couch hypotheses in direct observables is the internal principles. The bridging principles align with direct observables, that is their job, but the internal principles are the generating principles to create these bridging principles
- Hempel criticizes 3 views of those diametrically opposed:
	- Requirement of full definitions strictly in terms of antecedently available concepts to have any real meaning (this hints at verificationism). Hempel justifies by examples that bridging principles offer sufficient characterization. The structure of a theory lends itself to empircal import therefore it is verifiable
	- The possibility of serval laws. Here Hempel echoes PGS's eliminative inference. We may not be able to find THE  correct theory, but we can know the wrong ones.
	- Science as a goal is to explain things that we can perceive, so it does not make sense to go beyond the perceivable. Hempel's criticism is a practical one: we have less predictive power if we stick strictly with the empircal. Here Hempel criticizes the view on the ambiguity of what distinguishes observable from unobservable.
- The last thing Hempel notes as an aside is a criticism of the view that theories reduce complex seemingly unrelated phenomena to something very familiar and understandable. He claims the actual goal is a systematic unification (unficationism?) by showing the underlying internal principles that account for observable phenomena. To provide such a unified experiment does not require it to "feel familiar",. In fact it can be very unintuitive./
