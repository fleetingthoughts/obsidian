## Answer Keys & Explanations

## Day 1 Key

1. A — Socrates uses the weapon example to show that absolute rules like "always return what you owe" fail in complex moral realities.
2. B — The dialogue begins with Socrates walking down to the Piraeus (the port of Athens) and being pulled into Cephalus' home.
3. A — Cephalus steps out to tend to a sacrifice, handing the philosophical inheritance of the argument over to his son.
4. C — Plato uses vivid beast-like imagery to convey Thrasymachus' aggressive, hostile rhetorical style.
5. B — These three individuals are the historical figures who signed the formal indictment against him.
6. C — Meletus represented the poets, Anytus the craftsmen/politicians, and Lycon the rhetoricians.
7. B — Crito's wealth and frequent visits allowed him to build a familiar, bribable relationship with the guard.

## Day 2 Key

1. A — They want him to look past simple rewards and show that justice is inherently healthy for the internal soul.
2. B — Gyges is explicitly described as a humble shepherd serving the Lydian crown before a storm changes his life.
3. A — An earthquake splits the ground, and inside the opening, he finds a hollow bronze horse containing a giant corpse wearing the ring.
4. A — Glaucon mocks this simple arrangement as a "city of pigs" because it has no luxury, fine food, or entertainment.
5. B — Socrates fears the decades of rumors because they prejudiced the jury box long before he could defend himself.
6. B — Aristophanes' _The Clouds_ depicted Socrates floating in a basket, studying weather patterns, and charging money to make bad arguments look good.
7. A — Executions were banned while the sacred ship was away on its mission; its return meant his death sentence would happen the next day.

## Day 3 Key

1. B — Introducing fine lifestyle items creates a demand for land and resources, which directly introduces warfare and the need for a professional army.
2. C — Emotional outbursts break down the inner stoicism and rational discipline required to defend the city.
3. B — Music/poetry forms the inner character of the mind, while gymnastics builds the physical vessel that houses it.
4. A — Socrates uses basic cross-examination to show Meletus cannot claim he introduces _new_ gods while simultaneously claiming he believes in _zero_ gods.
5. B — Socrates lists these three real military campaigns where he stood his ground to show he respects duty over death.
6. A — Crito is deeply worried that the public will view his friend group as cheap cowards who valued their wallets over Socrates' life.
7. C — The entire conversation is an intimate dialogue held inside his stone jail cell before dawn.

## Day 4 Key

1. A — The myth tricks citizens into believing they are literal siblings born from the local soil, fostering fierce patriotism.
2. B — Gold is assigned to the rulers; silver is assigned to the auxiliary soldiers; bronze/iron belongs to the farmers and craftsmen.
3. B — Eliminating private property prevents the military class from turning into domestic predators against their own citizens.
4. B — Chaerephon’s question to the Pythian oracle is the historical spark that forced Socrates to start testing public figures.
5. B — The voice is purely prohibitive; it steps in to stop him from bad paths but never explicitly dictates positive commands.
6. C — Thessaly is targeted because Crito has wealthy friends there who can provide food, lodging, and physical security.
7. C — Socrates notes that you don't ask the public how to heal a broken bone; you ask a doctor. The same applies to the soul.

## Day 5 Key

1. B — A decadent, undisciplined populace wrecks its health (needing doctors) and constantly fights over property (needing lawyers).
2. B — They are monitored closely from childhood to ensure they do not forget their duty under pressure, pain, or deceptive allurements.
3. B — The state is a strict meritocracy based on soul composition; family lineages do not guarantee class status.
4. C — The craftsmen actually knew how to build real things, but because of that skill, they falsely believed they knew everything about morality and politics too.
5. B — Socrates claims his public service deserves the highest civic honor instead of a criminal penalty.
6. A — The Laws point out he was such a homebody that he practically never left the city limits, proving his absolute satisfaction with Athenian life.
7. A — Fleeing proves he treats laws with contempt, which would make the jury say, "See? We were right about him corrupting people."

## Day 6 Key

1. A — Socrates proves that a true professional (like a doctor or shepherd) seeks the welfare of the subject, not self-enrichment.
2. A — Adeimantus argues that children are told to be good only so they can land good jobs, secure marriages, and please the gods.
3. B — Simple, stable, martial melodies build an internal psychic harmony; complex arrangements cause psychological chaos.
4. B — The official text of the charge accused him of failing to worship the state gods, bringing in new spirits, and corrupting young minds.
5. C — Socrates notes that if a mere 30 votes had swung the other way, he would have walked out of the courtroom a free man.
6. B — Running away harms everyone involved by breaking a binding legal promise, destroying his integrity, and making his friends look like criminals.
7. C — The voice of civic duty and his agreement with the city screams so loudly in his mind that Crito’s practical escape plans are completely drowned out.
