PGS summarizes the views held by the following people in criticism of scientific realism:
- Bruno Latour
- Nelson Goodman
- Laudan
- Feyerabend
- Thomas Kuhn (to varying degrees. His position is hard to pin down)
The views presented by these people can be described as "metaphysical constructivism": the idea that we cannot know anything about an independent reality because reality is dependent on what people say and think. In other words, we construct our own reality. While there are varying degrees of this belief such as with Kuhn, Goodman has insisted his claims were not metaphorical and he believes Science constructs its own reality. PGS does not offer any particular criticism in Chapter 12 when he discusses this idea, but he makes his opposition to their views clear. 

PGS illustrates the position held by these people through a meta-analysis done by Michael Devitt on these debates. These ideas appear to be a version of Kant's noumenal world (the world we hope to know something about that is independent of us) and the phenomenal world (the world we can only know about but that is partly our creation and does not exist separately of our minds). The major difference with Kant is that the constructivists in opposition to realism incorporate a relativism  where different communities impose their own reality depending on their corresponding social influences (see the views held by sociologists in [Theory and Reality - 8 The Challenge from Sociology of Science](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%208%20The%20Challenge%20from%20Sociology%20of%20Science.md)). This is in contrast to Kant who held that all humans held the same universal framework to interpret the phenomenal world (this may be linked to his synthetic a priori).

While PGS does not criticize these views directly, he does criticize how they were formulated and the general context from where these views came from. He notes that appeals to Kant's ideas is superfluous. It is simple enough to just say the independent reality is hard to interpret without introducing a layer called the "phenomenal world" that impedes us. This is just adding terms and concepts for its own sake. Secondly, while PGS acknowledges there are more moderate sociologists and academics from Science Studies who hold a more moderate view that does accept a form of realism but that our social influences inhibit us from obtaining it, PGS does wonder why these views are so prevalent in Science Studies and social science. He notes that there is a tendency for sociologists to avoid what PGS calls the<u> "Bad View" of Science that holds that reality stamps itself on the passive mind</u>. The Bad view apparently has some negative consequences socially and politically so this is why sociologists like to intrude into science to stamp out this view and going to the extreme with antirealist views. I'm not too clear on what this bad view is and it sounds like a contextual thing that requires a meta-analysis of sociologists' works to see the shape of their arguments with respect to politics, but it sounds like PGS is hinting that these people have a political agenda.
