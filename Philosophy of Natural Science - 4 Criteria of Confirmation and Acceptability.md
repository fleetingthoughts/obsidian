[Chapter 2](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%202%20Scientific%20Inquiry,%20Invention,%20and%20Test.md) proposed that science is characterized by a "criteria of sound arguments" and so in this chapter, we look at <u>a criteria required to test the soundness of a hypothesis that leads to acceptance</u>. While not explicitly stated by Hempel, this criteria is characterized by a larger scale testing. Tests by themselves have practical limitations and cannot offer deductive support as noted in [chapter 3](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%203%20The%20Test%20of%20a%20Hypothesis.%20Its%20Logic%20and%20Its%20Force.md). Hempel instead proposes that testing offers a different kind of support called "confirmation" which cannot be a deductive relation between statements with the results from the individual tests referred to as "evidence". In this chapter, Hempel outlines the "soundness of argument criteria" required of a test and hypotheses to obtain the end goal of confirmation leading to acceptance:
- The criteria for the testing of a hypothesis, and the soft requirements of predictive power that have a timing element
- The criteria for a hypothesis: how it fits with other existing hypothesis
- The criteria for a hypothesis: the idea of simplicity. The lack of a hard criteria for simplicity and is instead characterized by what it is NOT.
- The proposed view of characterizing confirmation by probabilities and what the view lacked during Hempel's time.

A test that confirms a hypothesis is characterized by the following:
- The quantity of evidence (i.e. amount of testing done)
- The diversity of the testing where diversity is characterized by the number of different variables accounted for.
The justification for these criteria is to provide rigor by casting a wide net search of the possibilities that can discredit the hypothesis. Testing in a sense, is the search for a counterexample like in math.



- The psychological strength of a hypothesis having predictive power in explaining future data. Hempel notes an inherent paradox in this tendency by noting that logically, whether the evidence came before or after the hypothesis should have no bearing on validating the hypothesis.
- A justification for predictive power is simplicity.
