[Chapter 2](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%202%20Scientific%20Inquiry,%20Invention,%20and%20Test.md) proposed that science is characterized by a "criteria of sound arguments" and in this chapter, we look at <u>a criteria required to test the soundness of a hypothesis that leads to acceptance</u>. While not explicitly stated by Hempel, this criteria is characterized by a larger scale testing. Tests by themselves have practical limitations and cannot offer deductive support as noted in [chapter 3](Philosophy%20of%20Natural%20Science%20-%203%20The%20Test%20of%20a%20Hypothesis.%20Its%20Logic%20and%20Its%20Force.md). Hempel instead proposes that testing offers a different kind of support called "confirmation" which cannot be a deductive relation between statements with the results from the individual tests referred to as "evidence". In this chapter, Hempel outlines the "soundness of argument criteria" required of a test and hypotheses to obtain the end goal of confirmation leading to acceptance:
- The criteria for the testing of a hypothesis, and the soft requirements of predictive power that have a timing element
- The criteria for a hypothesis: how it fits with other existing hypothesis
- The criteria for a hypothesis: the idea of simplicity. The lack of a hard criteria for simplicity and is instead characterized by what it is NOT.
- The proposed view of characterizing confirmation by probabilities and what the view lacked during Hempel's time.

A test that confirms a hypothesis is characterized by the following:
- The quantity of evidence (i.e. amount of testing done)
- The diversity of the testing where diversity is characterized by the number of different variables accounted for.
The justification for these criteria is to provide rigor by casting a wide net search of the possibilities that can discredit the hypothesis. Testing in a sense, is the search for a counterexample like in math.

A soft requirement of testing that Hempel notes is the "timing of the hypotheses". The justification for this is largely descriptive and historical where in cases like Balmer's formula for the hydrogen emission line, hypotheses that is later confirmed by ***future new evidence*** have more weight as opposed to hypotheses that are formulated on solely past data. Hempel recognizes that this has no deductive merit as deductive arguments have no dimension in time, hence why it is considered a soft subjective requirement. The essence here is that hypotheses need to have a kind of predictive power, and a tentative justification for why ***future new evidence*** better confirms a theory is requirement of simplicity (discussed later), where a hypotheses that took fewer parameters to formulate to explain a larger data set is better than a hypotheses that took more past data inputs to explain the same data set.


With regards to hypotheses, Hempel recognized two characteristics that allow hypotheses to lend themselves better to confirmation:
1) The new hypotheses' relationship to existing ones: a hypotheses that can be deduced from an existing hypotheses that is already accepted will be better corroborated
2) The hypotheses must be considered simple compared to competing hypotheses

But with each of these, Hempel acknowledges limitations with these two criteria. They are as follows respectively:
1) While a hypotheses can be confirmed or disconfirmed by an existing accepted hypotheses that it can be deduced from, this must be balanced by the the ability for the new hypotheses to be acknowledged as adverse evidence that overthrows the existing hypotheses. A hypotheses must also have the ability to be confirmed and accepted ***in spite of*** existing theories
2) There is no firm criteria for simplicity and is only applicable when "choosing between descriptions of the one and same set of facts". But in the more general case of choosing between several different theories that originate from different set of facts "we also adopt the predictions it implies for yet untested cases." While simplicity is appealing to describe existing facts, the uncertainty of future data sets (i.e. the predictive power of a hypotheses), makes the criteria of simplicity hard to define
With respect to 2), Hempel outlines some proposals for simplicity and offers criticism for why they cannot be a standard for simplicity, but highlights the appealing elements in their views:
- Simplicity defined by the number of assumptions, or in terms of data, by the number of parameters used to describe it. This definition is untenable as the way to categorize the assumptions can be unambiguously categorized such that 1 assumption can really be 5 assumptions. In the case of data for example, a curve may be considered less simple than a line, but it is not the case in polar coordinates.
- Reichenbach nevertheless pushes for simplicity defined by the fewest assumptions with justification by a mathematical algorithm: if we continuously make the simplest assumptions and aggregate them, then the functions will converge eventually to the true function $y=f(x)$. Hempel criticizes this claim noting that this assumes $y=f(x)$ is indeed the true relationship otherwise convergence will never occur and we have no way of determining how fast this convergence should be to know when to abandon a hypotheses.
- Popper proposes simplicity as defined by what lends themselves better to falsification. In this sense, simplicity are the most general statements and therefore makes more prediction and testing implications to be falsified. Simple statements are those that "tell us more; because their empirical content is greater; and because they are better testable (p. 44)." Unintuitively, this means simplicity is defined by the ***more content***. As an example, in comparing two statements $A$ and $B$, if $A \implies B$, then $B \subseteq A$ and $A$ is considered the simpler statement because it predicts more and includes all the predictions that $B$ already makes. To Hempel, this is untenable by itself as this would imply the simple conjoining of statements (e.g. if $A$ and $B$ are competing statements then we should simply test $A \lor B$ which is "simpler" by Popper's criteria) would lead to a better hypotheses. We could even conjoin two unrelated hypotheses like Boyle's law and Snell's law together which would result in a Popperian simple hypotheses because it has more predictive power than either hypotheses by themselves.
All these points are to note that simplicity is a subjectively appealing quality for a hypotheses but we have no firm criteria for it.

Hempel also looks at a criteria of soundness and the idea of confirmation described by probabilities. The idea is to determine a function $c$ that outputs a quantifiable amount of confirmation given a hypotheses $H$  and evidence $K$, that is, we find a function form for $c(H,K)$. Modern Bayesian confirmation theories follows this model, but as of Hempel's time, the two current unsolved problems were the following with tentative solutions are provided by Bayesianism in parentheses:
- The function $c(H,K)$ depends on other probabilities being known. (Bayesianism proposes a solution in the subjectivist interpretation of probabilities that defines the a priori probabilities)
- There is no general definition of the probability of a hypotheses relative to given information. (Bayesianism proposes a formula in Bayes' Theorem).
