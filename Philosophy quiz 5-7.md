## 📅 Day 5 Master Key

## 1. Methodology of Scientific Research Programs

- Structural Mechanics: Imre Lakatos structures science into competing _Research Programs_ (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
    
    - The Hard Core: The central, defining theoretical axioms of the program. By programmatic decision (the _negative heuristic_), the hard core is rendered completely irrefutable and protected from falsification (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
    - The Protective Belt: A vast arrangement of auxiliary hypotheses, mathematical techniques, and operational assumptions surrounding the hard core. The _positive heuristic_ directs anomalous data to this belt, where modifications absorb shocks via custom adjustments to save the core (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
    
- Evaluation Criteria: A research program is progressive if it possesses both theoretical progress (predicting entirely novel, unexpected facts) and empirical progress (some of these novel predictions are verified). It is degenerative if it stalls, falls behind data, and merely generates post-hoc, defensive adjustments to handle anomalies after they occur (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 2. Research Programs vs. Research Traditions

- Laudan's Modifications: Larry Laudan replaces research programs with _Research Traditions_ (Godfrey-Smith 2003). He strips out Lakatos's rigid mechanics: (1) Theories within a tradition can be structurally modified, swapped out, or integrated over time. (2) The elements of the hard core are not frozen forever; they can be revised or abandoned if the tradition warrants it (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- Solving Historical Problems: Lakatos fell into historical trouble because a degenerative program could theoretically "bounce back" centuries later, making it impossible to rationally declare a program dead. Laudan solves this by disconnecting progress from absolute truth, defining it as problem-solving capacity. Progress is calculated by evaluating the number and weight of empirical and conceptual problems a tradition solves over time, allowing for contextual rational evaluation (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 3. Feyerabend's Anarchistic Principle

The only structural principle that does not inhibit historical progress is: "anything goes" (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 4. Epistemological Anarchism

- The Epistemological Thesis: Science is an inherently unpredictable, creative process with no single, permanent "scientific method." Any rigid methodological rule imposed on science will, historically, be shown to have impeded its progress (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Galileo Argument: Feyerabend uses Galileo's defense of Copernicanism to demonstrate this. In 1600, Copernicanism was heavily falsified by the available physics of motion and data. To win the argument, Galileo did not follow a neutral method; he deployed clever rhetoric, propaganda written in Italian rather than academic Latin, and introduced unverified _ad hoc_ hypotheses about telescopic optics to trick his peers into accepting a moving earth. If Galileo had followed the strict, textbook rules of logical empiricism or falsificationism, he would have rationally rejected Copernicanism, halting physics (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 5. Analytical Critique of Theology

The sentence "God exists" purports to express a genuine statement of fact, but it describes a transcendent entity completely removed from possible sensory observation. Because the assumption of God's existence cannot yield conditional test implications, it is completely untestable in principle. Within a strict verificationist framework, the claim is not false, and it is not agnostic (which implies a real question exists whose answer is unknown); it is literally senseless nonsense devoid of factual meaning (Ayer 1936).

## 6. Mertonian Sociology vs. The Strong Program

- Mertonian Sociology ("Old"): Focuses on the social structure and _institutional norms_ of science. It analyzes how the community polices its researchers but leaves the actual content of scientific theories to logicians, assuming that evidence alone accounts for scientific truth (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Strong Program ("New"): Focuses directly on the _social causes of scientific beliefs_. It asserts that scientific concepts and natural facts are socially negotiated products (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Symmetry Principle: Mandates that the sociologist evaluate true and false beliefs symmetrically. Instead of using psychological irrationality or political bias to explain scientific errors while letting "evidence" explain scientific truths, the sociologist must show that _both_ accepted facts and rejected myths are caused by identical types of social factors, network alliances, and institutional variables (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 7. Mustachioed Phenomenalist

The Austrian physicist/philosopher was Ernst Mach (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 8. The Symmetry Principle Definition

The Symmetry Principle requires that the sociologist use the exact same types of social, psychological, and institutional causes to explain both successfully accepted scientific truths and discarded scientific errors, treating all knowledge claims impartially (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 9. 1903 Photoelectric Effect Crucial Test

- The Case: Philipp Lenard's 1903 experiment showed that the energy of electrons emitted from a metal plate irradiated with light depended strictly on the light's wavelength (color), not its intensity (brightness) (Hempel 1966).
- The Challenge: On classical wave theory, continuous wave fronts should transfer cumulative energy, meaning high intensity should produce high-energy electron emissions, which was flatly falsified. Einstein used this as a practical crucial test to show light consists of discrete packets (quanta/photons) colliding with electrons (Hempel 1966).
- Why Not Strictly Refuted: The wave theory was not strictly destroyed because investigators could always adjust internal auxiliary laws regarding how subatomic matter absorbs wave energy, proving that complex systems are modified rather than cleanly annihilated by a single test (Hempel 1966).

## 10. Analytical Problem of Perception

- The Problem: Traditional representative and causal theories of perception argue that our minds only directly experience inner representations ("ideas"), forcing a skeptical gap over whether an unobservable material world actually causes those ideas (Ayer 1936).
- The Analytic Resolution: By defining a material object purely as a logical construction out of immediate sense-contents, the hidden unobservable world is discarded. Saying a material object exists is analytically equivalent to asserting that specific families of sense-contents are obtainable under certain behavioral conditions. Because existence is tied directly to sensory verification rules, the representative-causal gap is dissolved _a priori_ (Ayer 1936).

## 11. Radical Theory Change Problem

If the history of science is a graveyard of heavily successful but ultimately falsified worldviews (e.g., phlogiston or the mechanical ether), it is highly probable that our own current successful theories are also fundamentally false. This problem threatens scientific realism by showing that empirical success is decoupled from ontological truth, forcing us to reconsider if science accumulates true descriptions of unobservables or merely predictive adequacy (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 12. Minimalist Analysis of "True" and "False"

The words "true" and "false" do not denote real physical or metaphysical properties of sentences, nor do they require a complex correspondence theory of reality. They operate strictly as formal linguistic signs of assertion and negation. Asserting "It is true that $p$" is completely equivalent to simply asserting "$p$," and writing "It is false that $p$" is identical to writing "Not $p$" (Ayer 1936).

## 13. Sokal Hoax Mechanics

Alan Sokal published a parodic article loaded with trendy but nonsensical post-modern terminology in a cultural studies journal, then unmasked it as a hoax. This clash exposed an absolute lack of cognitive or logical filters within radical social constructivism, triggering the Science Wars over whether science tracks an objective reality or merely negotiates social power structures (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 14. Propositions Referring to the Remote Past

Logical empiricists defended claims about the remote past under weak verificationism by proving they are not meaningless. To state a historical claim (e.g., "Caesar crossed the Rubicon") is to construct a rule for the anticipation of future experience. The claim is factually significant because it deductively implies that if an investigator digs in a specific geographic parameter or translates a newly discovered ancient codex, certain sensory sense-contents will occur (Ayer 1936).

## 📅 Day 2 Master Key

## 1. Scientific Realism Dimensions

Scientific Realism is the philosophical commitment that:

1. Metaphysical: There exists a real, objective physical world completely independent of human conceptualization, belief, or linguistic frameworks (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
2. Semantic: Scientific theories concerning both observable and unobservable entities are to be interpreted literally as statements with true-or-false value, not as mere calculation tools or instrumental symbols (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
3. Epistemological: Human beings are capable of gaining real, reliable knowledge about the hidden unobservable structures described by our best confirmed scientific theories (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 2. Scientific Realism vs. Constructive Empiricism

- The Core Conflict: Bas van Fraassen rejects the realism requirement regarding unobservables (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Criteria: Realism asserts the goal of science is absolute literal truth. Constructive Empiricism asserts the goal of science is empirical adequacy—a theory is acceptable if it gives a completely true description of all _observable_ phenomena (past, present, and future) (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Line of Demarcation: Constructive empiricism draws a strict line at the boundaries of human physiology. Entities that are directly observable by an unassisted human eye (like an airplane or a dinosaur skeleton) have real ontological status. Entities that require an upstream causal instrument to be detected (like an electron or a DNA strand viewed via electron microscope) are treated instrumentally; we care about the empirical adequacy of their mathematical outputs, not their literal existence (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 3. Pragmatic Alternative Property

The property is empirical adequacy (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 4. Operational Definition Limitations

- Definition: An operational definition defines a scientific concept entirely in terms of a specific, repeatable measurement operation (e.g., defining "length" as the specific physical operation of laying a meter stick end-to-end) (Hempel 1966).
- Systematic Limitations: This approach creates a fatal fragmentation of language. If a concept is tied _exclusively_ to one operation, then measuring length via a laser finder instead of a wooden meter stick creates a completely new, separate scientific term. It strips concepts of their systematic import by destroying our ability to construct generalized universal laws across diverse experimental setups (Hempel 1966).

## 5. Operationally Meaningless Definition

A term is _operationally meaningless_ if it cannot be attached to a specific, repeatable set of experimental operations or testing procedures that dictate when it applies (Hempel 1966).

## 6. Miracle Argument vs. Pessimistic Meta-Induction

- The Miracle Argument: The primary defense of realism. It notes that our advanced scientific theories possess spectacular predictive success. If these theories were false, this success would be an absolute, inexplicable miracle. Therefore, theories must be true (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- Pessimistic Meta-Induction: The devastating realist counter-attack. It points to the history of science: fields are littered with past theories (caloric, phlogiston, ether) that were phenomenally successful in their era but turned out to be completely false. By induction, our current successful theories are likely false, neutralizing the miracle argument (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 7. Theoretical Reduction Conditions

To reduce a secondary discipline (e.g., biology) to a primary discipline (e.g., chemistry/physics), two formal conditions must be satisfied (Hempel 1966):

1. Condition of Connectability: Every theoretical term of the secondary science must be connected to terms of the primary science via custom "rules of correspondence" (bridge laws) (Hempel 1966).
2. Condition of Derivability: All the general laws and universal principles of the secondary science must be deductively derivable from the fundamental laws of the primary science (Hempel 1966).

## 8. Discarded Vitalistic Force

The force was the entelechy (Hempel 1966).

## 9. Internal Principles vs. Bridge Principles

- Internal Principles: Axioms that define the baseline characteristics of a theory's hidden entities (e.g., stating how electrons behave inside an electromagnetic field) (Hempel 1966).
- Bridge Principles: Sentences that map those unobservable entities directly to observable phenomena (e.g., linking electronic drops to visible flashes on a fluorescent screen) (Hempel 1966).
- Systematic Impotence: Without bridge principles, internal principles remain trapped in a closed, circular loop of syntax. The theory becomes completely untestable, generates zero test implications, and lacks empirical import, devolving into an unempirical pseudo-theory (Hempel 1966).

## 10. Mechanism-Vitalism issue

- The Neo-Vitalist Case: Vitalists argued that living organisms cannot be reduced to physical-chemical systems because biological development is guided by an immaterial, purposeful force called an _entelechy_ (Hempel 1966).
- The Logical Critique: Hempel proves that the concept of an entelechy is operationally meaningless. Vitalists provided no tracking criteria or measurement operations to determine how much entelechy an organism possessed or when it shifted. Because it generates zero independent test implications, vitalism provides a fake explanation that says nothing (Hempel 1966).

## 11. Reduction of the "Self"

The concept of a substantial, enduring "Ego" or soul is a fictitious entity born from grammatical confusion—we assume that because a single personal pronoun ("I") is the subject of multiple sentences, there must be a single fixed object matching it. Logical analysis shows the self is a logical construction. It is nothing over and above a specific, tracking framework of sequentially ordered sense-experiences (sense-history) linked by memory (Ayer 1936).

## 12. Metaphysical Constructivism & Ramses II

- The Thesis: Metaphysical constructivism claims that reality itself is fabricated and stabilized entirely by social scientific practice; nature does not exist prior to its scientific construction (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Ramses II Case: Bruno Latour claimed that it is an anachronistic error to assert that Ramses II died of tuberculosis in ancient Egypt, because the _Koch bacillus_ was only discovered and socially stabilized in 1882. Realists reject this as an absurd collapse of the boundary between a discovery and the entity itself, showing that constructivism confuses our knowledge of a fact with the independent existence of the fact (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 13. Bundle of Sensations Self

This viewpoint is a Humean phenomenalist approach to personal identity (Ayer 1936).

## 14. Reduction to the Familiar Critique

Many thinkers assume that a valid scientific explanation must make an unfamiliar phenomenon look "familiar." This is false. A D-N explanation often explains a familiar event (like an apple falling) by subsuming it under a highly abstract, mathematical, and completely unfamiliar set of laws (like gravitation and general relativity). Valid explanations prioritize systematic import and predictive power over psychological comfort (Hempel 1966).

## 📅 Day 3 Master Key

## 1. Philosophical Naturalism Definition

Philosophical Naturalism is the epistemological and methodological commitment that philosophy does not stand above or separate from the empirical sciences as an _a priori_ foundation. Philosophy is continuous with science, and philosophical questions must be investigated using identical types of empirical tools, evolutionary frameworks, and tracking data as natural science (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 2. Why Phenomenalism Does Not Collapse into Solipsism

- The Solipsistic Threat: If all knowledge is constructed out of _my_ immediate sense-contents, how can I ever know that other people have minds (Ayer 1936)?
- The Resolution: Ayer proves that "other minds" are also logical constructions. I speak meaningfully about another person's mind by translating their experiences into structural patterns of _my own actual or possible observations_ of their physical movements, verbal behavior, and biological expressions. Because other minds are defined entirely by these observable metrics, solipsism disappears (Ayer 1936).

## 3. Core Empiricist Slogan

The only source of real knowledge about the world is experience (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 4. Kitcher's Division of Scientific Labor

- The Problem: If a community is populated by purely altruistic researchers who only care about the absolute truth, they will all cluster on the single most probable hypothesis, leaving riskier but potentially revolutionary pathways unexplored (Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Naturalistic Solution: By designing an institutional reward system based on individual credit and status competition, a community populated by egoistic researchers will optimize cognitive diversity. When too many scientists crowd a single hypothesis, the individual payoff shrinks, incentivizing greedy researchers to hedge, distribute risk, and explore peripheral hypotheses, creating an efficient division of scientific labor (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 5. Analytic-Synthetic Distinction

- The Distinction: An analytic statement is true or false simply in virtue of its linguistic conventions and symbol meanings. A synthetic statement is true or false based on both its meaning and the actual empirical arrangement of the world (Ayer 1936, Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- Empiricist Safeguard: Traditional empiricism struggled to explain why mathematics is completely certain without needing experiential testing. Positivism solved this by proving that mathematics is purely analytic. Math doesn't describe the world; it merely records our conventional decision to use symbols in a particular way. Because math is an empty tautology, it can be known _a priori_ with absolute certainty without violating the core empiricist axiom that all factual knowledge derives from experience (Ayer 1936).

## 6. Analytic Proposition Definition

An analytic proposition is an empty tautology whose truth depends entirely on linguistic rules and conventional symbol relationships, containing zero factual content about reality (Ayer 1936).

## 7. Subjectivist Probability (Bayesianism) Mechanics

Bayesianism treats evidence using mathematical conditional probability. An investigator assigns a _prior probability_ $P(H)$ to a hypothesis based on subjective confidence. When new evidence ($E$) occurs, the prior probability is formally updated into a _posterior probability_ $P(H \mid E)$ via Bayes' Theorem (Godfrey-Smith 2003):  
$$P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H)P(H)}{P(E)}$$  
This formalizes confirmation by showing that evidence supports a theory if the likelihood ratio $P(E \mid H)$ is high while the probability of that evidence occurring by pure chance $P(E)$ is low (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 8. CUDOS Imperatives

The imperatives are Communism, Universalism, Disinterestedness, and Organized Skepticism (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 9. Pettenkofer-Koch Cholera Experiment

Max von Pettenkofer drank a glass of water loaded with live cholera bacteria isolated by Robert Koch to prove Koch's germ theory wrong. Pettenkofer suffered zero ill effects because his highly specific stomach acid parameters protected him, and he claimed he had refuted the theory. This historical case serves as an empirical warning that direct, real-world tests can easily yield devastating false negatives if an investigator overlooks hidden auxiliary variables (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 10. Critique of Foundationalist Projects

Descartes tried to build an unshakeable system of knowledge by using _cogito ergo sum_ as an indubitable first principle. Logical analysis proves this project is a failure. "I exist" does not follow deductively from "there is a thought now," because a single standalone moment of experience cannot entail that an enduring personal identity exists. If the _cogito_ is modified to be completely indubitable, it reduces to the empty linguistic tautology: "there is a thought now," from which absolutely no factual information about the physical universe can be deduced (Ayer 1936).

## 11. Purely Logical Inquiry Limitation

A purely logical inquiry studies only the formal consequences of language definitions. Because its operations are tautological rearrangements of symbols, it is a closed loop of syntax that cannot generate or establish a single factual claim about the empirical world (Ayer 1936).

## 12. Merton’s Institutional Imperatives

Merton’s four structural norms dictate how science operates as a social institution (Godfrey-Smith 2003):

1. Communism: Scientific findings are common property; secrets are banned to allow free spread of data.
2. Universalism: Knowledge claims must be evaluated using impersonal, objective criteria, completely ignoring the race, gender, or status of the investigator.
3. Disinterestedness: Scientists must act for the collective tracking of truth, avoiding financial or personal bias.
4. Organized Skepticism: The community must suspend judgment and rigorously test all claims, completely disregarding traditional authority (Godfrey-Smith 2003).

## 13. Procedural Naturalism Synthesis

- The Tension: Linguistic positivism (Hempel/Ayer) demands a strict, abstract mathematical logic of verification and translation, but ignores historical change. Thomas Kuhn tracks historical revolutions and paradigm psychology, but falls into relativistic shifts. The Sociology of Science claims that science is merely a social network negotiating power, discarding objective data (Ayer 1936, Hempel 1966, Godfrey-Smith 2003).
- The Resolution: Peter Godfrey-Smith’s _Procedural Naturalism_ resolves this paste. It accepts the historicist and sociological insight that science is a highly localized, social institution populated by credit-seeking agents working under paradigms. However, it preserves the empiricist core by demonstrating that the unique social structure of scientific communities (peer review, replication demands, institutional CUDOS norms) acts as a physical engine that keeps the community intensely responsive to sensory observation. Social cooperation and institutional competition are the precise mechanisms that allow science to accumulate true empirical tracking data of the mind-independent world (Ayer 1936, Hempel 1966, Godfrey-Smith 2003).
