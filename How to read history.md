Adler comes to terms with the audience defining his understanding of historical books and argues for it inductively as a valid way of writing history books and was indeed the most impactful way of writing history. Mortimer is largely concerned with history as a narrative that of course has to lay out the facts, but the prime difficulty is attributing the causes and motivations of the people and events involved. Adler admits that history is likely close to fiction than science in that sense. Conclusions on the cause and motivation are the most elusive and important part to understand historical books and this will be our guide to the rules and advice he provides

In reading a historical book, it is necessary to know what the author explains and what he doesn't explain about an event. The author may exclude the economic effects for example. The reader must know what limitations the author has set on himself. The understanding of motivations and causes is critical to then answer question 4) What of it, and so in this sense, historical books can be interpreted as practical books to guide our own lives

Adler provides a list of question aimed at current events (or current as of the author's writing), but I believe they are applicable for any historical books:
1. What is the author proving? History always has the author's own input necessarily because describing motivations and underlying causes is an interpretive matter
2. Who are they convincing. This lets you determine whether you want to read the book or not
3. What special knowledge do they assume
4. What special language do they use
5. Do they know what they're talking about
