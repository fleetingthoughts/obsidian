Thesis:
1) Our strategy so far: we've split things into analytic and synthetic definitions. The validity of analytic statements is defined by the symbols and we state to be true. We now look at criteria for empirical statement to be true
2) Answering traditional philosophical questions. What is truth? Under our method of philosophy as a use of analysis, the question of what is truth is an elliptic way of asking whether a proposition is true.
	1) The solution: truth is just a function in a sentence. Saying "x is true" is simply to state "x". 
	2) Why this question is there anyways: a misunderstanding due to gramamtical construction of our sentences putting truth as a noun therefore understanding it as a relation to some objective "substance" that actually exists out there outside our senses
	3) Truth is a function not a relation
3) Distinguish analytic from empirical criteria of validation. An analytic statement is false if it is self-contradictory. A empirical criteria can still be false if not self-contradictory so we need something different.
4) Ayer criticizes attempts to "pseudo-axiomatize" science by ostensive propositions. Propositions so certain that they serve as the "axioms" for empirical statements. Using ostensive to derive validity of other empirical statements is circular because any attempts to proposition ostensive properties will include references to past empirical experiences. Saying something is green is still referencing it against past "green" things you saw
