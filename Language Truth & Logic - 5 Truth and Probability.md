In chapter 4 we looked at the rules of validation of analytic statements by definition and tautology, we now look at the criteria to validate synthetic statements. We first discuss two things that our criteria should not deal and we propose that the criteria is by pragmatism: to validate synthetic statements is an eliminative process to select a hypothesis that <u>probabilistically fulfill</u>s its intended purpose in setting our expectations for what senses to experience in our intended context and limitations of our rationality to abide by this criteria.
1) Our strategy so far: we've split things into analytic and synthetic definitions. The validity of analytic statements is defined by the symbols and we state to be true. We now look at criteria for empirical statement to be true
2) Answering traditional philosophical questions. What is truth? Under our method of philosophy as a use of analysis, the question of what is truth is an elliptic way of asking whether a proposition is true.
	1) The solution: truth is just a function in a sentence. Saying "x is true" is simply to state "x". 
	2) Why this question is there anyways: a misunderstanding due to grammatical construction of our sentences putting truth as a noun therefore understanding it as a relation to some objective "substance" that actually exists out there outside our senses
	3) Truth is a function not a relation. MY OWN NOTES: Relation implies the existence of some other objective truth which is metaphysical which is the definitive reason for Ayer to reject truth as a relation.
	4) The problem of truth is to ask how propositions are validated.
3) Distinguish analytic from empirical criteria of validation. An analytic statement is false if it is self-contradictory. A empirical criteria can still be false if not self-contradictory so we need something different.
4) Ayer criticizes attempts to "pseudo-axiomatize" science by ostensive propositions that propose that the immediate sense experiences at the moment are so certain that all empirical statements can be deduced from it. Essentially they are axioms. Using ostensive to derive validity of other empirical statements is circular because any attempts to proposition ostensive properties will include references to past empirical experiences. Saying something is green is still referencing it against past "green" things you saw
5) Hume's mistake in conflating analytical statements with empirical ones. "All men are mortal" are used in the context of analytic tautologies, but then proposed as factual statements rendering them susceptible to the problem of induction
6) An expansion on chapter 1's weak and strong verification: the reason we cannot strongly verify is due to auxiliary hypotheses. Because we cannot directly verify, empirical validation must be an eliminative procedure.
7) Thesis: the criteria for empirical validation is the hypothesis that best fulfills its intended function. It is the hypothesis that most accurately sets up our expectations for what to sense in our given context. So synthetic propositions are characterized from each other by different situations or context. The criteria is therefore predictive power.
8) Because we cannot strongly verify a hypothesis, we can only look at the most probabilistic hypothesis that does so. We define probability and what it is not:
	1) Probability is not an intrinsic property or a logical relation with some "objective" substance. That strays into the metaphysical
	2) It is defined by "our willingness to rely on it in practice as a forecast of our sensations, and to retain it in the preference to other hypotheses in the face of an unfavorable outcome". This is similar to the subjectivist interpretation of probabilities as a gamble.
9) Define rationality as holding rational beliefs: beliefs that are arrived at by methods deemed reliable. This seems incredibly circular. 
10) The definition of probability in 7) is a personal one that takes into account our limited ability to be rational. Probability is characterized by the confidence a rational individual would act on it. This is very similar to the presumption of rationality by Bayesian models in confirmation.
11) Our criteria focused a lot on predictive power and the future, but Ayer thinks it applies to history and propositions about the past too. Its a very short paragraph...

# Terms
- Ostensive propositions. The most immediate at the moment sensations we experience that are deemed to be "logically certain" from which all synthetic statements can be deduced. It is an attempt to axiomatize empirical statements.
