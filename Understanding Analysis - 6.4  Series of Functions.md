We now apply the results from [6.2  Uniform Convergence of a Sequence of Functions](Understanding%20Analysis%20-%206.2%20%20Uniform%20Convergence%20of%20a%20Sequence%20of%20Functions.md) and [6.3  Uniform Convergence and Differentiation](Understanding%20Analysis%20-%206.3%20%20Uniform%20Convergence%20and%20Differentiation.md), to functional infinite series. The infinite series of functions is distinguished from a conventional series where each term isn't a specific number, but a function defined on an interval. The majority of arguments is completely analogous to the way results about sequences were applied to conventional series.<u> The trick is to view the series as a sequence of its partial sums and then define the convergence of the series by the convergence of the sequence of partial sums</u>. By reframing functional infinite series this way, we can then apply our results and determine the following:
- Continuity of an infinite series by the uniform convergence of the sequence of its partial sums
- Differentiability of the infinite series guaranteed by the uniform convergence of the sequence of derivatives of the partial sums and point-wise convergence at a particular point in the interval of differentiation
- The Cauchy Criterion for Uniform Convergence of series
