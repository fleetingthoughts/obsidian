## Monday Answer Key

## M1 (Friedberg §1.2)

Context: Prevents structural ambiguity in linear structures by proving that the underlying arithmetic baseline is invariant under the space's axioms.  
Answer: Identity vector uniqueness holds because if 0 and 0' are both identities, then 0 = 0 + 0' = 0'. Inverse uniqueness holds because if y and y' are both additive inverses of x, then y = y + 0 = y + (x + y') = (y + x) + y' = 0 + y' = y'.

## M2 (Friedberg §1.4)

Context: Replaces geometric vector visualization with generalized finite scaling transformations.  
Answer: A vector v in V is a linear combination of a finite set of vectors {u1, u2, ..., un} if there exist scalars c1, c2, ..., cn in F such that v = c1_u1 + c2_u2 + ... + cn*un.

## M3 (Abbott §2.2)

Context: Avoids topological multi-targeting behavior by proving sequences cannot converge to distinct destinations simultaneously.  
Answer: If a sequence (an) converges to L and to M, then L = M. If they were distinct, choosing an epsilon equal to half the distance between them would yield disjoint neighborhoods, which violates the definition of convergence.

## M4 (Abbott §2.2)

Context: Introduces spatial containment boundaries to trap terms within a finite real window.  
Answer: A sequence (an) is bounded if there exists a real number M > 0 such that the absolute value of an is less than or equal to M for all indices n in the natural numbers.

## M5 (Friedberg §1.3)

Context: Identifies an anti-reflective entry constraint across the primary diagonal grid of a matrix.  
Answer: A square matrix A is skew-symmetric if its transpose equals its negative, which means entry-wise that A_ij = -A_ji for all indices i and j.

## M6 (Hogg §1.3)

Context: Splits counting problems into independent card value groups via the fundamental multiplication rule.  
Answer: The probability is evaluated by choosing 1 value out of 13 for the triple, 3 cards from that suit, 1 value out of the remaining 12 for the pair, and 2 cards from that suit, all divided by the total possible 5-card combinations. This translates to (C(13, 1)*C(4, 3) * C(12, 1)*C(4, 2)) / C(52,5), which equals 3,744 / 2,598,960 or approximately 0.00144.

## M7 (Hogg §1.4)

Context: Compresses the unconditioned sample space to isolate probabilities within a verified event subset.  
Answer: The conditional probability of B given A is the probability of the intersection of A and B divided by the probability of A, assuming the probability of A is strictly greater than zero.

## M8 (Friedberg §2.4)

Context: Verifies that a transformation preserves all algebraic attributes between two vector spaces identically.  
Answer: Prove that the mapping T is linear by showing T(cx+y) = cT(x) + T(y), verify that T is one-to-one by showing its null space contains only the zero vector, and verify that T is onto by confirming its range equals the target space W.

## M9 (Friedberg §5.1)

Context: Rephrases vector operator scaling attributes as traditional scalar polynomial roots.  
Answer: Set up the characteristic equation by subtracting lambda times the identity matrix from A, compute its determinant, set it equal to zero, and extract the roots of the resulting polynomial.

## M10 (Friedberg §1.2 / Courant §1.2)

Context: Establishes vertical reflection symmetry to simplify functional computations across symmetric intervals.  
Answer: A function f is even on the real line if it satisfies the structural identity f(-x) = f(x) for all inputs x in its domain.

## M11 (Friedberg §2.1)

Context: Guarantees that injective transformations preserve independent coordinate frameworks without collapse.  
Answer: If T is a one-to-one linear transformation and a subset S is linearly independent, then its transformed image set T(S) is strictly linearly independent in the codomain.

## M12 (Courant §2.8)

Context: Accounts for interactive rate variations when tracking the instantaneous change of two multiplied functions.  
Answer: The derivative of the product of two functions f and g equals the derivative of f multiplied by g, plus f multiplied by the derivative of g.

## M13 (Abbott §3.2)

Context: Enforces border closure by ensuring that any sequential boundary trajectory lands inside the set.  
Answer: A set F is closed if it contains all of its limit points, meaning that if a sequence of elements inside F converges to a real number x, then x must also be an element of F.

Answer: If neither subspace contains the other, we can choose a vector u in W1 not in W2, and a vector v in W2 not in W1. Their sum u+v cannot belong to W1 (as that would imply v is in W1) nor to W2 (as that would imply u is in W2), which violates addition closure and proves the statement.

## F12 (Abbott §2.4 / Courant §1.7)

Answer: An infinite series converges if its corresponding sequence of partial sums, defined as the sum of the first n terms, approaches a finite real number limit as n approaches infinity.

Answer: The nullity of a linear transformation is the dimension of its null space, which is the subspace of vectors mapped to zero, and its rank is the dimension of its range space, which is the subspace containing all output images.
