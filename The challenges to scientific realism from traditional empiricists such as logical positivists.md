While it seems counter-intuitive for traditional empiricists scientific realism as their goals are aligned, PGS presents the views of logical positivists who opposed scientific realism. The main concern from the empirical positivists is that if there exists an independent reality that is beyond our current senses, then it puts into question how we can ever say something about it if we cannot directly verify it. It conflicts with [the verifiability criteria](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%202%20Logic%20Plus%20Empricism.md), and opens the floodgate to all the non-sensical unverifiable philosophy that logical positivists had hoped to avoid if we allow ourselves to talk about causal explanations and a world that we cannot directly observe but assume to exist.

Overall, the issue from the traditional empiricists is that if there were such an independent reality, it is impossible for us know anything about it beyond our own senses. So it is either a meaningless endeavor or it doesn't exist.

Some logical positivists were committed to phenomenalism: the idea that all meaningful senses can be translated into sentences that only refer to sensations. If phenomenalism were true, there is no independent reality, but  only what our senses detect. Any attempts to go beyond our senses to a world that we cannot directly sense is then meaningless.

A more valid counter to scientific reality is the issue of underdetermination of theories. Given any data, a range of causal explanations are available and it would be impossible for us to pinpoint it down to one. Confirmational holism comes into play here and there is an irony in that the concept that is the Achilles' Heel for logical positivism is used in its own defence.
