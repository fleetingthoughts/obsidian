## 🗓️ Day 1: The Old Man and the Polite Argument (Book I)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. The Madman's Weapon: Cephalus suggests that being good means telling the truth and returning what you borrow. Socrates brings up a friend who lends you a weapon but then goes completely insane. Why does Socrates use this example, and what is it supposed to prove about simple rules of right and wrong?
2. Friends and Enemies: Polemarchus takes over the argument for his father. He claims that it is right to help your friends and hurt your enemies. How does Socrates use comparisons to horse trainers and dog breeders to show that hurting an enemy actually makes no sense?
3. The Trap: If you are Polemarchus, how could you defend your idea against Socrates? How might a human being be different from a horse or a dog when it comes to being corrected by punishment?
4. The Old Man's Wealth: At the very beginning of the book, what does Cephalus say is the greatest benefit of being rich when you realize you are nearing the end of your life?

## 🗓️ Day 2: The Angry Visitor and the Three Choices (Book I & II)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. The Strongest Person: Thrasymachus bursts into the conversation and yells that justice is just "whatever advantages the person in power." According to him, why do rulers make laws in the first place?
2. The Three Baskets of Goods: At the start of Book II, Glaucon asks Socrates to sort things we want into three specific groups: things we like for their own sake, things we like for their results, and things we like for both. In which group do regular people put good behavior, and where does Socrates put it?
3. The Shepherd's Secret: Socrates tries to defeat Thrasymachus by saying a doctor looks out for the patient, not himself. Thrasymachus mocks him and brings up a shepherd. How does Thrasymachus use a shepherd and his sheep to flip Socrates' logic upside down?
4. The Ring: Describe the story of the shepherd Gyges and the magic ring he finds in the crack of the earth. What does the shepherd do once he figures out how the ring works?

## 🗓️ Day 3: Building a City from Scratch (Book II)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. The Big Letters: Socrates says that looking for justice in a single person is too hard, like trying to read tiny writing from far away. What does he propose they look at instead to see the "bigger letters"?
2. The Healthy vs. Feverish City: The very first city Socrates builds has only a few basic workers (farmers, builders, weavers). But Glaucon gets disgusted and calls it a "city of pigs." What luxuries do they add to the city next, and why does adding these items immediately force them to go to war with their neighbors?
3. One Person, One Job: Socrates insists that every single citizen in the city must do exactly one job that matches their natural talent. Why does he think it is dangerous for a farmer to also fix his own roof or make his own shoes?
4. The Watchdog: When selecting the soldiers to protect the city, Socrates says they must have a psychological nature exactly like a well-bred watchdog. Why a watchdog? How does a watchdog treat people it knows versus strangers?

## 🗓️ Day 4: Rewriting the Stories (Book II & III)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. Gods Don't Lie: Socrates says the soldiers must respect the gods, but he hates the stories told by Homer and Hesiod. What is his specific problem with stories where gods change their shapes, dress up in disguises, or tell lies to humans?
2. Banning the Underworld: In Book III, Socrates deletes lines of poetry that describe the afterlife as a terrifying, cold, and gloomy place. Why does he think it is dangerous for young soldiers to read descriptions of a scary underworld?
3. Laughter and Crying: Why does Socrates ban stories that show heroes or gods weeping uncontrollably or bursting into massive fits of laughter? What kind of personality is he trying to build in his soldiers by cutting these scenes?
4. Acting the Part: Socrates says that if the young protectors mimic or act out the roles of cowards, madmen, or bad characters in theater, it will ruin them. How does he think pretending to be a bad person affects someone's actual character?

## 🗓️ Day 5: Music, Instruments, and Rhythm (Book III)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. Banning Flutes: Socrates completely bans flutes and multi-stringed instruments from his city. What basic, simple instruments does he allow to remain, and who is allowed to play them out in the countryside?
2. The Sad and Lazy Tunes: Socrates sorts musical styles into different types of moods. Which musical scales does he ban for making people soft, lazy, or sorrowful, and which two scales does he keep for war and peaceful work?
3. Art in the Environment: Socrates says that even the decorations on houses, furniture, and clothing must be simple and clean. How does living around ugly or chaotic craftsmanship slowly poison a young person's mind, even if they don't notice it?
4. The Syrian Story: How does Glaucon use the story of Gyges' ancestor to argue that if you gave a good person and a bad person the exact same power to be invisible, both of them would end up committing the exact same crimes?

## 🗓️ Day 6: Sports, Doctors, and Judges (Book III)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. Too Much Exercise: Socrates says that if a soldier only does physical training and ignores music and poetry, his mind undergoes a specific change. How does he describe a person who is purely athletic but completely uneducated?
2. When to Refuse Treatment: Plato has a very radical view of doctors. Under what exact conditions does Socrates say a doctor should look at a patient and say, "No, I will not treat you"? What is his reason for letting certain sick people die?
3. The Old Judge: Socrates says a good judge shouldn't be a young person who understands crime because they've committed crimes themselves. What should a judge's background look like, and when in life should they finally start studying what bad behavior is?
4. The Diet of an Athlete: What kind of food are the soldiers allowed to eat, and what common treats (like sweet desserts or Corinthian girls) are they completely banned from experiencing to keep them alert?

## 🗓️ Day 7: The Metal Lie and the Rules of the Camp (Book III)

## 📝 Quiz Questions:

1. The Story of the Metals: Describe the famous "Noble Lie" that the rulers tell the citizens. Where does the story say the citizens were actually made, and what are the three types of metals mixed into their souls?
2. Demoting a Child: According to the story of the metals, what must the rulers do if a gold parent gives birth to a bronze or iron child?
3. The Rules of the Barracks: At the end of Book III, Socrates describes how the soldiers must live. Why are they banned from owning private property, having a locked door, or touching physical gold and silver coins?
4. Making the Soldiers Sad: Adeimantus interrupts and objects that Socrates is making the soldiers miserable by taking away their money and nice houses. What is Socrates' response? Is his goal to make the soldiers happy, or is he looking at something else?
