The final chapter examines the reduction of one scientific field into another. The concepts are explained and argued using the recurring example of the neovitalistic doctrine of vital forces and entelechies. The neovitalistic doctrine proposes that living systems are driven by vital forces and entelechies that cannot be explained by physical sciences alone. Hempel has repeatedly used this doctrine as a counterexample of improper science and it will be the central illustrative example of this chapter. The chapter is outlined as follows:
- The structure of a theoretical reduction of one field into another characterized by two processes:
	1) The characteristics of biology can be fully described in terms of the concepts of physics and chemistry
	2) The concepts of biology can be explained by the laws and theories of physics and chemistry
- The 

- On M1: descriptive terms not stipulative terms in a less stringent sense. Hempel argues there is no analytic tautological term that has a one-to-one correspondence with biological terms. The reduction in terms than has to be understood in a less stringent sense: extensional definitions
	- characterization of medicine by molecular structure. Note this is not an analytic definition, it is a synthetic one
	- Reduction of terms is not analytic and must be synthetic and empirical to a degree.
- On M2 some problems and solutions to derive biological concepts from physics:
	- Theory couched purely in physics and chemical terms cannot explain another concept couched in different terms, there must be bridging principles in the theory to connect the two. Therefore a component that's required are extensional definitions that are synthetic in nature.
	- Due to the fact that extensional definitions are required. The explanation of biological laws by physical laws cannot be armchair a priori'd. We need research and so it is synthetic in nature.
