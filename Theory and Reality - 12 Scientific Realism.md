In this chapter, PGS presents two extreme views with regards to scientific realism and proposes his own views that I see as a middle ground between the two. After clarifying his position, he presents views from 3 major fields that opposes his and offers varying levels of criticism

The views of several sociologists and from fields of Science Studies in their explanation of the workings of science and the production of scientific theories and results, PGS frequently criticized the gap and their inability to link the workings of science with the workings of the natural world. While their theories may be a factor for the description of the current working of science, it provides no normative description for how we should respond to the outside world. The whole endeavor of science should then be questioned if we do not take into account the scientists responsiveness to external phenomena or if we consider such a response to be pointless. Latour for instance, would view nature as a literal "product" from the settlement of scientific debate. In these views, reality is seen more as a social construction. What's the normative direction for science then? Should we still even do it? PGS contrasts this with the view of scientific realism, the idea that there is a separate reality that exists and is independent of our formulations or tribal culture that we seek to understand. PGS first formulates a "common-sense" version of realism as a framework to guide his discussion on the important questions to ask before presenting his own view of scientific realism. The common sense view is as follows (p.174):

"We all inhabit a common reality, which has a structure that exists independently of what people think and say about it, except insofar as reality is comprised of, or is causally affected by, thoughts, theories, and other symbols."

The main questions and concerns are with the last part of "... reality is comprised of, or is causally affected by thoughts theories, and other symbols." 
- How much confidence should we have in those thoughts and theories. What level of confidence should we have in our current description of science? To Michael Devitt, our current best theories are adequate as long as we don't commit ourselves to the speculative ideas at the frontier of science. Critics would be quick to point out historic examples of when even the best theories have been overthrown. 
- The issue with common sense realism is that it implies our thoughts and ideas (i.e. scientific theories) are THE causal explanation for phenomena but at the same time, these causal explanations are what justifies the realists views for every bridge and successful application of theory is justification of this.
- Ideas in realism have also been thrown in shambles by the work of quantum mechanics and the effects of observation/measurement on the phenomena itself

The issue appears to be that, as PGS notes, the majority of formulations of scientific realism propose a high degree of confidence in current science and he proposes a view of "naturalized scientific realism" that allows for pessimism in our current theories. PGS proposes the two major modifications two the common-sense realism:
1) That our current understanding of reality and the causal explanation are concurrent with the state of science in uncovering these causal explanations. This is where naturalism is applied.
2) The aim of science in uncovering causal explanations is a reasonable aim and to give us accurate representations of aspects of reality that are not observable.
PGS clarifies his position by countering some of the criticisms that were immediate to common-sense realism. His position allows for pessimism in our current theories as we accept science will continue to uncover what we should be confident in. The question of confidence in our current model is no longer a philosophical concern, it is a problem for science to figure out. Therefore, provides an adequate description of the "mistakes in science" as caloric theory or phlogiston, but provides a normative direction in these theories are still worthwhile endeavor as their confidence is to be established within science itself. Phlogiston and caloric theory lead to the kinetic model of gas and we even have successful theories such as germ theory which would be a worthless endeavor if we were to accept that there is no independent reality to discover.

While PGS's "middle-ground" formulation seems broad to be of any use as it allows for any kind of work to be valid, he does clarify what his formulation excludes: extreme pessimism in science. Here his 2nd clause clarifies that the endeavor of Science and the response to the outside world to find a causal explanation is a reasonable aim. He excludes the views of some sociologists and even confirmation-skeptics like Popper who may have the position that the search for accurate representations to depict the causal links in the world is a pointless endeavour because no such links exist (sociologists) or such a method is not required for science (Popper).

To conclude, PGS clarifies his choice of terms "accurate representations" as opposed to traditional language and logic based terms like "truth". While science has traditionally been described with these kinds of terms, PGS comments on an emerging field which criticizes the view of the representational vehicles used in Science. While Science is definitely communicated in words and languages where "truth" matters, PGS believes science is more accurately expressed through "models" which describe a similarity rather than an absolute truth. The use of models is illustrated, by the immense flexibility in science to use these models for various purposes to great success despite not fitting the conventional language based ideas of "truth". The model may be used for application or for a guide for further experimentation to find whether there is any literal meaning (e.g. actual germs in germ theory). The idea is that "truth" and language/logic-based approaches have a one-to-one correspondence with reality which is not what happens in Science at all that exercises great flexibility. PGS proposes we must detach ourselves from this traditional tendency to analyze science through these language-based lens (e.g. deductive logic) and find some kind of way that takes into account this "model-based" approach that science actually uses in describing similarities with many possibilities as opposed to a one-to-one correspondence. This is why PGS avoids using terms like "objective" and "truth" which invite this strict one-to-one correspondence analysis.

- Major claims that PGS lists offering varying levels of counterarguments
	- The challenge to realism from empiricism.
		- Phenomonalism and the meaningless of statements about a reality we can't describe
		- Underdetermination.
	- Metaphysical constructavism
		- Attempts to interpret through Kant's noumenal and phenomenal world. 
		- Michael Devitt counters by analyzing debates and noting the anti-realists mix relativism with Kant's views. Kant believed we had a basic conceptual framework to interpret the world.
		- While PGS acknowledges the use of it as an illustration, he finds Kantian model excessive. Calling a phenomenal world and noumenal world when we can just say its hard to interpret reality without all these extra layers.
		- PGS also notes the difficulty in distinguishing socialists from this metaphysical constructivist view or a more moderate "social constructavism" which acknowledges a form of realism
		- PGS also criticizes the tendency of sociologists to formulate such weird views to avoid what's called the Bad view
	- Van Fraasen and his version of instrumentalism ("constructive empiricism")
		- All we aim to do is create empirically adequate theories, models are a tool to expand theory to account and find relations with other phenomena but the underlying causal explanations are meaningless
	- Counters to van fraasen:
		- Where does van fraasen cut off the boundary of what is "observable phenomena". Historically, this boundary is constantly shifting. Van Fraasen's views would reject our search for germs or atoms as real things instead of just models that we use as tools. PGS differentiates between detectable and directly observable.
		- Empirical adequacy is a mistake to PGS because it throws away the part of science that challenges the unobservable and the most extraordinary science are the ones that comment on the unobservable.
