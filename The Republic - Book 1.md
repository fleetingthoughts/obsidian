The plot is outlined as follows:
1) Socrates goes to Piraeus with Glaucon to pray to the goddess and see how the Thracians conducted a festival they were conducting for the first time
2) A slave boy owned by Cephalus stops Socrates so Polemarchus can catch up and jokingly strong arms Socrates to his home
3) At Cephalus' house, Socrates and Cephalus talk about the value of old age and wealth and when asked by Socrates, Cephalus believes the value of wealth is that wealth helps you not cheat or lie against your will and you will not owe a debt to other people or gods in sacrifices.
4) Socrates pivots this to a question of justice. Does Cephalus' comments on the advantages of wealth constitute justice. Polemarchus interjects and states that Simonides' definition of justice is to give a man what he is owed
5) Practicality of Simonides' justice: we make mistakes in who we address as friends and foes so Simonides' definition of justice has limitations
6) We define friend and enemy. A friend is someone who is good while enemy is someone who seems good
7) Socrates compares justice to a skilled craftsman to show it cannot be the harm in another individual. A skilled craftsman cannot make someone unskilled. We also operate on th following assumptions:
	- Doing harm makes someone worse with respect to themselves
	- Justice is a human virtue, so making someone worse makes them more unjust
	 Justice is like a skilled crafts in that we only making someone better at our art. We cannot making someone less skilled at it
8)  Thrasymachus violently interjects like a wild beast looking to tear them apart. Thrasymachus demands a punishment which socrates responds the best punishment is to learn from the fitting, but thrasymachus demands a fine too which Glaucon volunteers
9)  Thrasymachus asserts that: "Just is nothing other than the advantage of the stronger". Thrasymachus illustrates by example in that cities everywhere, what is considered just is established by the rulers to keep them in power. Thrasymachus is giving a descriptive definition of justice in how he perceives it to be practiced.
10) Socrates illustrates there is a contradiction in that justice can be performing actions to the advantage AND disadvantage of rulers on the presumption that people can make mistakes.
11) Cleitophon gives Thrasymachus the benefit of the doubt and reframes his definition as the idealized version of what must be the advantage to the ruler not just what they believe (which can be wrong). This distingusihes between the idealzied and how it is done. Polemarchus makes a side swipe and says this is not what Thrasymachus actually argued but Socrates agrees to go on this line of inquiry.
12) Thrasymachus: The ideal ruler makes no mistake. When they issue out an unjust action: something that is to the disadvantage of the ruler, they are no longer a just person. Analogy with a skilled craftsman not being skilled when they make a mistake.
13) Socrates continues the analogy of Justice as a craft.  
	- A craft is a ruler of its respective area of expertise.
	- Craft does not look to perfect itself but to the advantage of the thing it was intended for. Medicine is intended for sick not for itself. Inherent assumption that all knowledge is in a form of craft. Therefore there is no knowledge that is to the benefit of itself but rather to the benefit of thatt which it rules: the less skilled.
14) Thrasymachus concedes that ruling is a form of art.
15) Thrasymachus doubles down in a long tirade that justice is the advantage of the man who is stronger and rules and a personal harm to the man who obeys. His points are as follows:
	- The just man always has less than the unjust man. The just man gets less in the dissolution of partnership, the just man pays more taxes.
	- Thrasymachus' shifts the role of injustice from the ruling class to the individual in any society. 
	- Thrasymachus's argument now focuses on the practical value of justice: The just man is less profitable than the unjustman
16) Socrates begins. Notes that Thrasymachus has slightly shifted his definition of a craftsman when referring to sheppards: they are no longer concerned with the art of sheparding but with the money making. The true essence of the craft is not for profitability:
	1) We agree each art serves different capacity. Doctors concern medicine, shepards sheep, etc.. Socrates distinguishes the actual art itself from the profiting, the money making. Each craft is distinguished by their expertise, but they have money making in common, so money making cannot distinguish them.
	2) Even if wage-earning does not distinguish the craft, we agree that the craft still produces benefit in spite of the personal befit of the practictioner themselves. So the true essence of the craft is not to profit.
17) Socrates extends 16) to ruling as an art. The idealized ruler looks to the benefit of the art itself and not themselves. Socrates expands on this saying therefore to incentivize those idealized ruler to rule, they must be paid a wage or punished for not doing so.
18) Glaucon asks Socrates to expand on why they need the punishment. Socrates believes asserts that assuming his proposition is true and the idealized ruler does not look to rule, the reason why he must rule is the greatest penalty of all: the possibility of being ruled by a lesser man.
19) We return to the just man being less profitable according to Thrasymachus. 
	1) Socrates gets thrasymachus to flip the conventional morality:
		1) Injustice is not a vice but it is virtue and wisdom
		2) Justice is not a vice but innocence.
		3) The just man would not be trying to get the better of the just man
		4) The just man would try to get the better of the unjust man
		5) The unjust man would try to get the better of everyone.
20) Socrates argues that justice is virtuous: it is wise and good and the unjust man is bad and unlearned by comparison to a skilled craftsman who "gets the better" of the "unlike" unskilled craftsman by being better:
	1) Using examples of musical men and doctors, tThe skilled craft gets the better of the unskilled only.
	2) The skilled craft is agreed to be wise
	3) The wise is agreed to be good
	4) The ignorant try to get the better of both the unskilled and skilled
	5) Therefore by comparison, the unjust man also tries to get the better of the like and unlike and the just man only tries to get the better of the unlike
21) Socrates now moves to the practicality of justice. Does justice produce strength and mightier than justice? Thrasymachus does not think so. On a large scale, injustice is impractical for a city to become mighty because it does not foster cooperation:
	1) Thrasymachus agrees that cities become stronger through injustice by his definition that injustice tries to get the better and advantage of themself
	2) We agree people cannot cooperate if they are trying to get the better of the other
22) Injustice is not  profitable  at the individual level first starting with the gods
	1) We agree that injustice should it manifest on a large scale is equivalent to manifesting in the individual. So injustice at the individual scale is similar to injustice at a larger scale.
	2) We assume that for an individual, it must be that that he is trying to get the better of himself and his mind.
	3) The unjust man would have no quarrel with trying to get the better of the gods.
23) Continue on the profitability of justice at the individual level with happiness.
	1) We assume everything has its purpose. Ears are for hearing, horses for horsing. The work of each thing is defined by what it can do better than others
	2) Virtue is defined by their ability to execute the work they can do better than others
	3) Now with definitions 1), and 2), what is the virtue of a soul is defined to be the good life
	4) Justice is virtue of the soul as carried over from 20). therefore justice supports a good life. THIS IS VERY UNCONVINCING FOR ME. If we agree that justice is virtue and human excellence from 20), that does not mean it is the virtue of the soul specifically. There seems to be another underlying assumption that the virtue of the soul requires human excellence. In 20), we were looking at human excellence towards other people and "getting the better of the like or unlike",  Socrates seems to make a direct 1:1 translation of human excellence between people to individual human excellence
24) At the end aporia: Socrates humbly compares himself to a glutton who grabs at what he can letting go of what he was currently holding. He initially intended to define justice but had let go of that argument to refute Thrasymachus' defintion of justice.
