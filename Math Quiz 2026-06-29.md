## Q1

- Source: Abbott, §1.4 / Courant, §1.1.
- Testing: Analytic statement of the Postulate of Nested Intervals and edge-case counterexamples.
- Significance: Guarantees the topological completeness of the real line, allowing nested approximations to converge to an actual number.
- Applications: Foundation for the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem, construction of real numbers, and checking root existence.
- Solution: For a sequence of closed, bounded intervals $I_n = [a_n, b_n]$ satisfying $I_{n+1} \subseteq I_n$, the intersection $\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} I_n$ is non-empty. If the intervals are open, the intersection can be empty. Minimal counterexample: $I_n = (0, \frac{1}{n})$. As n → ∞, no real number x > 0 satisfies $x < \frac{1}{n}$ for all n, and 0 is excluded by the open boundary, yielding $\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty} (0, \frac{1}{n}) = \emptyset$.

- Significance: Ensures foundational algebraic consistency, ensuring that arithmetic operations have well-defined, un-ambiguous results.
- Applications: Solving unique equations and establishing baseline matrix and transformation kernels.
- Solution: Suppose $\mathbf{0}$ and $\mathbf{0'}$ are both additive identities in V. By the property of $\mathbf{0}$ acting as an identity, $\mathbf{0'} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0'}$ (Axiom VS3/VS4 for identity). By the property of $\mathbf{0'}$ acting as an identity, $\mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0'} = \mathbf{0}$. By the commutativity of vector addition (Axiom VS1), $\mathbf{0'} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0'}$. Combining these equations yields $\mathbf{0'} = \mathbf{0'} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0'} = \mathbf{0}$. Thus, the identity is unique.

## Q6

- Applications: Constructs generalized coordinate spaces ($\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}$, or finite fields $\mathbb{F}_p$).
- Solution: A Field F is a commutative ring with identity where every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse. The specific axioms governing multiplication are:
    
    1. Associativity: a(bc) = (ab)c.

## Q7

- Source: Abbott, §1.3.
- Testing: Rigorous uniqueness verification of least upper bounds.

- Applications: Defines exact values for limits, integrals, and bounding functions.
- Solution: Suppose s₁ and s₂ are both suprema of a non-empty set $A \subset \mathbb{R}$. Since s₁ is a supremum, it is an upper bound of A. - Solution: The Archimedean Property states: (i) Given any $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists an $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that n > x. ## Q6

- Significance: Highlights how absolute convergence preserves infinite sums, whereas conditional convergence allows sums to change arbitrarily under rearrangement.
- Applications: Validates exchanging summation terms in infinite series and processing signal filters.
- Solution: A series $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely if the series of absolute values $\sum \vert{}a_n\vert{}$ converges. It converges conditionally if $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum \vert{}a_n\vert{}$ diverges. A classical example is the alternating harmonic series:

## Q7

- Source: Courant, §2.7 / Abbott, §5.3.
- Testing: Deriving the Mean Value Theorem equation from Rolle's stationary foundations.
- Significance: Links the average rate of change over an entire interval to an exact instantaneous derivative point inside that window.
- Applications: Bounding function growth, estimating remainder errors in Taylor series, and designing numerical steps.
- Solution: The Mean Value Theorem states that if $f: [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on (a, b), then there exists at least one point c ∈ (a, b) such that:  
    $$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$  
    To prove this, construct an auxiliary function g(x) that measures the vertical distance between the curve f(x) and the secant line connecting the endpoints (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)):  
    $$g(x) = f(x) - \left[ f(a) + \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}(x - a) \right]$$  
    Evaluating this helper function at the boundaries gives g(a) = 0 and g(b) = 0. Since g(x) inherits continuity and differentiability from f(x) and satisfies the boundary condition g(a) = g(b), it fulfills all requirements for Rolle's Theorem. Therefore, there must exist some point c ∈ (a, b) where its derivative equals zero (g'(c) = 0). Differentiating the expression for g(x) gives:  
    $$g'(x) = f'(x) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \implies g'(c) = f'(c) - \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} = 0$$  
    Solving for f'(c) yields the standard Mean Value Theorem equation. This transforms local tangent slopes into a predictable tool for establishing global bounding functions.
