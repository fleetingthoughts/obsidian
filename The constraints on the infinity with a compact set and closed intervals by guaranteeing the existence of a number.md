The reason why the nested interval property works for both compact sets and closed intervals (which are just compact sets) is because both compact sets and closed sets guarantee the existence of a certain number in them:
- Closed intervals are bounded and so they always contain a supremum,
- Compact sets always have a limit point which it contains.
Meanwhile the NIP does not apply to closed intervals, because closed intervals do not require the existence of a limit point. A closed set can be one that has no limit points at all! The classic example is $\mathbb{N}$.

The point in all these definitions, is to determine the minimum number of constraints we can put on objects that exhibit "infinity" to make them more manageable.
