To give you an honest, historically accurate roadmap, your original list actually contained several pieces that were highly radical, experimental, or avant-garde for their time. For example, Beethoven's _Eroica_, Haydn’s _Representation of Chaos_, and Liszt’s _Les Préludes_ were disruptions to the standard musical landscape rather than typical representations of it.

The revised list below replaces those experimental anomalies with the true "standard-bearers"—the pieces that defined the mainstream musical landscape, conventions, and status quo of each era before Mahler stretched those traditions to their absolute limits.

## Day 1: The Evolution of Mainstream Orchestral Form

- Baroque: J.S. Bach – _Brandenburg Concerto No. 3 in G major, BWV 1048_ (The definitive standard of late Baroque ensemble texture).
- Classical: W.A. Mozart – _Symphony No. 40 in G minor, K. 550 (I. Molto allegro)_ (The absolute blueprint of Classical sonata-allegro form).
- Early Romantic: Robert Schumann – _Symphony No. 1 in B-flat major, Op. 38 "Spring" (I. Andante un poco maestoso – Allegro molto vivace)_ (The standard, conservative continuation of post-Beethoven symphonic structure).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Symphony No. 1 in D major (I. Langsam, schleppend)_ (Mahler establishing his symphonic voice using traditional nature motifs).

## Day 2: The Evolving Concerto Blueprint

- Baroque: Antonio Vivaldi – _Violin Concerto in E major, Op. 8, No. 1 "Spring"_ (The standard three-movement ritornello form that everyone copied).
- Classical: W.A. Mozart – _Piano Concerto No. 21 in C major, K. 467 (II. Andante)_ (The peak of balanced, elegant Classical concerto dialogue).
- Early Romantic: Felix Mendelssohn – _Violin Concerto in E minor, Op. 64 (I. Allegro molto appassionato)_ (The definitive Romantic concerto standard bearer).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Symphony No. 5 (IV. Adagietto)_ (Standard Late-Romantic emotional intensity, adapted for strings and harp).

## Day 3: Mainstream Symphonic Development

- Baroque: Arcangelo Corelli – _Concerto Grosso in G minor, Op. 6, No. 8 "Christmas Concerto"_ (The foundational standard for Baroque orchestral writing).
- Classical: Joseph Haydn – _Symphony No. 94 in G major "Surprise" (II. Andante)_ (The textbook example of Classical phrase symmetry, theme, and variations).
- Early Romantic: Johannes Brahms – _Symphony No. 2 in D major, Op. 73 (I. Allegro non troppo)_ (The ultimate standard-bearer of conservative, lyrical Romantic symphonism).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Symphony No. 4 in G major (I. Bedächtig. Nicht eilen)_ (Mahler at his most accessible, looking directly back at Classical conventions).

## Day 4: Vocal and Song Traditions

- Baroque: G.F. Handel – _Messiah, HWV 56 ("For unto us a Child is born")_ (The standard-bearer for English Baroque choral polyphony).
- Classical: W.A. Mozart – _Don Giovanni (Ah, chi mi dice mai)_ (The definitive standard of Classical dramatic opera writing).
- Early Romantic: Franz Schubert – _Die Forelle, D. 550_ (The universal blueprint for the 19th-century German Lied).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Lieder eines fahrenden Gesellen (No. 2: Ging heut' morgens übers Feld)_ (Mahler expanding the intimate Lied into a massive orchestral landscape).

## Day 5: Sacred Foundations and Choral Norms

- Baroque: J.S. Bach – _Mass in B minor, BWV 232 (Kyrie eleison)_ (The monument of standard Baroque counterpoint).
- Classical: Joseph Haydn – _Mass in B-flat major, Hob. XXII:12 "Theresienmesse" (Kyrie)_ (Standard late-Classical sacred style blended with symphonic textures).
- Early Romantic: Felix Mendelssohn – _Elijah, Op. 70 ("He, watching over Israel")_ (The dominant, mainstream 19th-century choral standard).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Symphony No. 8 in E-flat major (Part I: Veni, creator spiritus)_ (The ultimate expansion of the choral tradition into symphonic space).

## Day 6: The Craft of Melancholy and Pathos

- Baroque: Johann Pachelbel – _Canon and Gigue in D major_ (The definitive standard of Baroque continuous variation/ground bass).
- Classical: W.A. Mozart – _String Quartet No. 19 in C major, K. 465 "Dissonance" (II. Andante cantabile)_ (Standard Classical elegance resolving brief tension).
- Early Romantic: Frédéric Chopin – _Nocturne in E-flat major, Op. 9, No. 2_ (The absolute mainstream standard of Romantic lyricism).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Kindertotenlieder (No. One: Nun will die Sonn' so hell aufgeh'n)_ (The peak of bittersweet late-nineteenth-century orchestral world-weariness). [1]

## Day 7: The Grand Finale and Structural Summation

- Baroque: J.S. Bach – _Orchestral Suite No. 3 in D major, BWV 1068 (II. Air)_ (The gold standard of Baroque linear melody).
- Classical: Joseph Haydn – _Symphony No. 104 in D major "London" (IV. Finale: Spiritoso)_ (The standard, joyful, folk-infused textbook Classical finale).
- Early Romantic: Johannes Brahms – _Symphony No. 1 in C minor, Op. 68 (IV. Adagio - Più andante - Allegro non troppo)_ (The supreme continuation of standard 19th-century symphonic triumph).
- Late Romantic: Gustav Mahler – _Symphony No. 9 (IV. Adagio)_ (The final, fading gasp of the traditional tonal system).
