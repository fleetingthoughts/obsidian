The problem of integration up to this point was to find the function $f(x)$ such that $f'(x)=g(x)$, but this issue can be extended to the more general problem of differential equations and the general relationship between $f(x)$ and all its derivatives. The interest in solving a general case of the problem of integration is obvious, but the usefulness of differential equations is demonstrated with the simple harmonic oscillator equation as an example. The trigonometric functions were initially defined to derive intuitive geometric results, but an alternate definition of  $\sin{x}$ and $\cos{x}$ as solutions to the simple harmonic oscillator differential equation can derive the usual trigonometric results purely analytically without appeal to geometry:
1) Define $\sin{x}$ and $\cos{x}$ as solutions to the simple harmonic oscillator differential equation and derive the familiar trigonometric properties from them.
2) Prove the solution to the simple harmonic oscillator equation given initial conditions $u(0)$ and $u'(0)$ is unique assuming the solution exists.
3) Derive the the derivative of the trigonometric functions
4) Derive the addition theorem

The solution to the simple harmonic oscillator is the function $u(x)$ that satisfies the following differential equation: $$u''(x)+u(x)=0$$
This chapter only offers a small taste of the power of differential equations and more will be expounded on in Chapter 9.
# Definitions
- ***Solution to a differential equation.*** The function (as opposed to a value), that solves a relationship between a function and its derivatives.
# Theorems
- ***The solution to the simple harmonic oscillator equation is unique given initial conditions (p.313).***
