In my study of the sciences, I have become curious into the creative and investigative methodologies of the vast subjects that all fall under the singular term "science". What is science and what can be considered good or even great science? How has the course of science changed with different subjects like alchemy and psychoanalysis considered pseudoscience and quantum mechanics considered scientific? In asking these questions, I have considered it useful to take a step back and look at the giant <u>institution</u> that is science and examine the criteria that makes science what it is so that I may improve my own creative process and take a meta-analysis of my studies.  It appears to me that all great scientists, such as Feynman, were great philosophers inquiring into the epistemological and metaphysical nature of the very things they were studying which spurned their creative inquiries especially as I recall Feynman's questioning of the nature of energy in my youth. 

Theory and Reality is a survey of the major milestones over the past century and provides further reading suggestions with each chapter covering a milestone or overall "community circle". PGS also provides commentary on his own position offering criticism of various positions. PGS himself is opposed to generalizations and offers a position that pieces together ideas from several different fields.

I suggest categorizing the book by chapter and then along with the major points to further identify the following: 
- key people (for follow-up reading)
- key terms used and definitions (as the philosophy of science has much to do with the use of language and its full context)
- historical examples.
The shape of the discussion of the overall book is as follows:
- Chapters 2-9 are an outline of the ideas in chronologically to capture the shape of the discussion as each idea is usually in response to a previous one
- Chapters 10-14 are topical modern ideas of the philosophy of science:
	- 10-11 is on Naturalism
	- 12 is on Scientific Realism
	- 13 is not a field a thought but on the topic of explanation. What is its role in science and how should it be done.
	- 14 is the most modern theory of confirmation (evidence is the precise term used) of Bayesianism
- Chapter 1-4 are language and logical based forms of the philosophy of science that try to establish an abstract foundation for science. 
- Chapters 5-6 talks about the shift in the way philosophy of science is discussed starting with Kuhn
- Chapters 7-9 are ideas on the philosophy of science that from fields outside of science namely sociology and feminism.
The book is well organized into its chapters with a crystal thesis that one can extract or interpret. I have provided my own as follows:
1) [Introduction](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%201%20Introduction.md): 
	- The two basic questions that the book will answer. 
	- The two criteria to evaluate the various ideas surveyed in the book and the 3 different levels they apply to
	- 3 tentative pieces that answer what science is.
2) [Logic Plus Empricism](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%202%20Logic%20Plus%20Empricism.md): 
	- Overview of the philosophy of the Vienna Circle and logical positivism/empiricism and the philosophy they were reacting against 
	- Two central dogmas to their philosophy
	- The challenges to their two dogmas
3) [Induction and Confirmation](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%203%20Induction%20and%20Confirmation.md)
	- The logical empiricists/positivists approach to the problem of induction and the application of confirmation
	- Different types of nondeductive inferences
	- Hypothetico-deductivism
	- 2 thought experiment problems that any theory of confirmation must answer
4) [Popper: Conjecture and Refutation](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%204%20Popper's%20Conjecture%20and%20Refutation.md)
	- Popper's criteria for science and the demarcation from non-science
	- Popper's normative views on science
	- Objections to Popper's falsifiability criteria for Science and confirmation and Popper's response
5) [Theory and Reality - 5 Kuhn and Normal Science](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%205%20Kuhn%20and%20Normal%20Science.md)
	- Kuhn's model of Science contrasted against the logical positivists and Popper. Focus on the Normal science of Kuhn
	- Kuhn's approach to science from sociological and historical perspective as opposed to an abstract level
6) [Theory and Reality - 6 Kuhn and Revolutions](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%206%20Kuhn%20and%20Revolutions.md)
	- Focus on the way Kuhn's explained the mechanism of scientific change
	- Challenges by PGS and the unanswered questions
7) [Theory and Reality - 7 Lakatos, Laudan, Feyerabend, and Frameworks](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%207%20Lakatos,%20Laudan,%20Feyerabend,%20and%20Frameworks.md)
	3 different views that are largely in reaction to Kuhn but respected his historical and sociological approach to science
	- Lakatos and the Research Programme
	- Laudan and the refinement of Lakatos' idea to the Research Tradition
	- Feyerbend, the maverick of Science, and his wild views on science ("Against Method")
8) [Theory and Reality - 8 The Challenge from Sociology of Science](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%208%20The%20Challenge%20from%20Sociology%20of%20Science.md)
	- The rise of sociology in the Philosophy science. Chapter 8 looks at 4 major inputs from sociology on Science.
	- Sociology marked the importance of social interactions in producing the norms and standards of science as described by Kuhnian Science
		- Merton's "weak view" and the underlying values for all of science
		- David Bloor and the "Strong Programme" which refined Merton's views adding further factors from society to explain the particular beliefs a scientist may hold at a specific time period with the prime driver being societal forces
		- Refinement of the Strong Programme in the work ***Leviathan and the Air Pump*** which explained the development of social norms through interactions at the individual level using the priority dispute between Hobbes and Boyle as an example
		- The criticism of the Strong Programme by Bruno Latour and Steven Woolgar's ***Laboratory Life*** which was critical of the hierarchal view of societal forces influencing Science and instead argued for an iterative relationship between human influencing society and vice-versa which also included non-human elements like important documents that played a role. Science is instead a network rather than a community in societal hierarchy
9) [[Theory and Reality - 9 Feminism]]
	- The influence of male-oriented ideas in shaping science and concrete examples of "feminine ideas" developing science
	- The idea of Science being a marketplace of ideas that requires diversity in people for diversity of ideas
	- The diversity of ideas pushed to the extreme with Science studies and the influence of humanisms in Science
10) [Theory and Reality - 10 Naturalistic Philosophy in Theory and Practice](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2010%20Naturalistic%20Philosophy%20in%20Theory%20and%20Practice.md)\
	- The field of naturalism defined as being opposed to foundationalism. Naturalism treats science as a resource to answer philosophical questions as opposed to laying out guidelines or a frame work to constrain Science
	- Application of Naturalism to address the views from Science studies that Science never has had had any real response to the outside world. 
	- A significant example of naturalism in action and a defense of empiricism by addressing the theory-ladenness of observation using psychological principles and scientific evidence to isolate cases where humans as biological organisms can by-pass subjectivity at the lower-level and form higher-level thoughts that run counter to the low level observations
11) [Theory and Reality - 11 Naturalism and the Social Structure of Science](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2011%20Naturalism%20and%20the%20Social%20Structure%20of%20Science.md)
	- Ideas on the social organization of science that came from naturalism ideals (unclear how naturalism specifically is applied here as it appears more sociological)
	- The refinement of recognition from the sociological views in Chapter 8 as "recognition through use"
	- The expanding of the descriptive sociological views to provide a normative direction for how science SHOULD be divided to maximize progress and what is the ideal division of labor. Specifically how should we hedge ourselves and not put all our eggs in one idea and structure incentives at the individual level to encourage that social structure
	- The layering of the social structure on top of the empirical views. Here PGS sketches his position by criticizing the views from chapters 8-9 of Science as a "PR exercises" and provides no role for the individual to be constrained by science. Here he continues his defense of empiricism and explores the option of these social views being layered on top of empiricism, a way of organizing the "strategy of empiricism" as opposed to replacing it.
12) [Theory and Reality - 12 Scientific Realism](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2012%20Scientific%20Realism.md)
	- A model and goal that provides a normative direction for science: scientific realism. The idea that there is an external reality and that it is a reasonable aim for science to provide explanations assuming there's such a reality
	- Counters to Scientific realism in 3 views:
		- <u>The counter to realism being a reasonable aim for science:</u> underdetermination of evidence by the traditional empiricists
		- <u>The counter to the idea that there is even an external reality:</u> The alternative views that our reality is a social construct
		- <u>The counter to science's goal in understanding that external reality</u>: Van Fraasen's modified instrumental views that explanatory schemas' ultimate goal is to make predictions and empirically explain phenomena without needing to understand causal mechanisms
13) [Theory and Reality - 13 Explanation](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2013%20Explanation.md)
	- 4 different views on the purpose and contents of an explanation in Science
	- The failed D-N model (covering law model) in providing a non-causal and purely deductive way of explaining things and the problem of asymmetry.
	- The alternative view of providing causal explanations which invites the criticisms of an "external reality" that  were surveyed in [chapter 12](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2012%20Scientific%20Realism.md)
	- A refinement of the covering law model in unificationism to resolve the problem of asymmetry by imposing the additional criteria of choosing the explanation that makes the fewest assumptions
	- PGS's contextualism position to choose whichever view that fits the standards of the field at the time. 
	- Van Fraasen's pragmatic view that an explanation is one that provides use outside of science to everyday people.
14) [Theory and Reality - 14 Bayesianism and Modern Theories of Evidence](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2014%20Bayesianism%20and%20Modern%20Theories%20of%20Evidence.md)
	- The current most popular theory of confirmation (and specifically evidence) that rose from the dead ashes of the logical positivists that ascribes an intrinsic property of probability to any statement
	- The use of Bayes formula to provide a normative and descriptive model confirmation and the subjectivist interpretation of the quantities in Bayes' formula and the requirement of coherence
	- The testing of Bayesianism by how it answers the two classical confirmation problems: Goodman's riddle of induction and Hempel's paradox
	- PGS's alternative view of confirmation by a process of elimination instead of direct affirmation: eliminative inferencing. This view can complement Bayesianism.
15) [Theory and Reality - 15 Empiricism, Naturalism, and Scientific Realism](Theory%20and%20Reality%20-%2015%20Empiricism,%20Naturalism,%20and%20Scientific%20Realism.md)
	- PGS sketches out his own views that combines empiricism, naturalism, and realism and specifically addresses the problems in empiricism. Empiricism required as our connection to the real world to counter the claims from Science studies
	- PGS's modification of empiricism as an interacting with the world to determine what is "not correct" as opposed to what "is the THE right answer" using ideas from eliminative inference
	- PGS's view of empiricism as a strategy and the social views of science discussed as a way to organize the strategy of empiricism. Here the views from social studies do not replace empiricism, but instead complement it.
	- PGS's extensive criticism of the views from Science Studies that strawman a specific (albeit traditional) view of empiricism but at the same time leaves no room for the scientist to respond to the natural world. Science studies' criticism of empiricism throws the baby out with the bath water and leaves a very empty model of science as a PR exercise
