## 📅 Day 1

## 💳 Card 1.1

- Front: Define the "criterion of verifiability" in its weak sense.
- Back: It requires that a factual statement be structured so that some possible observations or experiential propositions are relevant to determining its truth or falsehood by rendering it probable rather than logically certain.

## 💳 Card 1.2

- Front: Explain the "overcrowding hypothesis" regarding childbed fever and how it was empirically refuted.
- Back: It was a current conjecture that overcrowding caused the high mortality rate in the First Division. It was rejected because the crowding was actually heavier in the Second Division, largely due to patients desperately trying to avoid assignment to the notorious First Division.

## 💳 Card 1.3

- Front: Map the formal logic of _modus tollens_ in hypothesis testing.
- Back: If hypothesis $H$ is true, then so is test implication $I$ ($If\ H\ then\ I$). Evidence shows $I$ is not true. Therefore, $H$ is not true. This argument form is deductively valid; if the premises are true, the conclusion is unfailingly true.

## 💳 Card 1.4

- Front: Why are assertions of value (ethical or aesthetic judgements) classified as emotive expressions rather than scientific hypotheses?
- Back: They contain normative symbols that are not defined in terms of sensations or empirical occurrences. They have no factual content and cannot be verified or falsified by experience; instead, they function as non-literal expressions of feeling or prescriptive commands.

## 💳 Card 1.5

- Front: What unique validation method distinguishes nonempirical sciences (logic/pure mathematics) from disciplines investigating the physical world?
- Back: Nonempirical propositions are proved with absolute certainty strictly through their logical relationships as analytic tautologies. They record our determination to use symbols in a certain fashion and make no assertions about the empirical world.

## 💳 Card 1.6

- Front: Explain the difference between explicit definitions (such as are given in dictionaries) and definitions in use.
- Back: Philosophy provides definitions in use by showing how sentences containing a defined symbol can be translated into equivalent sentences that do not contain the symbol, rather than providing an identical, interchangeable word-for-word synonym.

## 💳 Card 1.7

- Front: Detail Pascal's balloon experiment prediction and how it contrasted with the ad hoc preservation of the horror vacui theory.
- Back: Pascal predicted that a partly inflated balloon carried up a mountain would become more inflated at the mountaintop due to decreased air pressure. This provided a new, independent test implication, whereas saving the "horror vacui" by claiming its "abhorrence" changes with altitude yielded no new test implications.

## 💳 Card 1.8

- Front: Why is the concept of a "substantive ego" or a separate soul entity rejected as a fictitious metaphysical concept?
- Back: Because systematic introspection reveals only a constantly changing sequence of separate sense-experiences, thoughts, and feelings. No one ever observes a static, underlying, independent substance that houses these thoughts, making the soul untestable in principle.

## 📅 Day 2

## 💳 Card 2.1

- Front: Distinguish between practical verifiability and verifiability in principle.
- Back: Practical verifiability applies to propositions we have the current physical means to test. Verifiability in principle applies when we cannot currently place ourselves in the test situation due to a lack of practical means, but we still know exactly what specific observations would theoretically decide its truth.

## 💳 Card 2.2

- Front: What is the historical case of the medical commission of 1846 regarding injuries in the First Maternity Division, and how was it refuted?
- Back: The commission blamed the illness on injuries from rough examinations by medical students. It was refuted because: (a) natural birth injuries are more extensive, (b) midwives in the Second Division examined patients similarly without ill effects, and (c) halving the student count failed to halt the mortality rise.

## 💳 Card 2.3

- Front: Explain the logical fallacy of "affirming the consequent" and its impact on confirming a hypothesis.
- Back: An invalid mode of reasoning: If $H$ is true, then so is $I$; evidence shows $I$ is true; therefore $H$ is true. This is deductively invalid because the conclusion ($H$) can be false even if the premises are true; alternative, unconsidered hypotheses may imply the exact same test outcome.

## 💳 Card 2.4

- Front: What is the logical relationship between everyday material things and sense-contents?
- Back: Material things are logical constructions out of sense-contents. This means that saying a material object exists is completely equivalent to saying certain sensations are obtainable under specific conditions. Any significant sentence about a material object can be translated into equivalent sentences referring solely to immediate data of sensation.

## 💳 Card 2.5

- Front: Detail the four sequential stages outlined in the ideal, yet untenable, narrow inductivist conception of scientific inquiry.
- Back: (1) Observation and recording of all facts without selection or prior guesses, (2) analysis and classification of these facts without hypotheses, (3) inductive derivation of generalizations from them, and (4) further testing of the generalizations.

## 💳 Card 2.6

- Front: Why is the view that philosophy is a search for first principles to build a complete deductive picture of reality rejected?
- Back: To build such a system, first principles must be logically certain. If they are empirical laws of nature, they are merely probable hypotheses; if they are _a priori_ truths, they are tautologies from which only further tautologies, and no new factual knowledge, can be deduced.

## 💳 Card 2.7

- Front: Detail Millikan's 1909 oil-drop experiment controversy with Ehrenhaft and its relation to auxiliary assumptions.
- Back: Millikan found electric charges were integral multiples of the electron's charge. Ehrenhaft claimed to find subelectronic charges. Millikan defended his core hypothesis by demonstrating Ehrenhaft’s apparatus violated necessary test conditions $C$ (e.g., dust interference, droplet evaporation, loss of optical focus), treating these as faulty auxiliary errors.

## 💳 Card 2.8

- Front: Explain how grammatical features regarding the verb "to be" lead to the metaphysical illusion of "Being" as an attribute.
- Back: Sentences expressing existence ("Martyrs exist") share the same grammatical form as those expressing attributes ("Martyrs suffer"). This leads thinkers to treat existence as an attribute, which is a fallacy; ascribing an attribute already asserts existence, so treating existence as an attribute would render all positive existential claims redundant tautologies.

## 📅 Day 3

## 💳 Card 3.1

- Front: Define what an "experiential proposition" is.
- Back: A proposition that records an actual or possible observation. It serves as the foundational unit for verifying factual propositions, which must allow the deduction of experiential propositions when combined with certain other premises.

## 💳 Card 3.2

- Front: Explain the "atmospheric-cosmic-telluric changes" hypothesis and why it was systematically rejected in medical history.
- Back: It attributed the ravages of childbed fever to vague epidemic influences spreading over whole districts. It was rejected because it failed to explain why the fever selectively plagued the hospital's First Maternity Division for years while completely sparing the adjacent Second Division and the surrounding city.

## 💳 Card 3.3

- Front: Define an ad hoc hypothesis and explain how it is systematically identified.
- Back: A qualifying assumption introduced for the sole purpose of saving a core hypothesis against threatening, adverse evidence. It is not called for by other findings and, crucially, leads to no additional, independent test implications.

## 💳 Card 3.4

- Front: Why is a work of art (like a poem or novel) not necessarily worse for the fact that all the propositions comprising it are literally false?
- Back: Because the difference between the man who uses language scientifically and the man who uses it emotively is that the artist is primarily concerned with the creation of a work of art rather than the expression of true propositions; his words are chosen for rhythm and emotive balance.

## 💳 Card 3.5

- Front: Why can a mechanical inductive procedure (like graphic curve-fitting) never fully generate a complex scientific theory?
- Back: Mechanical routines can only select a specific function to map variables already measured using existing concepts. They cannot perform the creative jump required to invent a theory stated in radically novel theoretical terms (such as 'atom' or 'sea of air') that do not appear in the raw data.

## 💳 Card 3.6

- Front: What is the fundamental target of Berkeley's phenomenalism that the analytic framework accepts?
- Back: Berkeley discovered that material things must be definable in terms of sense-contents. He correctly denied Locke's analysis that ideas are related to a single, unobservable underlying "somewhat," showing instead that objects are constituted out of the relations of sense-contents to one another.

## 💳 Card 3.7

- Front: Detail J.J. Balmer's 1885 spectral formula discovery and how it relates to confirmation by "new" test implications.
- Back: Balmer constructed a mathematically simple formula matching four known hydrogen spectrum lines. His formula was heavily confirmed because it accurately predicted the wavelengths of 31 subsequent consecutive lines that were either measured or discovered _after_ his formula was written.

## 💳 Card 3.8

- Front: Why is the traditional philosophical controversy between monists and pluralists concerning "substance" condemned as fictitious?
- Back: Because both parties admit it is impossible to imagine any empirical situation that would be relevant to the solution of their dispute. No possible observation could give any probability either to the assertion that reality is one substance or that it is many.

## 📅 Day 4

## 💳 Card 4.1

- Front: Explain the distinction between the "strong" and "weak" senses of the term "verifiable".
- Back: A proposition is verifiable in the strong sense if its truth can be conclusively established in experience. It is verifiable in the weak sense if experience can merely render it probable. Strong verifiability must be rejected because it rules out all general laws and statements about the past as nonsense.

## 💳 Card 4.2

- Front: What is the historical case of the priest and the death bell in the First Maternity Division, and what did its test demonstrate?
- Back: It was conjectured that the priest walking through the wards ringing a bell created a terrifying psychological dread that caused the fever. Semmelweis had the priest use a silent, roundabout route; the mortality rate did not drop, refuting the psychological hypothesis.

## 💳 Card 4.3

- Front: Define what a "test implication" is and state its typical conditional form.
- Back: A statement describing observable occurrences that are expected to happen under specified test conditions if a hypothesis is true. It normally assumes the conditional form: "If conditions of kind $C$ are realized, then an event of kind $E$ will occur."

## 💳 Card 4.4

- Front: How does David Hume rhetorically foreshadow the verification principle in his _Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding_?
- Back: He commands that if we take any volume of divinity or school metaphysics that does not contain abstract reasoning concerning quantity/number or experimental reasoning concerning matter of fact, we must commit it to the flames because it contains nothing but sophistry and illusion.

## 💳 Card 4.5

- Front: Why is it impossible in an experimental test of a quantitative hypothesis to vary only one quantitative variable while keeping literally all other conditions constant?
- Back: Changing a physical state naturally alters an infinite web of secondary conditions (e.g., relative humidity, laboratory illumination, distance from the moon); thus, an experimenter can only realistically keep constant those factors believed to be relevant based on background assumptions.

## 💳 Card 4.6

- Front: Explain the analytical example concerning the discovery of a picture suspected to be painted by Goya.
- Back: Experts examine details, look up records, and check for forgeries. While they may ultimately still disagree, each expert knows exactly what empirical evidence would confirm or discredit his opinion, which distinguishes genuine factual analysis from metaphysical interpretations of the picture's "reality."

## 💳 Card 4.7

- Front: Explain the concept of "confirmation from above" or theoretical support for a hypothesis.
- Back: Support that comes not from direct evidential testing, but from more inclusive, broader hypotheses or theories that logically imply the given hypothesis and already possess independent, widespread evidential confirmation.

## 💳 Card 4.8

- Front: Why is the claim that the world of sense-experience is altogether "unreal" classified as literally nonsensical rather than false?
- Back: Because no conceivable observation or series of observations could ever have any tendency to show that the revealed world is unreal. Sensory deceptions are corrected only by further, subsequent sense-experience, not a super-empirical reality.

## 📅 Day 5

## 💳 Card 5.1

- Front: What does it mean to say that the propositions of philosophy are not factual but entirely linguistic or analytic?
- Back: They do not describe the physical or mental behavior of objects; instead, they express definitions or the formal consequences of definitions. Philosophy is a department of logic, completely independent of empirical or metaphysical assumptions.

## 💳 Card 5.2

- Front: Why is the notion of collecting "all facts" prior to any tentative hypothesis a logical impossibility in scientific research?
- Back: There is an infinite number and variety of facts in the universe at any moment. Collection cannot begin without a selective principle (the hypothesis) to determine which specific data are relevant to the problem under study.

## 💳 Card 5.3

- Front: Why can an unfavorable test implication never definitively disprove a primary hypothesis in isolation?
- Back: Because the test implication ($I$) is derived from the core hypothesis ($H$) _combined_ with auxiliary assumptions ($A$). A negative test result proves only that $H$ and $A$ are not both true ($H\ and\ A\ are\ not\ both\ true$). The error may lie entirely in a mistaken auxiliary hypothesis, leaving the core hypothesis intact.

## 💳 Card 5.4

- Front: What is the precise logical definition of a metaphysical sentence or pseudo-hypothesis?
- Back: A sentence that purports or grammatically appears to express a genuine factual proposition but expresses neither an empirical hypothesis nor an analytic tautology. Because no conceivable observational data can confirm or disconfirm it, it lacks empirical import and is literally senseless.

## 💳 Card 5.5

- Front: Detail how Tycho Brahe’s stellar parallax observations illustrate how a false auxiliary assumption can cause an investigator to reject a true core hypothesis.
- Back: Brahe rejected the Copernican theory because he observed no stellar parallax. His core hypothesis (the Earth moves) was true, but his test relied on a false auxiliary assumption: that the stars were close enough to Earth for their parallax to be detected by his unassisted instruments.

## 💳 Card 5.6

- Front: Why does an investigator's failure to provide a perfect phenomenalist definition of an object have no bearing on the validity of everyday common-sense perceptual judgements?
- Back: Because what gives a person the logical right to believe a material thing exists is simply the immediate occurrence of certain sensations. Philosophy's task is critical clarification and defining terms, not validating or invalidating the direct data of sense-experience.

## 💳 Card 5.7

- Front: Detail Foucault's 1850 experiment regarding the velocity of light and why it was considered a crucial test.
- Back: Foucault compared the velocity of light in air vs. water. The wave theory predicted light travels faster in air; the corpuscular theory predicted the opposite. The experiment showed light was faster in air, which practically eliminated Newton's classical corpuscular theory.

## 💳 Card 5.8

- Front: Why is it a mistake to define a "cause" in terms of a relation of necessary enforcement or necessitation between specific events?
- Back: Because any proposition asserting a causal connection can be denied without logical self-contradiction, and it is impossible to conceive of any observations that could ever establish the existence of an unobservable relation of necessary enforcement.

## 📅 Day 6

## 💳 Card 6.1

- Front: Why is a crucial experiment strictly impossible in science, yet valid in a less rigid, practical sense?
- Back: Strictly, an experiment cannot isolate and prove one theory while completely refuting another, because adjustments can always be made within a theory's secondary system of laws and auxiliary assumptions. Practically, however, it can reveal one system as seriously inadequate while lending major support to its rival, decisively changing the direction of future research.

## 💳 Card 6.2

- Front: Why is it a mistake to define a cause as an object followed by another whose appearance "always conveys the thought to that other"?
- Back: Because it is entirely conceivable and common that there are causal laws in nature which have never yet been thought of by any human mind, and psychological expectations are distinct from physical causal relationships.

## 💳 Card 6.3

- Front: Why does the quantity of favorable test findings face a law of diminishing returns when confirming a hypothesis?
- Back: If thousands of confirmatory cases are already available, adding one more instance obtained by a test of the exact same kind raises the confirmation very little. The confirmation is significantly enhanced only if the new finding results from a completely different _kind_ of test (increasing variety).

## 💳 Card 6.4

- Front: If a metaphysician writes nonsense, how does his intent separate him entirely from a poet who might employ nonsense words?
- Back: A poet who writes nonsense does so intentionally, carefully selecting words for rhythm and aesthetic effect. The metaphysician, by contrast, does not intend to write nonsense; he lapses into it unknowingly by being deceived by grammar or committing logical errors.

## 💳 Card 6.5

- Front: Detail the logic of Kekulé's campfire dream of 1865 and what it demonstrates about the scientific method.
- Back: Kekulé hit upon the hexagonal ring structure of benzene after dreaming of a snake seizing its own tail. It demonstrates that scientific hypotheses may be freely invented or influenced by scientifically questionable notions; scientific objectivity is safeguarded not by how ideas arrive, but by critical validation via test implications.

## 💳 Card 6.6

- Front: Who is the specific starting-point target of the critique against transcendent metaphysics?
- Back: Those thinkers who believe that it is possible to have knowledge of a reality that transcends the phenomenal world, and who claim to be endowed with a special faculty of intellectual intuition that allows them to know facts outside sense-experience.

## 💳 Card 6.7

- Front: Detail how Semmelweis tested the conjecture concerning the lateral vs. supine position of women during delivery.
- Back: Women in the First Division delivered lying on their backs; in the Second Division, on their sides. Semmelweis introduced the lateral position in the First Division, but the mortality rate remained unaffected, leading him to discard the conjecture.

## 💳 Card 6.8

- Front: What gives a person the logical right to believe in the existence of any certain material thing?
- Back: Simply the factual occurrence of certain sensations. To say that a material thing exists is logically equivalent to saying that such sensations are obtainable, which is verified wholly by actual sense-experience.

## 📅 Day 7

## 💳 Card 7.1

- Front: What are "operational definitions" of scientific concepts and what is their systematic function?
- Back: Definitions that understand a scientific term by specifying a fixed, predetermined sequence of testing operations that determine whether the term applies. They ensure concepts retain clear empirical import rather than lapsing into operationally meaningless words.

## 💳 Card 7.2

- Front: What is the true critical function of philosophy if it is not to search for first principles or justify common-sense beliefs?
- Back: To exhibit the criteria used to determine the truth or falsehood of propositions, show us what we accept as sufficient evidence for a given empirical proposition, and define or clarify symbols and their logical relationships.

## 💳 Card 7.3

- Front: What are the two distinct major groups into which the different branches of scientific inquiry are divided?
- Back: Empirical sciences (which seek to explore, describe, explain, and predict occurrences in the world and depend on fact-checking) and nonempirical sciences (logic and pure mathematics, which are proved without essential reference to empirical findings).

## 💳 Card 7.4

- Front: Why is the conception of philosophy as a study of "reality as a whole" rejected if it implies taking a bird's-eye view?
- Back: Because projecting oneself outside the world to take a structural bird's-eye view is a completely metaphysical delusion; "reality as a whole" is not generically different from the reality investigated piecemeal by the empirical sciences.

## 💳 Card 7.5

- Front: Define the concept of "rationality" as outlined within an analytical framework.
- Back: Being rational does not mean possessing a logical guarantee of the future; rather, "being rational" strictly entails being guided in a particular, systematic fashion by past experience when forming expectations.

## 💳 Card 7.6

- Front: Why does the postulation of real non-existent entities (such as a mysterious "Nothing" or objective universals) occur?
- Back: It results from the primitive superstition that to every word or phrase that can serve as the grammatical subject of a sentence, a single real entity must somewhere correspond. When no place is found in the empirical world, a non-empirical world is invoked.

## 💳 Card 7.7

- Front: Define Snell's law of refraction and explain how a variety of evidence affects its verification.
- Back: It states that $\sin \alpha / \sin \beta$ is a constant for any pair of media. Testing it across varied pairs of media and varied angles of incidence provides a far more thorough check—and a higher degree of confirmation—than testing the same single pair of media at a single angle repeatedly.

## 💳 Card 7.8

- Front: Detail how the phlogiston theory’s postulation of "negative weight" serves as a primary example of an objectionable ad hoc shift.
- Back: Adherents claimed combustion involves the escape of phlogiston. When Lavoisier proved the end product weighed _more_ than the original metal, tenacity led adherents to claim phlogiston had "negative weight" solely to explain away the weight increase, without generating any other testable predictions.
